A Research Ethics Board (REB) is an independent, multi-disciplinary committee mandated to protect the rights, safety, and well-being of human participants in research. As a newly appointed legal representative, you join a board that sits at the intersection of science, ethics, institutional accountability, and the law — and your perspective is indispensable to that work.

## What Is a Research Ethics Board?

REBs in Canada operate under a framework anchored by the **Tri-Council Policy Statement: Ethical Conduct for Research Involving Humans (TCPS 2)**, issued jointly by the Canadian Institutes of Health Research (CIHR), the Natural Sciences and Engineering Research Council of Canada (NSERC), and the Social Sciences and Humanities Research Council (SSHRC). Institutions that receive federal research funding are required to establish an REB and ensure all human-subjects research undergoes ethical review before it begins.

<Info>
  The TCPS 2 is not a statute, but compliance with it is a contractual condition of federal Tri-Council funding. Québec legislation — including **Law 5** (_Act Respecting Health and Social Services Information_), arts. 20–25 of the **_Civil Code of Québec_**, and (residually) **Law 25** — adds binding legal obligations on top of the TCPS 2 framework.
</Info>

The REB's core mandate is to review research protocols **before** participant enrollment, monitor ongoing studies, and suspend or terminate research that poses unacceptable risk. The board is not a rubber stamp; it is a substantive gatekeeper with real authority over research conducted within or by the institution.

## Why Legal Expertise Matters

Ethical review and legal review are complementary but distinct. Many of the most consequential questions that come before an REB have explicit legal dimensions:

<CardGroup cols={2}>
  <Card title="Consent & Capacity" icon="file-signature">
    Determining whether informed consent processes meet legal standards, and how to handle participants with diminished decision-making capacity.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Privacy & Data Governance" icon="shield-halved">
    Assessing whether data collection, storage, and sharing practices comply with Law 5, Law 25, PIPEDA (where applicable), GDPR (for international studies), and the establishment's privacy policies.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Vulnerable Populations" icon="people-group">
    Applying heightened protections required by law and policy for minors, prisoners, individuals with cognitive impairments, and Indigenous communities.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Contracts & Liability" icon="scale-balanced">
    Identifying indemnification gaps, sponsor liability clauses, and intellectual property issues in research agreements that could affect participant protection.
  </Card>
</CardGroup>

Without a legal voice at the table, boards risk approving protocols that are ethically sound on the surface but legally vulnerable — exposing participants, researchers, and the institution to harm.
