Every member of a Research Ethics Board, regardless of their professional background, takes on a binding confidentiality obligation the moment they join the board. For legal counsel, these obligations are layered: they arise from your REB appointment, from institutional policy, from privacy legislation, and from the common law duties you already carry as a lawyer. Understanding the full scope of what you must protect — and for how long — is essential before your first meeting.

## What Information Is Confidential

The confidentiality obligation covers a broad range of information that flows to you in your capacity as an REB member. The categories below are not exhaustive, but they define the core protected universe.

<CardGroup cols={2}>
  <Card title="Submitted Research Protocols" icon="flask">
    Every research application, study protocol, investigator brochure, questionnaire, recruitment script, and supporting document submitted to the REB is confidential. This material may contain unpublished scientific findings, proprietary sponsor data, and intellectual property. Disclosure outside the REB could cause significant harm to investigators, sponsors, and participants.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Board Deliberations" icon="comments">
    The substance of REB discussions — what concerns were raised, which members supported or opposed approval, the reasoning behind conditions placed on a protocol — is confidential. Deliberations are recorded in minutes that themselves are confidential documents circulated only to board members and authorized administrators.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Personal Information of Investigators" icon="user-tie">
    Applications often contain personal and professional details about principal investigators and co-investigators: CVs, financial disclosure forms, employment history, and disciplinary records. This information is provided to the REB in trust and may not be shared with third parties.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Personal Information of Research Participants" icon="user-shield">
    Some protocols, particularly those involving continuing review, adverse events, or protocol deviations, may contain identifiable or potentially identifiable information about research participants. This information is subject to the highest level of protection and must never be disclosed outside the REB process.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Institutional and Sponsor Information" icon="building">
    Protocols may include confidential business information from commercial sponsors — drug formulations, device specifications, trade secrets, and unpublished trial data. Institutional financial arrangements, indemnification terms, and legal opinions prepared for the REB are also confidential.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Adverse Event and Deviation Reports" icon="triangle-exclamation">
    Reports of participant harm, protocol deviations, and unanticipated problems are submitted to the REB in confidence. Premature or unauthorized disclosure of such reports could harm ongoing studies, prejudice regulatory proceedings, or expose the institution to legal risk.
  </Card>
</CardGroup>

## Legal Basis for Your Confidentiality Obligations

Your obligation to protect REB information rests on multiple, overlapping legal foundations. No single instrument governs — the full picture is composite.

<Tabs>
  <Tab title="Institutional Policy">
    Your appointment to the REB is governed by your hospital's REB Terms of Reference and Standard Operating Procedures. These documents will typically contain an explicit confidentiality undertaking that you sign upon joining the board. That undertaking is a contractual obligation enforceable by the institution. Breach may constitute grounds for removal from the board and could give rise to a civil claim for damages if the institution suffers loss as a result.
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Privacy Legislation">
    In a Québec hospital-based REB, the following statutes will typically apply to information you handle as a member:

    - **Law 5** — _Act Respecting Health and Social Services Information_ (in force July 1, 2024): the primary statute governing health and social services information held by the establishment, including protocol material tied to identifiable patients.
    - **_Civil Code of Québec_**, arts. 20–25: the enforceable framework for consent to research, including the written-form requirement in art. 24.
    - **Law 25** (residual): the modernized Québec private-sector and public-sector privacy framework, applicable to personal information falling outside Law 5's scope.
    - **PIPEDA** (federal): may apply to specific inter-provincial or federally regulated commercial flows involving a sponsor or CRO.

    As legal counsel, you are likely familiar with these regimes. Your exposure as an REB member does not create new statutory obligations independent of what applies to the establishment, but it does mean you are handling information that the establishment is obligated to protect under Law 5 and Law 25 — and that obligation runs through you.
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Common Law">
    The equitable doctrine of confidence protects information imparted in circumstances where a reasonable person would understand the information to be confidential. Research protocols, deliberations, and personal information submitted to the REB clearly meet this standard. Unauthorized disclosure could give rise to an action for breach of confidence by an investigator, sponsor, or participant whose information you disclosed.
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Law Society Rules">
    As a lawyer, you are subject to your provincial law society's rules of professional conduct. While your REB role is not a lawyer-client relationship, the professional duty to maintain confidentiality of information received in a professional capacity may apply depending on how your law society interprets your REB appointment. Consult your law society's practice advisory service if you are uncertain.
  </Tab>
</Tabs>

## Obligations That Survive Your Departure from the Board

<Note>
  Your confidentiality obligations do **not** end when your term on the REB concludes, when you resign, or when your appointment is not renewed. Information you received during your tenure remains confidential indefinitely unless it has entered the public domain through authorized means. This means you may never disclose the content of deliberations, the details of a protocol you reviewed, or personal information about investigators or participants — even years after you have left the board.

  Before accepting any future engagement — such as representing a party in litigation involving a study you reviewed — consider whether that engagement would require you to use or disclose confidential REB information. If it would, you may need to decline the retainer or seek ethics guidance from your law society.
</Note>

## Practical Guidance on Handling Documents and Communications

Knowing what is confidential is only half the obligation. The other half is handling material in ways that prevent inadvertent disclosure.

### Receiving and Storing Meeting Packages

Access meeting packages only through the secure channel provided by the REB (typically an encrypted platform or institutional email). Do not forward packages to personal email accounts or download them to unmanaged personal devices. If you print materials for annotation, treat printed pages as you would any confidential legal document.

### Communicating About REB Business

<Accordion title="Email and Messaging">
  Use only your institutional email account for REB communications. Do not use personal email, personal messaging apps (including iMessage, WhatsApp, or SMS from personal devices), or social media to discuss any aspect of REB business. When you must email a protocol-related question to the REB Coordinator, do not paste protocol content directly into the message body — reference the protocol number and attach nothing beyond what the coordinator needs to assist you.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Phone and In-Person Conversations">
  Do not discuss the substance of protocols, deliberations, or decisions in shared spaces where you may be overheard — elevators, open-plan offices, cafeterias, or public transit. If you need to speak with the REB Chair or Coordinator by phone, use a private location. Be mindful that conversations with colleagues in your legal department about a protocol you are reviewing on the REB could constitute an unauthorized disclosure, even informally.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Storing and Disposing of Documents">
  Store printed meeting materials in a locked cabinet when not in use. When you no longer need a physical copy, shred it using a cross-cut shredder — do not place confidential documents in unsecured recycling or waste streams. For electronic files, follow your institution's records management policy on retention and deletion. Do not store REB materials on portable drives or in personal cloud storage (e.g., personal Dropbox, Google Drive, or iCloud).
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Requests From Third Parties">
  You may receive requests for information about REB decisions from investigators, sponsors, journalists, legal colleagues, or the public. Direct all such requests to the REB Coordinator or Research Ethics Office without disclosing any substantive information yourself. If you are served with a subpoena or other legal process requiring disclosure of REB information, notify the REB Chair and your institution's legal counsel immediately before responding.
</Accordion>

## Confidentiality Do's and Don'ts

The following guidance summarizes the practical obligations in a format you can use as a quick reference.

<Tabs>
  <Tab title="✅ Do">
    - **Do** access meeting packages only through your institution's secure platform or encrypted email.
    - **Do** use your institutional email account for all REB correspondence.
    - **Do** store printed materials in a locked drawer or cabinet.
    - **Do** shred printed REB documents when they are no longer needed.
    - **Do** direct media, public, and third-party inquiries about specific protocols to the REB Coordinator.
    - **Do** notify the REB Chair immediately if you receive a subpoena or legal process requesting REB documents or testimony.
    - **Do** consider your ongoing confidentiality obligation before accepting future professional engagements involving a study you reviewed.
    - **Do** report suspected breaches of confidentiality (including breaches by other members) to the REB Chair and your institution's Privacy Officer.
    - **Do** ask the REB Coordinator for guidance whenever you are unsure whether a disclosure is permitted.
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="🚫 Don't">
    - **Don't** forward meeting packages or protocol documents to personal email accounts.
    - **Don't** discuss protocol content in shared or public spaces.
    - **Don't** share details of board deliberations with colleagues, family members, or friends — even in casual conversation.
    - **Don't** post about REB matters on social media, blogs, or public forums, even without naming investigators or sponsors.
    - **Don't** use protocol documents to inform work you do outside the REB (e.g., advising a client on a related matter) without explicit institutional authorization.
    - **Don't** retain copies of meeting packages beyond the period authorized by your institution's records management policy.
    - **Don't** assume that a protocol's approval or rejection is public information — direct any inquiry to the REB office.
    - **Don't** allow unauthorized individuals to view or handle your printed or electronic copies of meeting materials.
    - **Don't** assume your obligations end when your term on the board ends — they continue indefinitely.
  </Tab>
</Tabs>

## When a Breach Occurs

Despite best efforts, breaches of confidentiality can happen — a misdirected email, a printed document left in a public place, or a conversation overheard. If you believe a breach has occurred or may have occurred, act immediately.

<Steps>
  <Step title="Contain the Breach">
    Take whatever immediate steps are available to limit further disclosure — recall a misdirected email, retrieve a document left in a public space, or ask the unintended recipient to destroy the information and confirm they have done so.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Notify the REB Chair and Privacy Officer">
    Report the breach to the REB Chair and your institution's Privacy Officer as soon as possible. Do not delay to assess the severity first — early reporting allows the institution to manage its legal and regulatory obligations, including any mandatory breach notification requirements under applicable privacy legislation.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Document What Happened">
    Write a factual account of the breach — what information was involved, how the breach occurred, who may have been affected, and what steps you took to contain it. Provide this to the Privacy Officer to support the institution's breach response process.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Cooperate With the Response">
    Follow your institution's direction on any remediation steps, regulatory notifications, or communications to affected individuals. Your early disclosure and cooperation are material factors in how the institution and any regulator will assess your conduct.
  </Step>
</Steps>

<Warning>
  Do not attempt to investigate, manage, or resolve a confidentiality breach on your own without notifying the institution. Self-managed responses that fall short of mandatory notification obligations can expose both you and the hospital to regulatory penalties and civil liability. When in doubt, report promptly and let the Privacy Officer lead.
</Warning>
