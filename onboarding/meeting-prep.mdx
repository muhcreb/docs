Attending your first Research Ethics Board meeting as legal counsel can feel unfamiliar — the vocabulary is clinical, the documents are dense, and the decision-making process follows conventions that differ from a courtroom or boardroom. Preparing thoroughly in advance will help you contribute meaningfully from day one and give the board confidence in your legal perspective.

## What Happens at a Typical REB Meeting

REB meetings follow a structured agenda designed to ensure every protocol receives fair, consistent, and documented review. While formats vary slightly between institutions, most meetings follow the same core sequence.

<Tabs>
  <Tab title="Agenda Structure">
    A standard REB meeting agenda typically includes:

    1. **Call to order and quorum confirmation** — The chair confirms that the minimum required membership is present, including the mandated community member and at least one non-scientist member (which may be you, in your legal capacity).
    2. **Approval of previous minutes** — Members review and adopt the minutes from the last meeting.
    3. **Conflicts of interest declarations** — Members declare any conflicts with protocols on today's agenda before substantive review begins.
    4. **Full-board review items** — Protocols designated for full-board review are presented and discussed in turn.
    5. **Delegated review reports** — The chair or designated reviewer reports on protocols approved under delegated (expedited) review since the last meeting.
    6. **Continuing reviews and amendments** — Ongoing studies seeking renewal or protocol changes are considered.
    7. **Adverse event and deviation reports** — Any submitted safety reports are reviewed.
    8. **Other business and correspondence** — Administrative matters, policy updates, or member announcements.
    9. **Adjournment**
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Protocol Presentation">
    For each full-board review item, a **primary reviewer** (a board member assigned in advance) presents a brief summary of the protocol — the study purpose, methodology, participant population, and the key ethical issues they identified. A **secondary reviewer** may add observations.

    The rest of the board then asks questions, raises concerns, or endorses the reviewers' analysis. As legal counsel, you are expected to comment on matters within your expertise — consent language, contractual obligations with sponsors, liability, privacy legislation compliance, and institutional risk — but you are also a full voting member and may raise any ethical concern.
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Discussion and Vote">
    Discussion is open to all members. The chair facilitates to ensure every concern is heard before calling a vote. Decisions are typically recorded as:

    - **Approved** — the protocol may proceed as submitted
    - **Approved with modifications** — minor changes required before or during the study, verified administratively
    - **Deferred** — substantive revisions required; the protocol returns for full-board review
    - **Rejected** — the board determines the research cannot be approved

    Most institutions aim for consensus; formal votes by show of hands or recorded ballot occur when consensus cannot be reached. Majority rules; the chair may vote to break a tie.
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Minutes">
    Minutes are a formal record of the meeting and are considered confidential documents. They capture attendance, quorum confirmation, decisions reached for each protocol (with the rationale), any conditions attached, and a record of declared conflicts and resulting recusals.

    Minutes do **not** typically attribute individual statements to named members — deliberations are recorded collectively to protect the integrity of the review process. You will receive draft minutes for review and approval at the next meeting.
  </Tab>
</Tabs>

## How to Read and Annotate Submitted Protocols

Your meeting package will arrive — usually by secure email or an electronic submission platform — several days before the meeting. It can be substantial: a single protocol package may run 50–150 pages, including the application form, study protocol, consent documents, investigator brochures, questionnaires, and supporting letters.

<CardGroup cols={2}>
  <Card title="Start with the Application Form" icon="file-lines">
    The application form is the board's own summary document. It flags the study type, funding source, participant population, and risk level. Reading it first gives you a map for the rest of the package.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Focus on Consent Documents" icon="signature">
    As legal counsel, consent forms and participant-facing materials are your primary territory. Check reading level, completeness of disclosure, voluntariness language, written-form compliance under art. 24 CCQ, and alignment with applicable privacy legislation (Law 5, Law 25, and — where applicable — PIPEDA).
  </Card>

  <Card title="Identify Legal Risk Signals" icon="triangle-exclamation">
    Flag sponsor agreements, indemnification clauses, insurance certificates, data sharing provisions, and any cross-jurisdictional elements. Note whether the protocol involves vulnerable populations, Indigenous communities, or minors — each carries additional legal considerations.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Note Questions, Not Just Answers" icon="circle-question">
    You are not expected to resolve every legal question before the meeting. Write down what you are uncertain about so you can raise it in discussion or consult the REB Coordinator afterward.
  </Card>
</CardGroup>

<Tip>
  Annotate your copy of the consent form line by line. Bring those annotations to the meeting — they make for precise, efficient discussion and demonstrate the depth of your legal review to your fellow board members.
</Tip>

## Your Pre-Meeting Preparation Checklist

<Steps>
  <Step title="Access the Meeting Package">
    Log in to your institution's electronic research administration system (e.g., Research Manager, ROMEO, or a similar institutional portal) or open the secure email from the REB Coordinator. Confirm you have received all listed attachments. Contact the REB Coordinator immediately if anything is missing or unreadable.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Block Adequate Preparation Time">
    Plan for **two to four hours of reading** for a typical agenda of four to six full-board items, plus delegated review reports. First-time members often underestimate this. Protect the time in your calendar at least 48 hours before the meeting so you have room to follow up on questions.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Review the TCPS2 and Relevant Policies">
    Keep the _Tri-Council Policy Statement: Ethical Conduct for Research Involving Humans_ (TCPS2, 2022) open as a reference while you read. Your institution's own REB Standard Operating Procedures (SOPs) may impose requirements beyond TCPS2 — review the relevant SOP sections for any protocol types new to you.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Check for Conflicts of Interest">
    Before reviewing protocol content, scan the investigator names, departments, sponsors, and study sites against your own professional relationships. If you identify a potential conflict, email the REB Coordinator before the meeting so the chair can manage the agenda accordingly. See [Declaring and Managing Conflicts of Interest](/onboarding/conflict-of-interest) for full guidance.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Read and Annotate Each Protocol">
    For each full-board item: read the application form first, then the consent documents, then the protocol. Mark legal issues, ambiguous language, missing disclosures, and any provisions that may conflict with institutional policy or applicable law. Use a consistent symbol system (e.g., "?" for questions, "!" for definite concerns).
  </Step>
  <Step title="Prepare Your Questions">
    Compile a short list of questions or concerns for each protocol. Rank them — lead with your most significant legal concern. This prevents you from forgetting a key point in the flow of discussion and ensures the minutes reflect the legal issues raised.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Confirm Logistics">
    Verify the meeting time, location (room number or video conferencing link), and whether the meeting is in person, virtual, or hybrid. Know where to find dial-in credentials. Arrive or log in five minutes early — quorum must be confirmed at the start, and a late arrival can delay proceedings.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Bring Your Materials">
    Whether in person or virtual, have your annotated package accessible during the meeting. Know the page numbers of the passages you want to discuss so you can direct other members efficiently.
  </Step>
</Steps>

## What Questions to Bring

You do not need to have legal opinions ready on every issue. The following categories are reliable starting points for your legal review of any protocol:

<Accordion title="Consent and Disclosure">
  - Does the consent form disclose all material risks in plain language accessible to the target population?
  - Is the consent process truly voluntary — are there any coercive elements (e.g., undue inducements, power imbalances between investigator and participant)?
  - For studies involving incapable adults or minors, is substitute decision-maker consent addressed correctly under applicable provincial legislation?
  - Does the form accurately describe what happens to data after the study ends?
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Privacy and Data Protection">
  - Does the protocol comply with applicable Québec privacy legislation (**Law 5**, **Law 25**, and — where applicable — **PIPEDA**)?
  - Are data storage, transfer, and retention practices described and compliant with institutional policy?
  - If data is transferred outside Canada, are the receiving jurisdiction's protections adequate?
  - Is de-identification described in sufficient detail to assess its adequacy?
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Liability and Institutional Risk">
  - Does the sponsor agreement contain indemnification and insurance provisions that protect the institution and participants?
  - Is there a compensation-for-injury clause? Does it comply with TCPS2 Article 11.7?
  - Are there intellectual property or publication restriction clauses that could compromise research integrity?
  - Does the protocol involve any activities that require regulatory approval beyond REB review (e.g., Health Canada CTA, biohazard committee)?
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Vulnerable Populations and Special Legal Contexts">
  - Does the protocol involving Indigenous communities comply with TCPS2 Chapter 9 and any applicable nation-specific agreements?
  - For research involving prisoners, are the additional protections in TCPS2 addressed?
  - For pediatric research, is the assent process described and appropriate for the age range?
</Accordion>

## Expectations for Preparation Time

<Info>
  Most experienced REB members spend **90 minutes to three hours** preparing for a meeting with a full agenda. As a new member, budget more time until the document formats and decision frameworks become familiar — typically after three to five meetings.
</Info>

Your institution may track member attendance and engagement. Consistent, substantive participation is both a professional obligation and a protection: the legal quality of an REB decision depends in part on the rigour of the legal review that informed it.

## Practical Tips for Your First Meeting

<Tip>
  **Common first-meeting mistakes to avoid:**

  - **Staying silent because you feel out of your depth** — Your legal perspective is precisely why you were appointed. If consent language is ambiguous or a liability clause concerns you, say so. The board needs to hear it.
  - **Waiting until the vote to raise a concern** — Raise issues during discussion so the board can deliberate on them. A concern raised only at the vote cannot be properly addressed in the minutes.
  - **Over-reviewing outside your expertise** — You are not expected to assess statistical methodology or clinical risk in the same depth as a scientist member. Focus your energy on legal and ethical issues where you add the most value.
  - **Forgetting that minutes are a legal record** — If you raise a concern and the board decides to proceed anyway, ensure your concern is recorded. This protects both you and the institution.
  - **Missing the package deadline for questions** — If you have pre-meeting questions, send them to the REB Coordinator with enough lead time for investigators to respond before the meeting.
</Tip>
