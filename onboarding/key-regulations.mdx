Every legal representative appointed to a Research Ethics Board needs a working knowledge of a specific body of regulatory and policy material. This page serves as your quick-reference guide to the most important documents — what each one governs, who enforces it, and why it matters to your legal analysis during protocol review. Bookmark this page and return to it whenever a protocol raises a question about applicable authority.

<Note>
  The summaries below are orientation-level introductions. Full statutory and regulatory text, annotated provisions, and linked guidance documents are available in the **Reference** tab of this knowledge base.
</Note>

## Federal Policy

<Tabs>
  <Tab title="TCPS2">
    ### Tri-Council Policy Statement: Ethical Conduct for Research Involving Humans (TCPS2), 2022

    **Issued by:** The Canadian Institutes of Health Research (CIHR), the Natural Sciences and Engineering Research Council of Canada (NSERC), and the Social Sciences and Humanities Research Council of Canada (SSHRC) — collectively the "Tri-Council."

    **Legal status:** TCPS2 is not a statute. It is a policy condition attached to federal research funding. Institutions that receive Tri-Council funding must agree to comply with it as a term of their funding agreements. However, because virtually every research hospital in Canada receives Tri-Council funding, TCPS2 has near-universal application, and courts and regulators treat it as the national standard of care for research ethics review.

    **What it governs:** The TCPS2 covers the full scope of human research ethics, including: the mandate and composition of REBs; the three core ethical principles (respect for persons, concern for welfare, justice); free and informed consent; privacy and confidentiality; conflicts of interest; research involving Indigenous peoples; research with children, pregnant women, prisoners, and other populations requiring additional protections; clinical trials; qualitative and social science research; and human biological materials.

    **Why you need to know it:** TCPS2 is the primary standard against which the REB evaluates every protocol. As a legal representative, you must understand its structure deeply enough to identify when a protocol's procedures are inconsistent with its requirements. Non-compliance can jeopardize institutional funding, expose the institution to regulatory sanction, and form the evidentiary basis for negligence claims if a participant is harmed.

    **Key chapters for legal review:**

    - Chapter 1: Ethics Framework and REB Mandate
    - Chapter 2: Scope and Approach
    - Chapter 3: Free and Informed Consent
    - Chapter 5: Privacy and Confidentiality
    - Chapter 7: Conflicts of Interest
    - Chapter 11: Clinical Trials
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="ICH-GCP E6(R3)">
    ### ICH Guideline for Good Clinical Practice E6(R3)

    **Issued by:** The International Council for Harmonisation of Technical Requirements for Pharmaceuticals for Human Use (ICH). ICH finalized E6(R3) on January 6, 2025. Health Canada formally adopted it — revising its own guidance document (GUI-0100) to match — effective **April 1, 2026**, replacing the prior E6(R2). Compliance with it represents the expected standard of practice for clinical trials in Canada.

    **Legal status:** ICH-GCP is guidance, not law. However, Health Canada's inspection standards for clinical trial sites and REBs are calibrated against ICH-GCP, and the standard of care in clinical trial litigation is typically assessed against it. Deviation from ICH-GCP requires documented justification; unexplained deviation is a red flag in any regulatory inspection or legal proceeding.

    **What it governs:** E6(R3) restructured GCP into eleven overarching Principles plus **Annex 1**, which covers the responsibilities and standards for sponsors, investigators, and ethics boards (IRBs/IECs — equivalent to Canadian REBs) in the conduct of clinical trials, plus a new standalone Data Governance section. It addresses protocol design, informed consent, data integrity, essential document management, adverse event reporting, and inspection readiness.

    **Why you need to know it:** Annex 1, Section 1 is dedicated entirely to IRB/IEC (REB) responsibilities. It specifies what documents the REB must receive and review, what decisions it must make and document, and the review timelines it must meet. These requirements define the standard of care against which your REB's conduct will be assessed in any Health Canada inspection, regulatory proceeding, or litigation.

    **Key sections for legal review:**

    - Annex 1, Section 1: IRB/IEC (REB) responsibilities and procedures
    - Annex 1, Section 2: Investigator obligations, including informed consent (2.8)
    - Annex 1, Section 3: Sponsor obligations, including quality management, monitoring, and insurance/indemnification (3.14)
    - Annex 1, Section 4: Data governance — new in E6(R3), covering data integrity and computerised systems
    - Appendix C: Essential records for the conduct of a clinical trial

    <Info>
      Older sponsor templates, REB SOPs, or training materials may still cite E6(R2) section numbers ("Section 3," "Section 4.8," "Section 5.18"). These no longer match the current structure — see the Reference tab's dedicated ICH-GCP page for the full remapping.
    </Info>
  </Tab>
</Tabs>

## Federal Legislation and Regulation

<Tabs>
  <Tab title="Food and Drug Act">
    ### Food and Drug Act (R.S.C., 1985, c. F-27)

    **Administered by:** Health Canada, primarily through the Therapeutic Products Directorate (TPD) and the Biologics and Genetic Therapies Directorate (BGTD).

    **Legal status:** Federal statute. Compliance is mandatory. Violation can result in criminal penalties, injunctive relief, product seizure, trial suspension, and significant civil liability.

    **What it governs:** The _Food and Drug Act_ is Canada's primary federal statute regulating the safety and efficacy of foods, drugs, medical devices, and natural health products. For research purposes, its most relevant provisions govern the authorization of clinical trials. No clinical trial of a drug or biologic may be initiated in Canada without either a Clinical Trial Application (CTA) approved by Health Canada or qualification under an exemption from CTA requirements.

    **Why you need to know it:** When a protocol involves a drug, biologic, or medical device, the _Food and Drug Act_ defines the baseline legal requirements the REB must consider as part of its review. While Health Canada, not the REB, authorizes the CTA, the REB must confirm that a CTA has been filed and, where appropriate, approved before granting REB approval for the trial to proceed. The REB's approval is a statutory prerequisite for the trial to commence, and that approval must be documented in a manner consistent with both the Act and ICH-GCP.

    **Key provisions:**

    - Section 5: Prohibition on false or misleading labelling
    - Section 21.7: Mandatory reporting of serious adverse drug reactions in clinical trials
    - Division 5 of Part C of the Regulations (see below)
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Division 5 Regulations">
    ### Food and Drug Regulations, Part C, Division 5 — Drugs for Clinical Trials Involving Human Subjects

    **Administered by:** Health Canada.

    **Legal status:** Federal regulation made under the _Food and Drug Act_. Mandatory compliance for all clinical trials of drugs, biologics, and certain medical devices involving human subjects in Canada.

    **What it governs:** Division 5 is the operational heart of Canada's clinical trial regulatory framework. It specifies: the requirements for a Clinical Trial Application (CTA); the obligations of sponsors and qualified investigators; the requirement for REB approval and its documentation; the process for protocol amendments; the timelines and content requirements for serious adverse event (SAE) reporting to Health Canada; and the obligations for record retention.

    **Why you need to know it:** Division 5 is the most directly relevant federal regulation to your day-to-day REB work for clinical trials. It establishes specific requirements that the REB must meet — not aspirational guidelines, but mandatory legal requirements. In particular:

    - **C.05.009** requires that no clinical trial begin until the sponsor has received written REB approval. Your REB's approval letter must be in the required form and retained as required.
    - **C.05.010** governs the circumstances under which the REB must be notified of SAEs and protocol amendments.
    - **C.05.012** establishes the SAE reporting timeline to Health Canada (15 calendar days for unexpected fatal or life-threatening SAEs; 30 days for all other unexpected SAEs).
    - **C.05.017** specifies that sponsors and investigators must retain clinical trial records for 25 years.

    **Key provisions for legal review:**

    - C.05.001: Definitions
    - C.05.006: Prohibition on trials without CTA
    - C.05.009: REB approval requirements and documentation
    - C.05.010: Amendment and SAE notification requirements
    - C.05.012: SAE reporting timelines
    - C.05.017: Record retention obligations
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="PIPEDA / C-27">
    ### Personal Information Protection and Electronic Documents Act (PIPEDA) and Bill C-27

    **Administered by:** The Office of the Privacy Commissioner of Canada (OPC).

    **Legal status:** Federal statute. Applies to personal information collected, used, or disclosed in the course of commercial activities, including research conducted by private-sector organizations or federally regulated institutions. In Québec, Law 25 (which amended Québec's private-sector privacy Act) has been declared substantially similar to PIPEDA, so PIPEDA is largely displaced for provincially regulated activity in Québec.

    **What it governs:** PIPEDA establishes rules for the collection, use, and disclosure of personal information, including health information, by organizations subject to it. It requires meaningful consent for the collection and use of personal information, provides individuals with access rights, and establishes accountability obligations for organizations.

    **Why you need to know it:** Québec's private-sector privacy law (the _Act Respecting the Protection of Personal Information in the Private Sector_, as amended by Law 25) has been declared substantially similar to PIPEDA, so PIPEDA is largely displaced for provincially regulated activity in Québec. PIPEDA can still apply where a protocol involves inter-provincial or international commercial flows of personal information — for example, a sponsor headquartered outside Québec collecting information from Québec participants for use elsewhere in Canada, a federally regulated entity, or a foreign CRO. PIPEDA contains research exceptions in Schedule 1 and in s. 7(2) that permit use and disclosure of personal information for research purposes without consent in limited circumstances — parallel to (but not identical to) the research pathways under TCPS2, Law 5, and Law 25.

    **Federal reform update:** The federal government's most recent attempt at private-sector privacy reform — Bill C-27 (the proposed _Consumer Privacy Protection Act_) — died on the Order Paper when Parliament was prorogued in January 2025. Until a successor bill is enacted, **PIPEDA remains the operative federal private-sector statute** where it applies. Monitor the Office of the Privacy Commissioner of Canada for any reintroduced legislation.
  </Tab>
</Tabs>

## Québec Legislation Governing Consent and Health Information

<Tabs>
  <Tab title="Civil Code arts. 20–25">
    ### _Civil Code of Québec_, arts. 20–25 — Consent to Research on the Person

    **Administered by:** The Québec courts; consent standards are enforced through civil actions and by MSSS-designated REBs on prospective review.

    **Legal status:** Québec statutory law. Enforceable, not guidance. Non-compliance can support civil actions in liability (art. 1457 CCQ), including for interference with bodily integrity.

    **What it governs:** Articles 20–25 of the CCQ set out the fundamental rules for consent to research involving the human body — the requirement that consent be free, informed, and given in writing (art. 20); special protections and criteria for research involving minors or persons incapable of consent (art. 21); the form of consent, including that consent may be withdrawn at any time, even verbally (art. 24); and the non-commercialization principle governing the alienation of parts or products of the human body (art. 25).

    **Why you need to know it:** These articles are the enforceable statutory foundation for consent to research in Québec. They apply to every protocol reviewed by the REB, regardless of funding source or sponsor jurisdiction. Consent forms that do not meet the CCQ standard cannot support legally valid participation, and the written-form requirement in art. 24 is stricter than what exists in most other Canadian jurisdictions.

    **Key provisions:**

    - Article 20: Free and informed consent to research; written form
    - Article 21: Research involving minors or persons incapable of consent — REB approval, proportionality, and substitute consent
    - Article 24: Consent must be in writing; may be withdrawn at any time
    - Article 25: Non-commercialization of the human body and its parts
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Law 5 — Health & Social Services Information">
    ### _Act Respecting Health and Social Services Information_ (Law 5) — Québec

    **Administered by:** The _Commission d'accès à l'information_ (CAI), in coordination with sectoral authorities under the health and social services network.

    **Legal status:** Québec provincial statute, **in force July 1, 2024**. Mandatory compliance for bodies in Québec's health and social services network — including hospitals (établissements), CIUSSS/CISSS, and their designated custodians.

    **What it governs:** Law 5 is the primary statute governing the collection, use, communication, and protection of **health and social services information** held by bodies in Québec's health and social services network. It sets the conditions under which such information may be communicated to a researcher without the individual's consent, including approval by an **REB designated for this purpose by the MSSS**, a written research agreement, demonstration that the research purpose cannot reasonably be achieved without identifiable information, and adequate safeguards proportionate to sensitivity.

    **Why you need to know it:** For a Québec hospital-based REB, **Law 5 takes precedence over Law 25** for information within its scope. When a protocol requires access to patient records, biological samples linked to identifiable individuals, or data from hospital information systems held by a network establishment, **Law 5 is the operative statute**. Only MSSS-designated REBs may authorize research use without consent under Law 5; confirm your REB's designation covers the type of research proposed.

    **Key features:** MSSS designation of REBs; mandatory written research agreement with prescribed content; explicit criteria for research without consent; CAI oversight; alignment with the MSSS directives and the FRQ _Standards on the Ethical Conduct of Research Involving Humans_.
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Law 25 (residual)">
    ### _Act to modernize legislative provisions as regards the protection of personal information_ (Law 25) — Québec

    **Administered by:** Commission d'accès à l'information (CAI).

    **Legal status:** Québec statute (SQ 2021, c 25, formerly Bill 64). Law 25 substantially amended Québec's private-sector privacy law (the _Act Respecting the Protection of Personal Information in the Private Sector_) and the public-sector _Act Respecting Access to Documents Held by Public Bodies and the Protection of Personal Information_. Phased implementation ran **September 22, 2022** → **September 22, 2023** (majority of provisions) → **September 22, 2024** (data portability).

    **What it governs:** Law 25 modernized Québec's general privacy framework to align with international standards. It governs the collection, use, disclosure, and protection of personal information (including health information) by **private-sector organizations** and by **public bodies outside the health and social services network**.

    **Relationship to Law 5:** For information within the scope of Law 5 (health and social services information held by a network establishment), Law 5 takes precedence. Law 25 applies as **residual** privacy law — for example, when a private-sector sponsor collects personal information directly from participants, when a public body outside the health network is involved, or when the information falls outside Law 5's definition of health and social services information.

    **Why you need to know it:** Law 25 introduced some of the most stringent privacy requirements in Canada, including mandatory **privacy impact assessments (PIAs)** before any new information system project and before any **communication of personal information outside Québec**; a high anonymization standard; explicit consent requirements; and significant penalties — administrative monetary penalties up to the greater of **\$10 million or 2% of worldwide turnover**, and penal sanctions up to the greater of **\$25 million or 4% of worldwide turnover**. Cross-border data transfer (including to other Canadian provinces or to non-Québec cloud servers) is one of the highest-frequency issues in industry-sponsored trials.

    **Key features:** Mandatory PIAs, privacy by default, enhanced individual rights (access, correction, portability, de-indexing), strict data minimization, and detailed rules on communication of personal information outside Québec.
  </Tab>
</Tabs>

## Institutional and Accreditation Standards

<Accordion title="Institutional REB Operating Procedures and Bylaws">
  Every hospital REB operates under a set of institution-specific policies, standard operating procedures (SOPs), and bylaws that translate federal and provincial requirements into day-to-day operational procedures. These documents specify the REB's composition, quorum requirements, scope of jurisdiction, review tracks (full board vs. delegated review), timelines for initial and continuing review, conflict of interest declaration procedures, appeals processes, and records retention policies.

  **Why you need to know them:** Institutional SOPs are the most immediately operative documents in your work as a legal representative. When a procedural question arises during a meeting — who has standing to vote, when a conflict of interest requires recusal, how an appeal is initiated — the institutional SOPs provide the answer. You should read your institution's full suite of REB SOPs and bylaws before your first meeting, and flag any provisions that appear inconsistent with higher-order obligations (TCPS2, Division 5, the _Civil Code of Québec_, Law 5, or Law 25).

  **Legal dimension:** The SOPs are not merely internal guidance — they are contractual commitments made to funding agencies, accreditation bodies, and regulatory authorities. Non-compliance with the institution's own SOPs may constitute a breach of those commitments and be cited as evidence of systemic non-compliance in a Health Canada inspection or legal proceeding.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="CAREB-ACCER Standards and Accreditation">
  The Canadian Association of Research Ethics Boards (CAREB-ACCER) offers a voluntary accreditation program for Canadian REBs. Accreditation involves an assessment of the REB's policies, procedures, and practices against a set of national standards aligned with TCPS2 and ICH-GCP.

  **Why you need to know it:** If your institution's REB is CAREB-ACCER accredited, or if it is working toward accreditation, the accreditation standards define an additional set of operational benchmarks. Accreditation may also be required by multi-site trial sponsors as a condition of authorizing your institution as a participating site. Familiarity with accreditation standards helps you understand the quality assurance framework within which the REB operates and supports your contribution to inspection readiness.

  **Legal dimension:** While CAREB-ACCER accreditation is voluntary, it is increasingly cited in research contracts and sponsor qualification assessments as a marker of REB quality. Non-accreditation can affect the institution's ability to participate in high-value multi-site trials.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Health Canada Guidance Documents for REBs">
  Health Canada publishes a suite of guidance documents specifically directed at REBs reviewing clinical trials. These are not legally binding, but they represent Health Canada's interpretation of the regulatory requirements and define the expectations against which the regulator will assess compliance.

  **Key documents:**

  - _Guidance for Research Ethics Boards — Drug Regulated Clinical Trials_: Addresses how Division 5 requirements apply to REB review of drug trials, including what documentation the REB must receive from the sponsor, what the approval letter must contain, and how ongoing oversight obligations work.
  - _Guideline for Good Clinical Practice E6(R3)_: Health Canada's currently adopted GCP standard as of April 1, 2026, referenced directly by the revised GUI-0100.
  - _Guidance on Clinical Trial Applications_: Relevant to understanding the sponsor's regulatory obligations, which inform your review of sponsor qualifications and agreements.

  **Why you need to know them:** Guidance documents fill gaps in the regulations and provide the interpretive framework that Health Canada inspectors apply. If a guidance document says that the REB approval letter must contain specific elements, the absence of those elements in your institution's template is a compliance gap — even if the regulation itself is ambiguous. Review these documents in conjunction with the regulations, not in isolation.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Declaration of Helsinki">
  The Declaration of Helsinki is a set of ethical principles issued by the World Medical Association (WMA) governing medical research involving human subjects. First adopted in 1964 and most recently revised in 2013, it is one of the most influential international documents in research ethics, although it does not have the force of law in Canada.

  **What it governs:** The Declaration covers physician obligations in research, the primacy of participant welfare over scientific and societal interests, requirements for ethical review, informed consent, post-trial access to beneficial treatments, and the registration and reporting of research results.

  **Why you need to know it:** While not legally binding, the Declaration of Helsinki is widely cited by courts, regulators, and ethicists as reflecting international consensus on the minimum standards for ethical research. In litigation involving research-related injury, the Declaration may be invoked as evidence of the applicable standard of care. Sponsors and investigators often reference it in protocols and consent forms. You should be familiar with its key provisions, particularly those on consent (paragraphs 25–32) and post-trial access (paragraph 34).
</Accordion>

<Warning>
  Protocol type classification matters. A study initially characterized as "observational" may become subject to Division 5 and ICH-GCP E6(R3) if it involves an unapproved drug or off-label use in a structured research context. If you are unsure whether Division 5 applies to a particular protocol, consult Health Canada's guidance on what constitutes a "clinical trial" under the regulations before the REB meeting — not during it.
</Warning>
