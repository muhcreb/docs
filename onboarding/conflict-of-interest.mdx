A Research Ethics Board depends on the independence and impartiality of every member. When a member has a personal, professional, or financial interest that could — or could reasonably appear to — influence their judgment on a protocol, that interest is a conflict of interest. Declaring conflicts promptly and fully is not an admission of wrongdoing; it is a professional obligation and a cornerstone of the board's integrity.

## What the TCPS2 Requires

The *Tri-Council Policy Statement: Ethical Conduct for Research Involving Humans* (TCPS2, 2022) addresses conflicts of interest directly. Article 6.4 requires that REB members who have a conflict of interest in relation to a research protocol **declare that conflict and absent themselves from deliberations and the decision** concerning that research. This obligation applies regardless of whether the conflict is financial, personal, or professional in nature.

<Info>
  TCPS2 Article 6.4 states: *"REB members who have a conflict of interest regarding a research proposal being reviewed shall declare the conflict of interest and absent themselves from the deliberations concerning that proposal and from the vote."* Your institution's REB Standard Operating Procedures will operationalize this requirement and may impose additional disclosure obligations.
</Info>

TCPS2 does not define "conflict of interest" exhaustively — it relies on a reasonable-person standard. Ask yourself: *Would a reasonable, informed observer believe this interest could influence my review?* If yes, declare it.

## What Constitutes a Conflict of Interest for Legal Counsel

As a lawyer or legal counsel on the REB, your conflicts may arise from sources that differ from those of your scientist or clinician colleagues. The following categories cover the most common situations.

<Tabs>
  <Tab title="Financial Interests">
    A financial conflict exists when you — or an immediate family member — stand to gain or lose financially based on the outcome of a protocol review. Examples include:

    - You or your firm has been retained (or is seeking to be retained) by the principal investigator, the sponsor, or the research institution as external legal counsel on this study or a related matter.
    - You hold shares, stock options, or other equity interests in the sponsoring company.
    - You have received honoraria, speaking fees, or consulting payments from the sponsor within the past two years.
    - Your firm represents a competitor of the sponsor in ongoing litigation where the outcome of this research could be material.
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Professional Interests">
    A professional conflict arises when your standing, career, or collegial relationships could be affected by the decision. Examples include:

    - You are a co-investigator or named collaborator on the protocol, even in a minor role.
    - You drafted the consent documents, sponsor agreement, or data sharing agreement that is under review.
    - You are the principal investigator's department head, direct supervisor, or reports to you.
    - You have provided a legal opinion on the protocol outside the REB process, such as through your institution's legal affairs office.
    - You are a close colleague or frequent collaborator of the principal investigator, such that your ability to offer candid criticism may be compromised.
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Personal Interests">
    A personal conflict arises from relationships or circumstances unrelated to your professional role. Examples include:

    - The principal investigator is a family member, close personal friend, or romantic partner.
    - You are a current or former patient of a clinician-investigator and the relationship may affect your objectivity.
    - You have a strong personal advocacy position (for or against) the research topic that could prevent you from applying the ethical framework evenhandedly.
    - You are a potential or current participant in the study under review.
  </Tab>
</Tabs>

## When and How to Declare a Conflict

Timing matters. Early declaration allows the chair to manage the agenda efficiently and protects you from the appearance of having participated in a review you should have sat out.

<Steps>
  <Step title="Declare Before the Meeting When Possible">
    When you receive the meeting package, review the list of protocols, principal investigators, sponsors, and study sites. If you identify a potential conflict, notify the REB Coordinator by email **before the meeting**. State the protocol title or number and describe the nature of the conflict in general terms. The coordinator will note your declaration in the agenda and advise the chair.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Declare at the Start of the Meeting">
    At the beginning of every meeting, the chair invites members to declare conflicts with items on the agenda. Even if you pre-declared in writing, stand by your declaration verbally. This ensures it is captured in the minutes.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Declare When a Conflict Emerges Mid-Discussion">
    If a conflict becomes apparent during the presentation or discussion of a protocol — for instance, the investigator mentions a sub-site you were not aware of — declare it immediately. Interrupt politely: *"I need to declare a potential conflict before we proceed."* The chair will direct you from there.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Use Clear, Unambiguous Language">
    State your conflict plainly. For example: *"I have a conflict of interest with Protocol REB-2025-041. My firm currently represents the sponsor, BioPharm Inc., on an unrelated regulatory matter."* You do not need to provide extensive detail, but the nature of the conflict should be intelligible from the minutes.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Leave the Room (or Virtual Meeting Room)">
    After declaring, you must absent yourself from **both the deliberations and the vote**. In person, leave the meeting room. In a virtual meeting, the chair will typically place you in a waiting room or ask you to disconnect until the item is concluded. Do not remain in the room as a silent observer — even passive presence can create the appearance of influence.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Return and Be Briefed Minimally">
    Once the item is concluded, the chair will invite you back. The minutes will record your absence and the decision reached. You will not be told how individual members voted or the full content of the deliberations you missed.
  </Step>
</Steps>

## What Happens After You Declare

<CardGroup cols={2}>
  <Card title="Quorum Is Reassessed" icon="users">
    After you recuse yourself, the chair confirms that quorum is still met with the remaining members. If your departure drops attendance below quorum, the protocol may need to be deferred or reviewed at a subsequent meeting.
  </Card>
  <Card title="The Decision Proceeds Without You" icon="gavel">
    The remaining board members deliberate and vote on the protocol without your input. Your legal perspective will be absent from that discussion — which is why the board must have sufficient legal competency among other members or may seek an external legal opinion.
  </Card>
  <Card title="Your Conflict Is Recorded" icon="file-pen">
    The minutes will record your name, the protocol, and the nature of the declared conflict. This record is part of the official documentation of the meeting and may be reviewed during institutional audits or external accreditation processes.
  </Card>
  <Card title="No Penalty for Declaring" icon="shield-check">
    Declaring a conflict is the correct and expected behaviour. The board's conflict of interest process exists precisely for this situation. No adverse inference is drawn from a declaration.
  </Card>
</CardGroup>

## Institutional COI Policies

Your hospital or research institution will have its own conflict of interest policy that applies to all REB members, and it may go beyond TCPS2 requirements. Common institutional additions include:

- A requirement to submit a **written annual disclosure form** covering financial interests and external relationships
- A defined **look-back period** (often two years) for financial relationships with sponsors
- A requirement to declare conflicts to your department head or compliance office in addition to the REB
- A threshold below which honoraria or gifts are not considered material (though the REB's own standard may be stricter)

<Note>
  Obtain a copy of your institution's COI policy from the REB Coordinator or the Research Ethics Office on your first day. Read it before your first meeting. If you are uncertain whether a particular relationship triggers disclosure obligations, err on the side of declaring — you can always explain that the conflict may be minor, but the record will show you acted in good faith.
</Note>

## Common Conflict-of-Interest Scenarios

The following scenarios illustrate how to apply the declaration obligation in practice.

<Accordion title="Scenario 1: Your Firm Represents the Sponsor">
  **Situation:** You are legal counsel at a firm that has an active retainer with PharmaCo Ltd. A protocol sponsored by PharmaCo is on today's REB agenda.

  **Analysis:** This is a clear financial and professional conflict. Your firm's commercial relationship with the sponsor creates an obvious incentive — conscious or otherwise — to view the sponsor's protocol favourably. Declare immediately and recuse.

  **What to do:** Pre-declare to the REB Coordinator when you receive the agenda. At the meeting, declare verbally and leave the room during the review. Advise the REB Coordinator so that the board can determine whether an external legal opinion is needed.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Scenario 2: You Drafted the Consent Form">
  **Situation:** You work in your hospital's legal affairs office and were asked last month to review the consent form for a protocol now before the REB.

  **Analysis:** You have a professional conflict. Reviewing the same document in your REB capacity would mean sitting in judgment of your own prior legal work. You have a personal interest in the form being approved as drafted, and the board loses the benefit of an independent legal review.

  **What to do:** Declare the conflict at the meeting or in advance. Consider also flagging this to the REB Chair so they can determine whether the board should request an independent legal review of the consent documents.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Scenario 3: The Investigator Is a Close Friend">
  **Situation:** The principal investigator on a protocol is a personal friend whom you see regularly outside of work.

  **Analysis:** This may or may not rise to a conflict depending on the closeness of the relationship. A casual professional acquaintance is generally not a conflict; a close personal friendship that could make it difficult for you to raise critical concerns is.

  **What to do:** Apply the reasonable-observer test. If you believe a knowledgeable outside observer would question your impartiality, declare. When in doubt, disclose to the chair and let the chair assist you in making the determination.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Scenario 4: You Are a Named Collaborator on the Study">
  **Situation:** Your name appears in a footnote of the protocol as a legal advisor to the investigator team during the study design phase. You did not realize the protocol would come to the REB while you were still a member.

  **Analysis:** This is a conflict. As a named study collaborator, you have a professional interest in the study proceeding. Your prior involvement means you cannot provide an independent evaluation of the consent documents or protocol design.

  **What to do:** Declare immediately. Notify the REB Chair and Coordinator. Depending on your institution's policy, you may need to consider whether ongoing participation as a collaborator is compatible with your REB membership for the duration of this study.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Scenario 5: Conflict Emerges During Discussion">
  **Situation:** During the presentation of a protocol, the primary reviewer mentions that the data will be stored with a cloud provider. You recall that your firm recently advised that same cloud provider on a data residency matter.

  **Analysis:** A conflict has emerged mid-review. You had no way to identify it from the meeting package.

  **What to do:** Interrupt the discussion and declare: *"I need to declare a potential conflict — my firm may have a relationship with the named data storage provider. I should step out."* The chair will confirm whether your departure is required. This is exactly the kind of situation the mid-meeting declaration process is designed to handle.
</Accordion>

## Consequences of Undisclosed Conflicts

<Warning>
  Failing to declare a known conflict of interest is a serious breach of your professional and ethical obligations. Consequences can include:

  - **Invalidation of the REB decision** — A protocol approved in the presence of an undisclosed conflict may be challenged and the approval set aside, exposing the institution and investigator to regulatory and legal risk.
  - **Professional discipline** — As a lawyer, you are subject to your provincial law society's rules of professional conduct. Participating in a decision while in a material conflict — without disclosure — may constitute professional misconduct and attract disciplinary proceedings.
  - **Personal liability** — Depending on the circumstances, undisclosed participation in a conflicted decision could expose you to personal liability in a subsequent legal challenge to the research.
  - **Removal from the board** — Your institution may have grounds to terminate your appointment if a serious undisclosed conflict is discovered.
  - **Reputational harm** — REB decisions and member conduct can come under public scrutiny, particularly in the event of a research ethics complaint or participant harm.

  If you realize — even after a meeting — that you participated in a decision while in a conflict you failed to declare, notify the REB Chair and your institution's Research Integrity Officer promptly. Early self-disclosure is treated far more favourably than a conflict discovered through external means.
</Warning>
