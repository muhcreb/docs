Your appointment as a legal representative is a formal designation — most REB constitutions and the TCPS 2 require that at least one member bring legal expertise to the board. You are not a passive observer. You hold a voting seat, bear full member responsibilities, and are expected to apply your legal knowledge actively and constructively at every stage of the review process.

## Your Core Responsibilities

<Steps>
  <Step title="Review assigned protocols for legal risk">
    Before each meeting, read the protocols assigned to you in full. Identify provisions that raise legal concerns: consent form language, data-sharing arrangements, indemnification clauses in sponsor agreements, jurisdiction issues in multi-site trials, or privacy practices that may conflict with applicable legislation.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Draft or comment on written legal assessments">
    For full-board reviews and complex delegated reviews, prepare a concise written note flagging legal issues. Route it to the REB Coordinator before the meeting so the chair can incorporate it into the agenda. For minor amendments, a verbal comment at the meeting is usually sufficient.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Participate in deliberation and vote">
    Attend scheduled meetings, engage in deliberation, and cast votes. The REB reaches decisions collectively; your vote carries equal weight to every other member's. Abstain only when you have a genuine conflict of interest — not simply because a protocol raises difficult legal questions.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Advise on consent processes and capacity">
    Review informed consent documents for legal adequacy: Are the required elements present? Is the language accessible? Are substitute decision-maker provisions legally compliant for the jurisdiction? Flag discrepancies between what the protocol proposes and what the law requires.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Monitor continuing review and amendments">
    Your role does not end at initial approval. When researchers submit annual continuing review reports or protocol amendments, apply the same legal lens you used at initial review. Escalate immediately if you learn of a serious adverse event or protocol deviation that carries legal implications.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Support the REB in responding to complaints and audits">
    If a participant complaint, regulatory audit, or institutional review touches on legal matters, the REB Chair may ask you to assist in preparing the board's response. Work closely with institutional legal counsel in these situations — your role is to advise the board, not to act as the institution's external counsel.
  </Step>
</Steps>

## Scope: Legal Review vs. Scientific and Ethical Review

Understanding the boundary of your role protects the board's deliberative integrity and prevents legal review from crowding out scientific and ethical judgment.

<Tabs>
  <Tab title="What You Review">
    - **Consent documentation**: legal sufficiency of language, required statutory elements, capacity and substitute decision-maker provisions
    - **Data governance**: compliance with Law 5, Law 25, PIPEDA (where applicable), applicable U.S. or EU law for communications outside Québec, and information-sharing / data-processing agreements
    - **Research agreements**: sponsor indemnification, publication rights, intellectual property clauses that could compromise participant protection or researcher independence
    - **Recruitment materials**: advertising language that may be coercive or constitute an unauthorized inducement under applicable law
    - **Vulnerable population safeguards**: legal requirements for minors (age of assent vs. age of consent by province), prisoners, individuals under guardianship
    - **Jurisdictional questions**: which law governs multi-site or international studies
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="What Others Review">
    - **Scientific validity** (scientific members and external reviewers): statistical methodology, study design, feasibility — you are not expected to evaluate these
    - **Risk-benefit analysis** (the full board): weighing the scientific merit and social value of the research against the risks to participants — this is a collective ethical judgment, not a legal determination
    - **Community and cultural considerations** (community and patient members): lived experience, cultural safety, and the perspectives of the populations being studied
    - **Clinical safety** (clinician members): medical standard-of-care questions, clinical monitoring requirements, adverse event thresholds
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Gray Areas">
    Some issues genuinely straddle the boundary. When a question is both an ethical concern and a legal concern — for example, whether a financial incentive is "undue inducement" — frame your contribution as legal context, not a legal veto. The board decides. Common gray areas include:

    - Compensation levels for participant time
    - Return of incidental findings (legal duty vs. ethical obligation)
    - Withdrawal rights and data retention after withdrawal
    - Emergency research and waiver of consent
  </Tab>
</Tabs>

## Authority and Its Limits

<Warning>
  You are a member of the REB, not the institution's legal counsel in your REB capacity. Do not provide legal opinions to researchers, sponsors, or the institution based on your REB role. If a researcher asks you directly for legal advice, direct them to the hospital's Office of Research or institutional legal counsel.
</Warning>

The REB's authority is real but bounded:

- The board may **approve, approve with conditions, defer, or reject** research protocols.
- The board may **suspend or terminate** ongoing research it previously approved.
- The board **cannot** negotiate contracts on the institution's behalf, bind the hospital legally, or override decisions made by institutional administration on matters outside the board's mandate.
- Your legal expertise informs deliberation — the **board as a whole** makes every decision by vote.

## Contributing Constructively Without Dominating

Legal language can be intimidating, and legal analysis can inadvertently derail discussions that should center on participant welfare. Apply these principles:

<Accordion title="Lead with questions, not conclusions">
  Rather than announcing "this consent form violates Law 5," ask "Can the researcher explain how the data-retention provision aligns with Law 5's requirement that health and social services information be retained only as long as necessary for the research purpose?" Questions invite dialogue; pronouncements shut it down.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Translate, don't lecture">
  Other board members are not lawyers. When you raise a legal issue, explain the practical implication for participants in plain language. Cite the specific provision, but always answer the question: "What does this mean for the people in the study?"
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Distinguish legal bars from legal best practices">
  Some things are legally prohibited; others are legally sub-optimal but not unlawful. Be explicit about which category an issue falls into. "This clause is unenforceable" is different from "this clause is unusual and we should ask the sponsor to explain their rationale."
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Escalate appropriately">
  If a protocol raises legal issues that go beyond your expertise or require an institutional response (e.g., a potential regulatory violation), flag it for the Chair and recommend involving institutional legal counsel. You do not need to resolve every legal question yourself.
</Accordion>

<Tip>
  When you are uncertain whether something is a legal problem or simply an unfamiliar practice in a new research area, ask the researcher or study coordinator to walk the board through that specific aspect of the protocol. Clarifying questions almost always surface the information you need and demonstrate engaged, thorough review — without putting you or the board in an awkward position.
</Tip>

## Preparation and Attendance Expectations

<CardGroup cols={2}>
  <Card title="Before Each Meeting" icon="calendar-check" href="/onboarding/role-overview">
    Read assigned protocols and consent documents in full. Submit written legal notes to the Coordinator at least 48 hours before the meeting. Review the agenda for any items that may require legal input even if you are not the primary reviewer.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Attendance" icon="user-check" href="/onboarding/reb-structure">
    Most REB SOPs require members to attend a minimum percentage of meetings per year (commonly 75%). Notify the Chair or Coordinator as early as possible if you cannot attend. Consistent absence can affect quorum and may result in removal from the board.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Conflicts of Interest" icon="triangle-exclamation" href="/onboarding/role-overview">
    Disclose any conflict of interest — real, potential, or perceived — to the Chair before the meeting. This includes matters involving your firm's clients, personal relationships with investigators, or financial interests in a study's sponsor. When in doubt, disclose.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Confidentiality" icon="lock" href="/onboarding/role-overview">
    All protocols, deliberations, and member discussions are strictly confidential. You must not share protocol information outside the board, including with colleagues at your firm, unless specifically authorized in writing by the REB Chair and the researcher.
  </Card>
</CardGroup>
