As a legal representative on a Research Ethics Board (REB), you operate at the intersection of law, medicine, and ethics. Before you review your first protocol, you need a clear map of the regulatory landscape — who makes the rules, which rules apply, how they interact, and where your legal analysis adds the most value. This page gives you that map.

<Note>
  This page provides a high-level orientation to the Canadian legal framework for research ethics. Detailed statutory text, regulatory citations, and annotated guidance are available in the **Reference** tab of this knowledge base.
</Note>

## The Regulatory Landscape at a Glance

Canada's framework for human research ethics is not contained in a single statute. Instead, it is built from overlapping layers of federal policy, federal regulation, provincial law, and international guidelines. As a legal representative, your role is to ensure that research protocols comply with all applicable layers simultaneously.

## Major Legislation and Guidelines

The following accordions walk through each major source of law or policy. Read each one carefully — you will reference these throughout your REB tenure.

<Accordion title="Tri-Council Policy Statement: Ethical Conduct for Research Involving Humans (TCPS2)">
  The TCPS2 is the foundational ethical policy governing all research involving human participants that receives funding from Canada's three federal research councils: CIHR, NSERC, and SSHRC. Canadian universities and hospitals adopt the TCPS2 as a condition of receiving federal research funding, which means that virtually every research institution in Canada is bound by it as a matter of contract and institutional policy.

  **What it covers:** Consent, privacy, confidentiality, conflicts of interest, research involving Indigenous peoples, research with vulnerable populations, clinical trials, qualitative research, and the composition and mandate of REBs.

  **Why it matters to you:** The TCPS2 is the primary document against which your REB evaluates research protocols. Its three core principles — respect for persons, concern for welfare, and justice — are the ethical lens through which every protocol is reviewed. Your legal analysis must account for whether a protocol's procedures are consistent with TCPS2 requirements, including those around consent, data handling, and participant protections. Non-compliance can jeopardize a researcher's funding and expose the institution to reputational and legal risk.

  **Key chapters to know:** Chapter 3 (Free and Informed Consent), Chapter 5 (Privacy and Confidentiality), Chapter 7 (Conflicts of Interest), Chapter 11 (Clinical Trials).
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Food and Drug Act and the Food and Drug Regulations (Division 5)">
  For clinical trials involving drugs, biologics, or medical devices, the _Food and Drug Act_ (R.S.C., 1985, c. F-27) and its associated regulations are federal law. Division 5 of Part C of the _Food and Drug Regulations_ specifically governs clinical trials in humans.

  **What it covers:** Clinical trial authorization (CTA) requirements, investigator and sponsor obligations, record-keeping, serious adverse event (SAE) reporting, and the requirement for REB approval before a trial may proceed.

  **Why it matters to you:** Health Canada enforces Division 5, and legal compliance with its requirements is mandatory — not aspirational. You must verify that a trial's design, consent process, SAE reporting procedures, and sponsor qualifications meet regulatory requirements. Non-compliance can trigger Health Canada enforcement action, including trial suspension and significant institutional liability.

  **Key provisions to know:** C.05.001 (definitions), C.05.006 (prohibited trials without CTA), C.05.009 (REB approval requirements), C.05.012 (SAE reporting timelines), C.05.017 (record retention).
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Health Canada and the Clinical Trials Oversight Role">
  Health Canada is the federal department responsible for regulating clinical trials involving drugs and medical devices under the _Food and Drug Act_. It reviews Clinical Trial Applications (CTAs), inspects sites, and can order a trial to stop.

  **What it covers:** Health Canada's Therapeutic Products Directorate (TPD) and Biologics and Genetic Therapies Directorate (BGTD) administer trial authorizations, review safety signals, and conduct compliance inspections of REBs, sponsors, and research sites.

  **Why it matters to you:** Health Canada inspections include audits of REB records and processes. If your REB's review procedures do not meet Division 5 requirements, Health Canada can find the institution non-compliant, which may result in a trial being halted and public findings posted. You should be familiar with Health Canada's guidance documents for REBs, particularly _Guidance for Research Ethics Boards: Drugs — Clinical Trials_.

  **Key resources:** Health Canada's adopted version of _ICH E6(R3) — Good Clinical Practice_ (which superseded the earlier "Integrated Addendum to E6(R1)/E6(R2)" in 2025), REB Guidance documents, and inspection readiness checklists.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="ICH-GCP E6(R3) — Good Clinical Practice Guidelines">
  The International Council for Harmonisation (ICH) Guideline for Good Clinical Practice E6(R3) is an international ethical and scientific quality standard for designing, conducting, recording, and reporting clinical trials. ICH finalized E6(R3) in January 2025, and Health Canada formally adopted it — updating its own guidance document (GUI-0100) to match — effective April 1, 2026, replacing the prior E6(R2). Compliance with it is the expected standard of practice for clinical trials in Canada.

  **What it covers:** Eleven overarching Principles, plus Annex 1, which applies those Principles across four sections: IRB/IEC responsibilities, investigator obligations, sponsor obligations, and a new Data Governance section covering data integrity and computerised systems.

  **Why it matters to you:** Annex 1, Section 1 is dedicated entirely to IRB/IEC (i.e., REB) responsibilities and procedures. It specifies what the REB must review, the documentation it must retain, and the timeline requirements for initial and continuing review. As a legal representative, you should understand these obligations because they define the standard of care against which REB conduct may be assessed in litigation or regulatory proceedings.

  **Key sections to know:** Annex 1, Section 1 (IRB/IEC responsibilities), Section 2 (investigator responsibilities, including informed consent at 2.8), Section 3 (sponsor responsibilities), Section 4 (data governance — new in E6(R3)). See the Reference tab's dedicated ICH-GCP page for a full section-by-section breakdown.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Québec Legal Framework — Civil Code, Law 5, Law 25, and the Act Respecting Health Services and Social Services">
  Québec has a distinct legal regime for research ethics that differs materially from the common-law provinces. As a legal representative on a Québec hospital-based REB, you must apply the following provincial instruments in addition to federal law.

  **_Civil Code of Québec_ (CCQ), articles 20–25:** The CCQ directly governs consent to research involving the human body. Article 20 requires free and informed consent for research that may involve serious risk to health. Articles 21 and 24 impose specific requirements for research involving minors, persons incapable of giving consent, and the form of consent (which must generally be in writing and given before a competent REB). Article 25 addresses the non-commercialization of the human body and its parts. These provisions are enforceable law, not guidance.

  **Law 5 — _Act Respecting Health and Social Services Information_ (in force July 1, 2024):** Law 5 is the primary statute governing health and social services information held by bodies in Québec's health and social services network, including hospitals. It sets out the rules for the collection, use, communication, retention, and destruction of health and social services information, and it defines the specific regime that applies to the use of that information for research purposes — including the conditions for use with or without the consent of the individual concerned, the required approvals, and the obligations of researchers and institutions.

  <Warning>
    **Law 5 takes precedence over Law 25 for hospital-based REBs.** Because your institution is a body in the health and social services network, health and social services information held by the hospital is governed by Law 5, not by Law 25 or its underlying private-sector and public-body privacy statutes. Apply Law 5 first for any protocol that involves access to hospital-held health information.
  </Warning>

  **Law 25 (formerly Bill 64) — _Act to modernize legislative provisions as regards the protection of personal information_:** Law 25 amended Québec's _Act Respecting the Protection of Personal Information in the Private Sector_ and the _Act Respecting Access to Documents Held by Public Bodies and the Protection of Personal Information_. It remains relevant when a protocol involves personal information that falls outside the scope of Law 5 — for example, information collected directly from participants by a private-sector sponsor, or information held by a public body that is not part of the health and social services network. The _Commission d'accès à l'information_ (CAI) enforces both Law 5 (jointly with sectoral authorities) and Law 25.

  **_Act Respecting Health Services and Social Services_ (ARHSSS):** The ARHSSS continues to govern the organization and operation of institutions in the Québec health and social services network, including the role of the director of professional services. Access to health information for research is now primarily governed by Law 5 rather than the former ARHSSS provisions on clinical records.

  **MSSS Ministerial framework and designated REBs:** Under the _Ministère de la Santé et des Services sociaux_ (MSSS) framework, certain REBs are designated by the Minister of Health under article 21 CCQ to review research involving minors and incapable adults. Designated status carries additional procedural and composition requirements that your REB must satisfy.

  **FRQ _Standards on the Responsible Conduct of Research_:** The _Fonds de recherche du Québec_ (FRQ) publishes standards that apply to research funded by the FRQ and are widely adopted by Québec institutions as the ethical baseline alongside the TCPS2.

  **Why it matters to you:** When a protocol involves access to hospital-held health records, biological samples linked to identifiable individuals, data linkage, or research with minors or incapable adults, the CCQ and Law 5 — not federal privacy statutes, not the privacy laws of other provinces, and not (as a general matter) Law 25 — control what the REB must assess before approving the research. Failure to properly analyze CCQ consent requirements or Law 5 obligations is the most significant source of legal risk for a Québec hospital.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Institutional Policies and Bylaws">
  Beyond federal and provincial law, your hospital's own institutional policies, REB operating procedures, and bylaws constitute an additional layer of obligation. These documents may impose requirements that exceed the minimum legal standard — and compliance with them is typically a condition of employment, appointment, and institutional accreditation.

  **What they cover:** Composition and quorum requirements for the REB, scope of REB jurisdiction, conflict of interest procedures, appeals processes, continuing review timelines, and records retention.

  **Why it matters to you:** Institutional policies are the most immediately actionable layer of the framework. When a conflict arises between what a researcher wants and what the REB requires, institutional policies provide the procedural framework for resolution. Your legal analysis should identify when institutional policies are inconsistent with higher-order obligations (federal law, provincial statute) and flag those inconsistencies for the REB Chair and institutional administration.
</Accordion>

## How These Layers Interact

When you review a protocol, these legal layers do not operate in isolation. They must all be satisfied simultaneously. The hierarchy works as follows:

<Steps>
  <Step title="Federal law prevails">
    The _Food and Drug Act_ and its regulations are federal law. No provincial statute, institutional policy, or REB decision can authorize conduct that violates federal law. For clinical trials of drugs and devices, federal compliance is the non-negotiable floor.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Québec law adds specific obligations">
    Once federal requirements are met, the _Civil Code of Québec_ and Law 5 impose additional obligations on consent to research and on the handling of hospital-held health and social services information. Law 25 applies to personal information that falls outside the scope of Law 5. A protocol may be scientifically sound and federally compliant but still require modification to comply with CCQ articles 20–25, Law 5, or Law 25.
  </Step>
  <Step title="TCPS2 sets the ethical standard">
    For all research — not just clinical trials — the TCPS2 defines the ethical standard the REB applies. Many TCPS2 requirements overlap with legal requirements, but some go beyond the legal minimum. The REB must be satisfied on both counts.
  </Step>
  <Step title="ICH-GCP defines the practice standard for clinical trials">
    For clinical trials, ICH-GCP defines the expected practice standard. Your institution's accreditation and Health Canada's inspection expectations are calibrated against ICH-GCP.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Institutional policy operationalizes everything">
    Institutional policies translate all of the above into day-to-day REB procedures. They specify timelines, documentation standards, and escalation paths. Your job is to ensure those policies are consistent with higher-order obligations.
  </Step>
</Steps>

## Your Role as Legal Representative During Protocol Review

<Info>
  Your value to the REB is not to second-guess the scientific or clinical experts on the board. It is to identify legal risks that the protocol's design, consent process, data handling plan, or sponsor agreements may create for the institution and for participants.
</Info>

During protocol review, apply this framework by asking:

1. **Federal compliance:** Does the protocol comply with the _Food and Drug Act_ and Division 5 requirements if it is a clinical trial? Has a CTA been filed where required?
2. **Québec privacy and consent compliance:** Does the consent process satisfy CCQ articles 20–25 (including the special requirements for minors and incapable adults)? For hospital-held health and social services information, does the data collection, use, or disclosure plan comply with Law 5? For any personal information outside the scope of Law 5, does it comply with Law 25? Is a use of information without consent for research purposes properly justified and documented under the applicable regime?
3. **TCPS2 compliance:** Does the consent process meet TCPS2 Chapter 3 requirements? Are vulnerable populations protected per TCPS2 requirements? Are conflicts of interest disclosed and managed?
4. **ICH-GCP compliance:** For clinical trials, does the sponsor agreement, monitoring plan, and SAE reporting process meet ICH-GCP requirements?
5. **Institutional policy compliance:** Do the proposed procedures align with your institution's REB operating procedures and any relevant institutional policies?

<Tip>
  Build a personal checklist that maps each of these five compliance domains to specific provisions. Over time, this checklist will become your most efficient tool for rapid protocol review.
</Tip>
