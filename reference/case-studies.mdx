The legal issues that arise in REB review are rarely abstract. They emerge from the fine print of sponsor agreements, the wording of a consent form, a researcher's undisclosed financial relationship, or the interaction of privacy statutes across provincial borders. The following case studies are drawn from recurring patterns in Canadian research ethics practice and illustrate how a prepared legal representative can materially improve the board's analysis and protect both participants and the institution.

<Info>
  These cases are composite illustrations based on common REB review scenarios. They do not represent the facts of any specific institution or proceeding. Details have been generalized to serve educational purposes.
</Info>

## How to Use These Case Studies

Read each case scenario carefully before reviewing the legal issues and the legal representative's contribution. Consider how you would have approached the same situation given your own institutional context and the specific legislation in your province. The goal is not to produce a single "correct" answer but to develop a framework for legal analysis that you can adapt to novel protocols.

## Case Studies

<Accordion title="Case 1 — Indemnification Clause Excluding Negligence in an Industry-Sponsored Trial">
  ### Scenario

  A large pharmaceutical company submitted a Phase III randomized controlled trial to the REB for full board review. The trial tested a novel monoclonal antibody therapy in patients with treatment-resistant rheumatoid arthritis. The clinical trial agreement (CTA) submitted alongside the protocol contained an indemnification clause in which the sponsor agreed to indemnify the hospital and principal investigator for claims arising from the study — but with an explicit carve-out excluding liability for losses "arising from the negligence, wilful misconduct, or failure to comply with the protocol by the Institution or Investigator."

  The clause was presented as standard industry language. The REB chair initially flagged it as a contractual matter to be resolved outside the ethics review. The legal representative requested that the item be held for discussion before the ethics approval letter was issued.

  ### Legal Issues Raised

  1. **Participant protection gap**: If a participant suffers harm as a result of the hospital's or investigator's failure to follow the protocol — even an inadvertent, non-wilful failure — the sponsor's indemnification would not respond, leaving the hospital and the participant exposed.
  2. **Scope of "negligence" carve-out**: In tort law, "negligence" is a broad category. A carve-out of this breadth could encompass protocol deviations caused by under-resourced study teams, inadequate investigator training, or systems failures unrelated to the sponsor's conduct.
  3. **Consent form alignment**: The participant-facing consent form stated that the sponsor would "cover costs related to study injuries." This representation was inconsistent with the CTA's carve-out and potentially misleading to participants.
  4. **Institutional liability exposure**: The hospital's risk management team had not been provided the CTA before the ethics submission. Standard practice — and institutional policy — required legal and risk management sign-off on CTAs prior to REB approval.

  ### Legal Representative's Contribution

  The legal representative identified the indemnification carve-out during pre-meeting review of the CTA and brought a written analysis to the full board. She explained the difference between a sponsor indemnifying the institution versus the institution retaining residual liability, using accessible language for non-lawyer board members. She also flagged the inconsistency between the CTA's carve-out and the consent form's injury coverage language, noting that this inconsistency could give rise to a claim by a participant who relied on the consent form representation.

  The legal representative recommended that the REB condition approval on: (1) revision of the consent form to accurately describe the limits of sponsor indemnification; (2) confirmation from the hospital's legal department and risk management that the CTA had been reviewed and was acceptable from an institutional standpoint; and (3) inclusion of a plain-language explanation of the participant's recourse options in the event of a study injury.

  ### Outcome and Lesson Learned

  The REB issued a conditional approval with the requirements above. The sponsor's legal team agreed to narrow the negligence carve-out to "gross negligence and wilful misconduct" — a more standard formulation — and the consent form was revised to reflect the limits of sponsor coverage accurately. Risk management signed off on the revised CTA before the study commenced.

  **Lesson**: Contract language that may appear standard to a sponsor is not always acceptable from a participant protection or institutional liability perspective. The legal representative's role is to translate contractual risk into concrete implications for participants and the institution — and to ensure that the consent form accurately reflects the protections (and their limits) that participants will receive.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Case 2 — Consent Form Language for a Cognitively Vulnerable Population">
  ### Scenario

  A university research team submitted a minimal-risk survey study to the REB for delegated review. The study sought to evaluate quality-of-life outcomes in adults with mild-to-moderate dementia residing in long-term care facilities. The consent form and capacity assessment procedure were forwarded to the legal representative as part of the pre-meeting package.

  The consent form was eleven pages long. It was written at a Grade 13 reading level (confirmed by a Flesch–Kincaid analysis the legal representative ran independently), contained cross-references to other sections, used phrases such as "phenotypically representative cohort," "longitudinal observational methodology," and "data de-identification protocols," and included a seven-point bullet list of participant rights written in compound-negative sentence structures.

  The consent form also included a section providing for consent by a substitute (in Québec, typically the mandatary, tutor, or curator under arts. 15 and 21 CCQ) but did not describe the capacity assessment process, specify who would conduct it, or address what would happen if a prospective participant's capacity fluctuated during the study.

  ### Legal Issues Raised

  1. **Validity of consent**: Under both the TCPS2 and, in Québec, arts. 11–25 of the _Civil Code of Québec_ (governing consent to care and consent to research), consent must be informed and given by a person with capacity — or, where capacity is absent, by the authorized substitute (tutor, curator, mandatary, or the person listed in art. 15 CCQ for consent to care) acting in the person's best interests and prior expressed wishes. Article 24 CCQ additionally requires research consent to be given in writing. A consent form that the participant cannot comprehend cannot support legally valid consent.
  2. **Capacity assessment gap**: The absence of a described capacity assessment process created uncertainty about who would make the capacity determination, what criteria would apply, and whether the assessment would be documented. This was both a legal and an ethical deficiency.
  3. **Substitute authorization**: The form did not clarify which categories of substitute were authorized under Québec law (mandatary under a protection mandate, tutor, curator, or — for consent to care under art. 15 CCQ — spouse or close relative in the statutory order), nor whether the institutional policy on substitute authorization had been applied.
  4. **Assent**: The TCPS2 and art. 21 CCQ jurisprudence recognize that individuals with diminished capacity — but who retain some ability to understand — should provide their assent in addition to substitute consent, and the CCQ recognizes their right to refuse participation. The form made no provision for participant assent.

  ### Legal Representative's Contribution

  The legal representative prepared a markup of the consent form annotating each identified deficiency with a citation to the TCPS2 provision or CCQ article engaged. She presented the analysis under four headings — readability, capacity assessment, substitute authorization under the CCQ, and assent — and recommended that the REB require substantial revision before any conditional or full approval was granted.

  She also noted that the cognitive vulnerability of this population engaged TCPS2 Chapter 4 (consent) and Chapter 9 (research involving adults in institutionalized settings), and that the board should consider whether the study offered a prospect of direct benefit to participants sufficient to justify any residual risk — a threshold the TCPS2 applies to research involving persons with diminished decision-making capacity.

  ### Outcome and Lesson Learned

  The REB returned the protocol to the investigator with a detailed list of required revisions. The research team worked with a health literacy specialist to produce a revised two-page consent form written at a Grade 6 level, supplemented by a visual information sheet. The team added a structured capacity assessment tool administered by a trained research coordinator at enrollment and at each follow-up contact, and revised the substitute-consent section to reflect the CCQ hierarchy for consent to research (art. 21 CCQ, read with arts. 15 and 258 CCQ). The study ultimately proceeded with a design that prioritized participants' expressed preferences and obtained assent alongside substitute consent.

  **Lesson**: The legal representative's role in consent review is not merely to confirm that a consent form exists — it is to evaluate whether the form can actually discharge the legal and ethical function of informed consent for the specific population involved. Vulnerability heightens this obligation, not diminishes it.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Case 3 — Cross-Border Data Transfer in a Québec Chronic Pain Survey Study">
  ### Scenario

  A Québec research hospital submitted an online survey study examining health-care-seeking behaviour in individuals living with chronic pain. The study proposed to collect self-reported health information, including diagnosed conditions, medication use, and descriptions of past health-care interactions. Participants would be recruited through social media platforms and primary care clinic waiting rooms across three CIUSSS regions in Québec. The data would be captured through a REDCap-style platform hosted by a U.S. cloud vendor, with analytics performed by a research partner based outside Québec.

  The privacy section of the protocol cited only _PIPEDA_ and noted that the data would be "de-identified" before analysis. The consent form stated: "Your information will be kept private in accordance with Canadian law." No _évaluation des facteurs relatifs à la vie privée_ (ÉFVP) was submitted with the package.

  ### Legal Issues Raised

  1. **Wrong governing statute cited**: The protocol cited PIPEDA as if it were the primary applicable law. In fact, for information collected by the establishment in the context of the health and social services network, **Law 5** (_Act Respecting Health and Social Services Information_, in force July 1, 2024) takes precedence, and **Law 25** applies as residual private-sector privacy law. PIPEDA would only apply, if at all, to specific inter-provincial or federally regulated commercial flows.
  2. **Missing ÉFVP for communication outside Québec**: Both the hosting of data on a U.S. cloud platform and the transfer to an analytics partner outside Québec constitute a **communication of personal information outside Québec**. Law 25 requires a documented **ÉFVP** — considering the sensitivity of the information, the purposes, and the destination legal regime — **before** the communication begins. No ÉFVP had been prepared.
  3. **De-identification vs. anonymization**: The protocol described "de-identification" as removal of "direct identifiers." This falls below the Law 25 **anonymization** standard, which requires that identification of the individual be **irreversibly** infeasible in the reasonably foreseeable circumstances. Given that the dataset retained quasi-identifiers — age range, diagnosed condition, medication regimen, and residential region — the data remained personal information and continued to attract Law 5 / Law 25 protections.
  4. **Written-form consent**: Under art. 24 CCQ, consent to research must be given in writing. A single click-through checkbox on a survey landing page does not, on its own, satisfy the written-form requirement for research on the person; the consent architecture had not been reviewed against art. 24.
  5. **Consent-form misrepresentation**: The statement "kept private in accordance with Canadian law" was both vague and misleading. It did not disclose that data would be communicated outside Québec, did not identify the destination jurisdictions, and did not disclose the risk of foreign government access — matters that Québec REBs regularly require to be spelled out in the consent form.

  ### Legal Representative's Contribution

  The legal representative prepared a structured analysis identifying the applicable statutes (Law 5 as primary, Law 25 residually, PIPEDA only for specific residual flows), the specific deficiencies in the protocol's privacy protections, and the outstanding procedural requirement for an ÉFVP. She presented the analysis in a short table that the full board could follow and recommended that the REB require the research team to: engage the establishment's _responsable de l'accès à l'information_ to conduct an ÉFVP covering the U.S. hosting and the extra-Québec analytics partner; execute a written information-sharing / data-processing agreement with each recipient outside Québec; revise the de-identification approach either to meet Law 25's anonymization standard or to be characterized honestly as pseudonymization with continuing legal protections; restructure the consent architecture so that written consent under art. 24 CCQ was properly captured; and rewrite the consent form's privacy section to accurately describe the applicable Québec protections, the communications outside Québec, and the recourse available to participants (including complaint to the CAI).

  ### Outcome and Lesson Learned

  The protocol was returned for major revisions. The research team worked with the establishment's _responsable_ to complete an ÉFVP; ultimately, the sponsor agreed to migrate hosting to a Canadian-hosted platform with contractual assurances of data residency in Québec, which materially reduced the residual risk identified in the ÉFVP. The consent form was rewritten to describe the Québec legal framework in plain language, to identify the specific extra-Québec communications that remained, and to point participants to the CAI and to the establishment's _responsable_ for privacy complaints. De-identification was replaced with a documented pseudonymization plan plus a separate anonymization-at-publication step. The revised protocol was approved on second review.

  **Lesson**: In Québec, citing PIPEDA and calling data "de-identified" is not a compliance strategy — it is a red flag. The legal representative is often the only board member positioned to identify that Law 5 (not PIPEDA) governs, that Law 25 imposes stringent extra-Québec transfer obligations, and that CCQ art. 24 imposes a written-form consent requirement that has no analogue in most other Canadian jurisdictions.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Case 4 — Undisclosed Financial Conflict of Interest by a Principal Investigator">
  ### Scenario

  A mid-career physician-researcher submitted an annual continuing review of a Phase II industry-sponsored trial evaluating a medical device. The conflict of interest (COI) declaration on file from the previous year noted no financial interests in the sponsor company. During the continuing review, an REB member who also served on a hospital foundation board mentioned — informally, before the meeting — that she had seen the principal investigator (PI) listed as an advisor on the sponsor's website. The REB coordinator flagged the comment to the REB chair and the legal representative before the meeting convened.

  At the meeting, the PI was asked to confirm his current conflicts of interest. He disclosed, for the first time, that he had accepted an advisory board position with the sponsor six months earlier, for which he received an annual honorarium of \$15,000 and stock options in the sponsor company. He stated he had not updated his COI declaration because he "did not believe it was relevant" to his conduct of the study.

  ### Legal Issues Raised

  1. **Breach of institutional COI policy**: Most research institutions require investigators to update their COI declarations whenever a new relationship arises — typically within 30 days. A six-month delay in disclosure was a clear policy breach and potentially a breach of the research contract with the sponsor and CIHR grant conditions.
  2. **Impact on study integrity**: An advisory board position and equity interest in the sponsor created a significant financial interest in the study's success. The REB needed to assess whether this interest had influenced: (a) any protocol amendments during the undisclosed period; (b) adverse event reporting; (c) recruitment pressure; or (d) data interpretation in interim reports.
  3. **Regulatory reporting obligations**: Under Health Canada's _Food and Drug Regulations_ (Division 5) and applicable ICH E6(R3) provisions, sponsors and investigators have ongoing obligations to disclose material changes in circumstances. The failure to disclose a significant financial COI may have been a reportable protocol deviation or an event requiring Health Canada notification.
  4. **Fiduciary duty to participants**: The PI occupied a dual role — treating physician and investigator — for some study participants. Undisclosed financial interests that may influence clinical judgment engage the common law fiduciary obligations owed to patients, and potentially the _Code of Ethics_ obligations enforced by the provincial College of Physicians and Surgeons.
  5. **REB record and liability**: The REB had continued to allow the study to proceed based on a COI declaration it now knew to be inaccurate. The legal representative needed to advise on the board's prospective obligations — including whether the study could continue, what oversight conditions should be imposed, and whether the matter should be referred to the institution's research integrity officer.

  ### Legal Representative's Contribution

  The legal representative advised the REB to pause the continuing review and request an immediate written disclosure of all financial interests the PI held in the sponsor, covering the period since the initial COI declaration. She outlined for the board the distinction between managing a declared COI (through monitoring, independent data review, or oversight conditions) and proceeding on the basis of an undisclosed COI — noting that the latter undermined the reliability of all decisions made during the undisclosed period.

  She recommended that the REB: (1) document the discovery of the COI and the PI's verbal disclosure in the meeting minutes; (2) require the PI to submit a formal written disclosure and updated COI declaration within five business days; (3) refer the matter to the institution's Research Integrity Officer and, if CIHR-funded, to CIHR's Standing Committee on Ethics; (4) commission an independent review of adverse event reports and protocol amendments filed during the six-month undisclosed period; and (5) assess whether a notice to Health Canada was required under the _Food and Drug Regulations_ in light of the undisclosed investigator interest.

  The legal representative also flagged that any study participants who were also the PI's patients should be considered for notification, depending on the findings of the independent review.

  ### Outcome and Lesson Learned

  The study was suspended pending the independent review. The Research Integrity Officer opened a formal inquiry. The independent review found no evidence that adverse events had been suppressed or that data had been manipulated, but identified two amendments filed during the undisclosed period that reduced the frequency of safety monitoring visits — amendments that warranted re-review by the full board with full knowledge of the PI's financial interest. The PI received a formal reprimand and was required to implement a COI management plan going forward. Health Canada was notified of the disclosure irregularity as a protocol deviation.

  **Lesson**: Financial conflicts of interest that go undisclosed are not merely administrative oversights — they call into question the reliability of all investigator-reported data and decisions made during the period of non-disclosure. The legal representative plays a critical role in identifying the full regulatory, contractual, and professional consequences of undisclosed COIs and in ensuring the board responds systematically rather than accepting a belated verbal disclosure and moving on.
</Accordion>

## Applying These Lessons to Future Reviews

<Tip>
  After each REB meeting where a legally complex protocol is reviewed, take five minutes to note the legal issue encountered, the analysis applied, and the outcome. Over time, this personal case log becomes an invaluable reference — both for handling similar future protocols and for demonstrating your ongoing contributions to the board. Patterns across cases often reveal systemic gaps in the institution's standard templates, such as outdated consent form language or COI declaration forms that do not capture advisory board compensation — gaps you are well-positioned to raise with the REB chair.
</Tip>
