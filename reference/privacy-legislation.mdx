Privacy law applicable to research at a Québec hospital-based REB is layered: Québec's own health-sector and general-privacy statutes are the primary framework, supplemented by federal law where a federal institution, federal undertaking, or cross-provincial commercial data flow is involved. As a legal representative, you must be able to identify which of these laws apply to any given protocol, understand what each requires for the research use of personal information, and flag when a protocol's data management plan does not meet the legal standard. Getting this analysis right is especially important for protocols involving secondary use of clinical data, cross-border data transfers, and data linkage.

## The Landscape for Québec Hospital-Based Research

For research conducted in a Québec hospital (an establishment in the health and social services network), the primary privacy framework is:

- **Law 5** — the _Act Respecting Health and Social Services Information_ (in force **July 1, 2024**), which governs health and social services information held by network establishments and is the operative statute for hospital-based REBs.
- **_Civil Code of Québec_** (CCQ), arts. **20–25**, which governs consent to research involving the human body and is enforceable law, not guidance.
- **Law 25** — the modernization of Québec's general private-sector and public-sector privacy statutes, with phased implementation between **September 22, 2022** and **September 22, 2024**. Law 25 applies as **residual** privacy law for information that falls outside Law 5's scope (e.g., information held by private-sector sponsors or public bodies outside the health and social services network).

At the federal level, the _Privacy Act_ governs personal information held by federal government institutions, and the _Personal Information Protection and Electronic Documents Act_ (PIPEDA) can apply to interprovincial or international commercial data flows. Québec's private-sector privacy law has been declared substantially similar to PIPEDA, so PIPEDA is largely displaced for provincially regulated activity in Québec.

<Info>
  Determining which statute applies is a threshold legal question for every protocol involving identifiable personal information. The answer depends on: (1) the type of information (health and social services information vs. other personal information), (2) the type of organization holding it (a network establishment, another public body, a private-sector organization, or a federal institution), (3) whether information will be communicated outside Québec, and (4) whether the activity engages a federal undertaking or federal institution.
</Info>

    ## Law 5 — Act Respecting Health and Social Services Information

    **Citation:** _Act Respecting Health and Social Services Information_, CQLR c R-22.1 (commonly referred to as **Law 5**), in force **July 1, 2024**.

    Law 5 is the primary statute governing the collection, use, communication, and protection of **health and social services information** held by bodies in Québec's health and social services network — including hospitals (établissements), CIUSSS/CISSS, and their designated researchers. For hospital-based research at a Québec establishment, **Law 5 takes precedence over Law 25** for information within its scope.

    ### Key Definitions

    - **Health and social services information:** Information collected or held by a body in the health and social services network about a person that concerns their physical or mental health, the provision of health or social services, or related administrative matters. The definition is broad and includes clinical, administrative, and social services records.
    - **Body (organisme) subject to Law 5:** Establishments in the health and social services network, health professionals in private practice in some circumstances, and other bodies designated by regulation.

    ### Research Use Without Consent

    Law 5 sets out conditions under which health and social services information may be communicated to a researcher without the individual's consent. These generally require:

    1. Approval of the research by an REB **designated for this purpose by the Minister of Health and Social Services (MSSS)**.
    2. A written **research agreement** between the establishment (or its authorized custodian) and the researcher, containing prescribed conditions on use, security, retention, and destruction.
    3. A demonstration that the research purpose cannot reasonably be achieved without the use of health and social services information in identifiable form.
    4. Adequate safeguards proportionate to the sensitivity of the information.

    <Warning>
      Under Law 5, only **MSSS-designated REBs** may authorize the communication of health and social services information for research without consent. Confirm that your REB holds the required MSSS designation and that its designation covers the type of research proposed. Approval by a non-designated REB does not satisfy Law 5.
    </Warning>

    ### REB Role Under Law 5

    The REB's approval under Law 5 is a **distinct legal authorization**, separate from the ethics approval under TCPS2 and separate from any approvals required by Health Canada for a clinical trial. Your REB must satisfy itself that the Law 5 statutory criteria are met, not merely that the research is ethically acceptable. Document the Law 5 analysis explicitly in your REB decision.

    ### FRQ Standards and Ministerial Framework

    The **Fonds de recherche du Québec (FRQ)** publishes the _Standards on the Ethical Conduct of Research Involving Humans_, which set expectations for REB operations, member training, and documentation for Québec-funded research. The **Ministère de la Santé et des Services sociaux (MSSS)** issues directives and designation criteria for REBs authorized to review protocols using health and social services information. Familiarize yourself with the current versions of both — they define the operational benchmarks against which your REB's practice will be assessed.

    ### Oversight — Commission d'accès à l'information

    The **Commission d'accès à l'information (CAI)** is Québec's privacy oversight body. It has jurisdiction over both Law 5 and Law 25 (in coordination with sectoral authorities under Law 5). CAI can investigate complaints, order corrective measures, and impose administrative monetary penalties. REB decisions authorizing research use without consent should be thorough, documented, and defensible to CAI scrutiny.

    ## _Civil Code of Québec_ — Consent to Research on the Person

    **Citation:** _Civil Code of Québec_, CQLR c CCQ-1991, arts. **20–25**.

    Articles 20–25 of the CCQ directly govern **consent to research involving the human body** and are enforceable statutory law, not guidance. They operate alongside Law 5 and Law 25 and cannot be overridden by policy or contract.

    ### Article 20 — Free and Informed Consent

    Research that could interfere with the integrity of a person may be carried out only with the person's **free and informed consent**, provided the research does not involve serious risk to health. The consent must be given in writing.

    ### Article 21 — Research Involving Minors or Persons Incapable of Consent

    A minor or a person of full age incapable of giving consent may participate in research only if:

    - The research is part of a project **approved by a competent research ethics committee** (i.e., an MSSS-designated REB where applicable);
    - The research **could not be carried out** on persons capable of giving consent for themselves and yielding the same knowledge, if it is not expected to produce results directly beneficial to the participant;
    - The **risk is proportionate** to the expected benefit; and
    - Consent is given by the parent, tutor, curator, or mandatary — with the participant's own assent where they are capable of understanding.

    ### Article 24 — Form of Consent

    Consent to research must be given **in writing**, and it may be **withdrawn at any time**, even verbally. The written-form requirement is a stricter standard than exists in most other Canadian jurisdictions.

    ### Article 25 — Non-Commercialization

    The alienation of parts or products of the human body must be **gratuitous**; it cannot be the object of remuneration, except for expenses incurred. This provision is directly relevant when reviewing consent forms for tissue banking, biobanking, or trials involving human biological samples.

    <Note>
      The CCQ's consent requirements are enforceable through civil actions in the Québec courts. A defective consent process is not merely an ethics concern — it can support claims in civil liability (art. 1457 CCQ), including for interference with bodily integrity.
    </Note>

    ## Law 25 — Modernization of Québec Privacy Statutes

    **Citation:** _Act to modernize legislative provisions as regards the protection of personal information_, SQ 2021, c 25 (**Law 25**, formerly Bill 64). Law 25 substantially amended:

    - The _Act Respecting the Protection of Personal Information in the Private Sector_ (LPRPSP), governing private-sector organizations in Québec.
    - The _Act Respecting Access to Documents Held by Public Bodies and the Protection of Personal Information_ (Access Act), governing public bodies.

    Law 25's amendments were phased in on **September 22, 2022**, **September 22, 2023** (the majority of substantive provisions), and **September 22, 2024** (data portability).

    ### Relationship to Law 5

    For information within the scope of Law 5 (health and social services information held by a network establishment), **Law 5 takes precedence**. Law 25 applies as **residual** privacy law when:

    - A private-sector sponsor collects personal information directly from participants, or
    - A public body outside the health and social services network is involved, or
    - Personal information falls outside Law 5's definition of health and social services information.

    ### Core Requirements

    - **Privacy impact assessments (PIAs):** Required before any new information system project involving personal information, and before any communication of personal information outside Québec.
    - **Anonymization:** Under Law 25, information is only truly anonymous if it is, at all times, reasonably foreseeable in the circumstances that it can no longer irreversibly allow identification of the individual. De-identified information that could be re-linked remains personal information.
    - **Consent:** Consent must be **free, informed, and specific**, and given for **specific purposes**. Implied consent is generally not sufficient for sensitive personal information (which includes health information).
    - **Research exception (LPRPSP, private sector):** Personal information may be communicated without consent for study, research, or statistical purposes if the objective can only be achieved by using information in a form permitting identification, and — depending on the pathway used under the amended LPRPSP — subject to a documented privacy impact assessment and, where required, an information-sharing agreement whose terms are set out in the statute.
    - **Penalties:** Non-compliance can attract administrative monetary penalties of up to the greater of **\$10 million or 2% of worldwide turnover**, and penal sanctions of up to the greater of **\$25 million or 4% of worldwide turnover** — among the most stringent in Canada.

    ### Cross-Border Transfers

    Before communicating personal information **outside Québec** — including to another Canadian province or to a cloud provider operating outside Québec — Law 25 requires a documented **privacy impact assessment** that considers, among other factors, the sensitivity of the information, the purposes of the communication, and the legal regime applicable in the destination jurisdiction. Where the assessment identifies risks, an information-sharing agreement with appropriate safeguards is required.

    <Tip>
      Cross-border data transfer under Law 25 is one of the highest-frequency issues in multi-site or industry-sponsored trials. Flag it at the earliest possible stage of REB review — protocols routinely arrive with monitoring, safety reporting, or central-lab arrangements that involve transfer of Québec participant information outside Québec without a documented PIA in the submission package.
    </Tip>

    ## Federal Privacy Framework

    ### Privacy Act (RSC 1985, c P-21)

    The _Privacy Act_ applies to personal information collected, used, or disclosed by **federal government institutions** — including federal research granting agencies, federal health departments, and federal public bodies. It does not directly apply to hospital research unless the hospital is a federal institution (e.g., a federal correctional facility health service), but it is relevant when a protocol requires access to data held by a federal institution.

    **Research relevance:** If your protocol involves accessing data held by a federal institution (e.g., Statistics Canada data, CIHI-federal data-sharing arrangements, federal health registry data), compliance with the _Privacy Act_ and any associated data access agreements is required.

    ---

    ### PIPEDA (SC 2000, c 5)

    PIPEDA applies to private-sector organizations engaged in commercial activities in provinces that have not enacted substantially similar legislation covering the same domain. Québec's _Act Respecting the Protection of Personal Information in the Private Sector_ (as amended by Law 25) has been declared substantially similar to PIPEDA, so **PIPEDA is largely displaced for provincially regulated activity in Québec**. PIPEDA may still apply where a protocol involves:

    - Inter-provincial or international commercial flows of personal information (e.g., a sponsor headquartered outside Québec collecting information from Québec participants for use in another province),
    - Federally regulated organizations (banks, telecommunications, interprovincial transport), or
    - A pharmaceutical company, contract research organization (CRO), or insurer whose activities are not fully covered by Québec's private-sector regime.

    **Research exception:** PIPEDA Schedule 1, Principle 5 and s. 7(2) permit collection and use of personal information without consent for research purposes if: (a) the purpose cannot be achieved with anonymized information, (b) obtaining consent is impracticable, (c) the organization has notified the Office of the Privacy Commissioner of Canada, and (d) the information is used only for the stated purpose and destroyed or anonymized as soon as possible.

    ---

    ### Federal Reform of Private-Sector Privacy Law

    Federal private-sector privacy reform (the proposed _Consumer Privacy Protection Act_, previously introduced as **Bill C-27** in the 44th Parliament) has not been enacted. Bill C-27 died on the Order Paper when Parliament was prorogued in January 2025. If a comparable bill is reintroduced and enacted, PIPEDA may eventually be replaced or substantially amended. Until then, **PIPEDA remains the operative federal private-sector statute** where it applies. Monitor the status of any successor legislation through the Office of the Privacy Commissioner of Canada.

## Cross-Border and Multi-Jurisdictional Studies

<Tip>
  For any protocol involving communication of personal information outside Québec — to another Canadian province, to a U.S.-based sponsor, to an EU central lab, to a cloud provider not hosted in Québec, or to an international coordinating centre — build a **jurisdictional matrix at the outset**: list each destination, the type of information involved, the applicable Québec statute (Law 5 or Law 25), whether a PIA has been completed, and the safeguards in the information-sharing agreement. Flag any transfer without a documented PIA. A single non-compliant transfer can expose the institution to CAI action and jeopardize the entire trial.
</Tip>

## Applying Privacy Law in REB Review

When you review a protocol that involves personal information, work through these questions in every case:

<CardGroup cols={2}>
  <Card title="Is consent required?" icon="signature">
    Start with the default: consent is required. An exception applies only if the protocol satisfies the specific statutory criteria in the applicable law — Law 5 for hospital health information, Law 25 for other personal information. The burden is on the researcher to demonstrate the exception, not on the REB to prove consent is required.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Is de-identification or anonymization possible?" icon="user-slash">
    Most research-without-consent pathways require that the purpose cannot be achieved with anonymized information. Assess this claim critically — if anonymized information could answer the research question, the pathway may not apply. Remember Law 25's high anonymization standard: de-identified information that could be re-linked is still personal information.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Is the information-sharing agreement adequate?" icon="file-contract">
    For research using health and social services information under Law 5, or personal information under Law 25, a written agreement is required and must contain the conditions prescribed by the applicable statute. Review the agreement, not just the protocol.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Is the privacy management plan adequate?" icon="lock">
    The protocol's data management section must address collection minimization, anonymization or de-identification timelines, storage location (in Québec or outside), access controls, retention periods, destruction, and — for any transfer outside Québec — a documented privacy impact assessment. Inadequate safeguards are a basis for requiring conditions on approval.
  </Card>
</CardGroup>
