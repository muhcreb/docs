Beyond TCPS2, ICH-GCP, and privacy legislation, your hospital operates under a layer of institutional policies that directly shape the REB's work and your role as a legal representative. These policies — standard operating procedures, research agreement templates, conflict of interest rules, privacy governance frameworks, and records management requirements — define how the institution implements its regulatory and ethical obligations. As legal counsel on the REB, you must be familiar with these documents, know where to find them, and understand how they interact with external regulatory frameworks. When a researcher, sponsor, or institutional administrator asks you whether a proposed course of action is permissible, the answer usually requires consulting both the external standard and the institutional policy.

<Note>
  **Institutional policy and the TCPS2 hierarchy:** Institutional policies may be _more_ protective or more stringent than TCPS2 requires, but they may never be _less_ protective. If an institutional policy purports to authorize something that TCPS2 prohibits — or to exempt an activity from ethics review that TCPS2 requires to be reviewed — the institutional policy is without effect to the extent of the inconsistency. TCPS2 compliance is a condition of the institution's funding agreements with the Tri-Councils; the institution cannot contract out of it through internal policy. The same principle applies to applicable law: institutional policy cannot override statutory requirements, and where a statute sets a floor, institutional policy sets the ceiling by imposing higher standards if appropriate.
</Note>

## Key Policy Categories

<Accordion title="REB Standard Operating Procedures (SOPs)">
  **What they are:** The REB's SOPs document every aspect of the REB's constitution, operation, and decision-making process. They are typically maintained by the REB office and approved by the REB Chair and institutional research leadership. SOPs cover membership qualifications and terms, quorum and voting rules, the protocol review process (delegated review, full board review, expedited review), continuing review timelines, adverse event triage, handling of protocol deviations, and appeal procedures.

  **Why they matter for legal review:**

  - **GCP compliance:** ICH-GCP E6(R3) requires the IRB/IEC to establish, follow, and maintain written procedures (SOPs). During a Health Canada inspection, the REB's SOPs are a primary document. Gaps, outdated procedures, or departures from SOPs in practice can constitute GCP deficiencies.
  - **Procedural fairness:** If the REB refuses to approve a protocol, the researcher may challenge the decision. Consistent adherence to SOPs supports the defensibility of the REB's process. Departures from SOPs — even well-intentioned ones — can create procedural fairness claims.
  - **Liability limitation:** Clear SOPs that define the scope of the REB's review and the limits of its authority help manage the institution's legal exposure. Ensure SOPs do not inadvertently expand the REB's liability by creating obligations the REB cannot reasonably discharge.

  **How to access them:** The REB office maintains the current SOP set. Request the complete indexed set at the beginning of each academic year, and flag any SOP that has not been reviewed in the past three years.

  **What to do when SOPs conflict with TCPS2 or law:** Escalate immediately to the REB Chair and the institution's research compliance officer. Do not apply an SOP that requires conduct inconsistent with TCPS2 or applicable law. Document the conflict and the steps taken to resolve it.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Research Agreements and Contracts Policy">
  **What it is:** Most institutions have a policy — often administered through the research office or technology transfer office — governing the review and execution of research agreements, including sponsored research agreements (SRAs), clinical trial agreements (CTAs), data sharing agreements (DSAs), material transfer agreements (MTAs), and collaboration agreements.

  **Why it matters for legal review:**

  - **Sponsor terms that affect REB review:** Research agreements sometimes contain clauses that affect the REB's independence — for example, provisions requiring sponsor approval before safety information is reported to the REB, publication embargo periods exceeding acceptable limits, or sponsor rights to alter the protocol without REB review. Your institutional policy should specify what terms are acceptable and which are prohibited. If it does not, advocate for an amendment.
  - **Indemnification and insurance:** Research agreements typically allocate liability between the institution, the researcher, and the sponsor. Ensure your institution's standard clauses adequately protect the institution and — critically — ensure that participant compensation for research-related injury is addressed. TCPS2 Article 11.7 requires that trial protocols describe how research-related injury will be compensated.
  - **Authority to sign:** Institutional policy specifies who is authorized to execute research agreements on behalf of the institution. Researchers who sign agreements outside their delegated authority create personal liability exposure and may bind the institution in ways not intended. Confirm signature authority for all agreements reviewed in connection with REB submissions.
  - **Data ownership:** Agreements that vest data ownership exclusively in the sponsor can create post-trial complications — particularly where the REB may need access to trial data for ongoing monitoring or adverse event investigation. Ensure the agreement preserves the institution's and researcher's rights to access trial data for safety and regulatory purposes.

  **How to access it:** The research office or legal services department maintains the agreements policy and standard contract templates. Request a current copy and keep it on hand during REB meetings where sponsored protocols are on the agenda.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Conflict of Interest Policy">
  **What it is:** The institution's COI policy defines what constitutes a conflict of interest for researchers and, separately, for REB members. It typically requires disclosure of financial interests, personal relationships, and other interests that could affect objectivity, and specifies management measures (recusal, divestiture, monitoring).

  **Why it matters for legal review:**

  - **REB member COI:** You have a personal obligation to comply with the institutional COI policy as well as TCPS2 Chapter 5. Maintain a current COI disclosure form with the REB office and update it whenever your interests change. Where you are uncertain whether an interest constitutes a conflict, err on the side of disclosure and recusal.
  - **Researcher COI in protocol review:** The REB must assess whether researcher COI has been adequately disclosed and managed. Review COI disclosure forms submitted with protocols. Vague disclosures ("I have a consulting relationship with industry") are insufficient — require specifics (name of company, nature of relationship, dollar value) and assess whether the management plan is adequate.
  - **Institutional COI:** TCPS2 Article 7.3 requires REBs to take steps to manage institutional COI. If the hospital holds equity in a company sponsoring a trial, or stands to receive royalties from a product being tested, this creates an institutional COI requiring heightened scrutiny of the review process — potentially including independent external review.
  - **Intersection with securities law:** Researcher or institutional equity interests in publicly traded companies sponsoring trials may implicate insider trading rules if trial results could affect share price. This is a legal concern beyond the REB's mandate, but you should flag it to the general counsel's office when you identify it.

  **How to access it:** The research office, human resources, or general counsel's office maintains the COI policy. Confirm that the REB's COI disclosure process aligns with the institutional policy — not merely with TCPS2.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Privacy and Data Governance Policy">
  **What it is:** The institution's privacy and data governance policy governs how personal information — including personal health information — is collected, used, disclosed, stored, retained, and destroyed across the institution. It designates the Privacy Officer, specifies mandatory Privacy Impact Assessment (PIA) requirements, sets standards for data security, and defines breach notification procedures.

  **Why it matters for legal review:**

  - **REB review and privacy-law compliance:** As described in the Privacy Laws reference page, the REB's authorization for research using health and social services information without consent — under **Law 5** (arts. 44 ff.) and, where applicable, **Law 25** and the **Civil Code of Québec** — must be consistent with the MUHC's privacy and data governance policy and with the requirements of the **Commission d'accès à l'information du Québec (CAI)**. If the protocol's data management plan does not meet the institutional standard, require revisions before approving.
  - **PIA requirements:** Many institutional policies require a PIA before a new research activity involving PHI is approved. Confirm with the Privacy Officer whether a PIA is required for a given protocol and, if so, whether it has been completed. An REB approval issued before a required PIA is completed does not satisfy the institutional privacy requirement.
  - **Breach reporting:** The policy specifies what constitutes a reportable privacy breach, to whom it must be reported (Privacy Officer, IPC, participants), and within what timeframe. Ensure your REB's adverse event and protocol deviation reporting procedures integrate breach reporting obligations.
  - **Data residency and cloud storage:** Institutional policy may restrict where research data can be stored (e.g., prohibiting storage on non-Canadian servers without specific authorization). Protocols that propose cloud-based data storage should be checked against this policy, particularly for studies involving Quebec participants given Law 25's cross-border transfer requirements.

  **How to access it:** The Privacy Officer or health information management department maintains this policy. Request the current version at the start of each year and any time a protocol involves novel data collection or storage arrangements.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Records Management Policy">
  **What it is:** The institution's records management policy governs the creation, maintenance, retention, and destruction of institutional records — including REB records. It specifies retention periods for different record types, storage media requirements, access and confidentiality controls, and procedures for lawful destruction.

  **Why it matters for legal review:**

  - **REB record retention:** TCPS2 and GCP both require that REB records be maintained for defined periods. Your institutional records management policy must be at least as protective as the most stringent applicable external requirement. For clinical trial REB records, GCP's retention standard (often 15 years or longer for trials supporting marketing authorization) typically exceeds general institutional retention schedules — ensure the policy addresses this.
  - **Electronic records:** An increasing proportion of REB records are created and stored electronically. The institution's policy should specify requirements for electronic record integrity, audit trails, and long-term preservation. ICH-GCP E6(R3)'s data governance and essential-records requirements, together with Health Canada's electronic records guidance, apply to clinical trial electronic records; ensure your institution's policy is compliant.
  - **Litigation holds:** When litigation or regulatory proceedings are anticipated, the records management policy is superseded by a litigation hold — all relevant records must be preserved and destruction suspended. As legal counsel on the REB, you are likely the first to recognize when a litigation hold is appropriate. Advise the REB Chair and the records management officer promptly.
  - **Access to records:** REB records may be requested by participants, researchers, regulators, privacy commissioners, or litigants. The policy governs how access requests are handled. Understand the process before a request arrives — responding to a Health Canada inspection request or a court order under time pressure is not the moment to locate the policy.

  **How to access it:** The health information management or records management department maintains this policy. The legal services department can advise on litigation hold procedures and the interface between records management and legal proceedings.
</Accordion>

## Finding Institutional Policies

<Steps>
  <Step title="Start with the Research Office">
    The institution's research office — often led by a Vice-President Research or Director of Research Services — is the primary custodian of research-specific policies, including the REB SOPs, research agreements policy, and COI policy. Request a policy index at the beginning of your term.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Check the Intranet Policy Repository">
    Most hospitals maintain a policy repository on their intranet. Search for the relevant policy category (privacy, conflict of interest, records management) and confirm you are accessing the current version — look for the effective date and the scheduled review date.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Consult the Privacy Officer">
    The institution's Privacy Officer is the authoritative source for privacy and data governance policy. Establish a working relationship early — you will need the Privacy Officer's input regularly when reviewing protocols involving PHI.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Engage General Counsel">
    For policies with significant legal implications — agreements authority, COI with securities law dimensions, records in anticipated litigation — consult the general counsel or in-house legal team. Your role on the REB is as a legal representative of the institution; you should not be operating without institutional legal support.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Request Annual Policy Updates">
    Policies change. Ask the REB office to distribute a summary of policy updates at the beginning of each academic year and whenever a relevant policy is amended. Keep your personal policy reference set current.
  </Step>
</Steps>

## When Institutional Policy and Regulation Appear to Conflict

<CardGroup cols={2}>
  <Card title="Policy Is More Restrictive" icon="shield">
    This is the normal and permissible case. The institution may require additional safeguards beyond what TCPS2 or applicable law mandates. Apply the institutional policy — it represents the institution's risk tolerance and ethical commitment. Document in the REB record that the institutional policy requirement was applied.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Policy Is Less Protective" icon="triangle-exclamation">
    This is a problem. Apply the external standard (TCPS2, applicable law) regardless of what institutional policy says. Immediately escalate to the REB Chair, the Privacy Officer, and/or general counsel, and advocate for revision of the institutional policy to bring it into compliance.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Policy Is Silent" icon="question">
    When institutional policy does not address a situation, default to TCPS2, applicable law, and the ethical principles that underlie them. Document your analysis. Use the gap to recommend that the relevant policy be updated to address the situation explicitly.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Apparent Conflict Due to Ambiguity" icon="magnifying-glass">
    Before concluding there is a true conflict, assess whether the apparent conflict results from ambiguous drafting. Consult the policy owner and general counsel. An interpretation that reconciles the institutional policy with the external standard is always preferable to a conflict resolution that overrides one or the other.
  </Card>
</CardGroup>

<Warning>
  If you identify a genuine conflict between an institutional policy and a statutory requirement, do not simply resolve it quietly in the REB meeting. Raise it formally, document it in the REB minutes, and escalate it to the appropriate institutional authority (Privacy Officer, General Counsel, VP Research). Unresolved conflicts between institutional policy and law create legal risk for the institution and, in some circumstances, personal liability for REB members who follow the non-compliant policy. Your role as legal counsel on the REB includes proactively identifying and escalating these situations.
</Warning>
