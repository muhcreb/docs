Serving as a legal representative on a Research Ethics Board is not a static role. Regulations evolve, new research modalities emerge — from AI-driven diagnostic tools to gene-editing therapies — and judicial interpretations of research-related statutes shift over time. Investing in regular professional development keeps your contributions to the REB substantive, timely, and grounded in the most current standards.

## Why Continuing Education Matters

Research ethics in Canada operates at the intersection of federal guidance, provincial legislation, institutional policy, and international standards. A single year can bring amendments to Health Canada's _Food and Drug Regulations_, updated ICH guidelines, or new TCPS2 interpretation documents. As the legal voice on the REB, your role is to translate these developments into practical guidance for the full board — a responsibility that requires you to stay ahead of the field.

<Info>
  Emerging research areas such as machine learning studies, whole-genome sequencing, decentralized clinical trials, and first-in-human gene therapy protocols each carry novel legal questions. Targeted training in these areas allows you to contribute meaningfully when the REB encounters them for the first time.
</Info>

## Required and Core Training

<Note>
  **TCPS2 CORE is typically required** for all REB members, including legal representatives, before participating in reviews. Confirm your institution's specific requirement with your REB coordinator, as many hospitals make completion of the CORE tutorial a condition of appointment.
</Note>

<Accordion title="TCPS2 CORE — Panel on Research Ethics">
  The CORE (Course on Research Ethics) tutorial is the Government of Canada's official interactive training module based on the _Tri-Council Policy Statement: Ethical Conduct for Research Involving Humans_ (TCPS2). It covers core ethical principles, consent, privacy, conflicts of interest, and special populations. Completion produces a certificate that many institutions require you to retain on file. The tutorial takes approximately four hours and is available at no cost through the Panel on Research Ethics website. You should retake CORE — or the updated module — whenever a new edition of the TCPS2 is published.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="ICH Good Clinical Practice (GCP) Training">
  If your REB reviews industry-sponsored or Health Canada–regulated clinical trials, familiarity with ICH E6(R3) — adopted by Health Canada in 2025 — is essential. GCP training is commonly offered by TransCelerate BioPharma, professional associations such as SOCRA (Society of Clinical Research Associates) and ACRP (Association of Clinical Research Professionals), and online platforms. As a legal representative, your priority within GCP is understanding investigator responsibilities, sponsor–investigator agreements, indemnification structures, and the regulatory inspection framework — areas where your legal expertise directly augments the scientific members' review.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Privacy Law and Data Governance Training">
  For the MUHC REB, data-intensive and multi-site studies primarily implicate Québec's **Law 5** (_Act Respecting Health and Social Services Information_) and **Law 25** (_Act Respecting the Protection of Personal Information in the Private Sector_, as amended), together with the **Civil Code of Québec** (arts. 20–25) for the consent framework. For federally regulated or trans-border data flows, _PIPEDA_ may apply, and GDPR arises for European collaborations. The **Commission d'accès à l'information du Québec (CAI)** and the **Office of the Privacy Commissioner of Canada** publish guidance documents and recorded webinars relevant to health research. The Centre for Digital Rights and the Future of Privacy Forum also offer practitioner-focused resources on emerging data governance issues in research.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Specialized Research Modality Training">
  Certain research types require targeted legal knowledge:

  - **Gene therapy and genomics**: The Canadian Institutes of Health Research (CIHR) and Global Alliance for Genomics and Health (GA4GH) publish frameworks and policy briefs on the legal implications of genetic research, return of results, and incidental findings.
  - **AI/ML research studies**: Health Canada's guidance on Software as a Medical Device (SaMD) and the Office of the Privacy Commissioner's guidance on automated decision-making are important starting points.
  - **International research**: IRPA and export-control considerations, along with WHO's _Standards and Operational Guidance for Ethics Review of Health-Related Research with Human Participants_, are useful references.
  - **Indigenous research**: OCAP® (Ownership, Control, Access, and Possession) principles training, offered through the First Nations Information Governance Centre, is particularly important for REBs that review community-based participatory research.
</Accordion>

## Major Training Resources

<CardGroup cols={2}>
  <Card title="Panel on Research Ethics (PRE)" icon="graduation-cap">
    The federal body responsible for interpreting and promoting the TCPS2. The PRE publishes CORE tutorials, guidance documents, and annotated updates to the policy statement. Subscribing to PRE communications is one of the most efficient ways to receive official TCPS2 clarifications as they are released.
  </Card>

  <Card title="CAREB–ACCER Annual Conference" icon="calendar">
    The Canadian Association of Research Ethics Boards holds an annual national conference that brings together REB administrators, chairs, members, and researchers. Sessions frequently address emerging legal and regulatory issues, and attendance counts as professional development hours for many legal professional bodies.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Health Canada Guidance Documents" icon="book-open">
    Health Canada regularly publishes guidance on clinical trial regulations, drug submissions, and medical device studies. Subscribing to the Health Canada MedEffect notice service and monitoring the Canada Gazette for regulatory amendments ensures you receive material changes as soon as they are posted.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Canadian Bar Association — Health Law Section" icon="scale-balanced">
    The CBA's Health Law Section hosts continuing legal education (CLE) programs, publishes the _Health Law Journal_, and maintains a network of practitioners working at the intersection of law and health research. Attending section events can satisfy Law Society CPD requirements while deepening your REB expertise.
  </Card>

  <Card title="CIHR — Research Ethics Resources" icon="flask">
    The Canadian Institutes of Health Research produces research ethics policy, funds knowledge translation in ethics, and publishes position statements relevant to funded research. Their resources are especially useful when reviewing CIHR-funded studies or interpreting grant-linked ethics conditions.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Law Society CPD Programs" icon="landmark">
    Your provincial Law Society likely offers CPD sessions touching on health law, privacy, consent, and administrative decision-making — all areas directly relevant to your REB role. Review the upcoming CPD calendar each year and flag sessions with REB application to your practice manager.
  </Card>
</CardGroup>

## Staying Current with Health Canada Guidance

<Steps>
  <Step title="Subscribe to regulatory update services">
    Register for Health Canada's MedEffect e-Notice, the Canada Gazette RSS feed, and the International Council for Harmonisation (ICH) email alerts. These services deliver amendments and new guidance directly to your inbox without requiring manual monitoring.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Review the Health Canada website quarterly">
    At the start of each quarter, check the "Clinical Trials" and "Medical Devices" sections of the Health Canada website for newly posted guidance documents, information letters, and policy notices. Keep a running log of documents reviewed so you can demonstrate currency if questioned during an inspection or appeal.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Monitor case law and regulatory decisions">
    Set up a CanLII alert for decisions involving research ethics, clinical trials, and informed consent. Decisions from the **Commission d'accès à l'information du Québec (CAI)** and the **Tribunal administratif du Québec** on health information and privacy are equally important and sometimes directly affect protocols the MUHC REB reviews.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Engage in peer discussion">
    Participate in the CAREB member listserv or other REB professional networks where regulatory developments are discussed in practical context. Peer exchange often surfaces nuances that are not immediately apparent from reading a guidance document alone.
  </Step>
</Steps>

## Raising a Learning Need with the REB

If you encounter a protocol type or regulatory question you feel underprepared to assess — whether a first-in-human gene therapy trial, a study using blockchain-based data storage, or a research project involving temporary incapacity consent — raise it proactively.

<Tabs>
  <Tab title="Speaking with the REB Chair">
    Contact the REB chair before the meeting at which the protocol is scheduled. Describe the specific area of uncertainty, indicate any training or reading you have already done, and ask whether the REB can seek an external legal or regulatory opinion, defer the review, or bring in a consultant member. Chairs are generally receptive to members who flag knowledge gaps early — it avoids uninformed deliberation and protects the board's integrity.
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Working with the REB Coordinator">
    The REB coordinator often maintains a resource library and has access to institutional subscriptions for legal and regulatory databases. Ask the coordinator to send you relevant guidance documents ahead of the review and to identify whether the institution has previously reviewed similar protocols. The coordinator may also be able to connect you with colleagues at other REBs who have encountered the same issue.
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Requesting Formal Training">
    If a gap in your knowledge is systemic rather than protocol-specific — for example, you have never received formal PIPEDA training — put a formal request to the REB chair in writing. Document the request and any response. Institutions are generally expected to support ongoing education for board members, and a written request creates a record that you acted in good faith to address the limitation.
  </Tab>
</Tabs>

## Suggested Reading

<Accordion title="Core Texts and Policy Documents">
  - _Tri-Council Policy Statement: Ethical Conduct for Research Involving Humans_, 3rd edition (TCPS2 2022) — the foundational Canadian document for all REB members.
  - Health Canada, _Guidance Document: Conduct of Clinical Trials in Canada_ — essential for legal reps reviewing Phase I–IV pharmaceutical trials.
  - ICH E6(R3) _Good Clinical Practice Guideline_ (adopted by Health Canada in 2025; replaces E6(R2)) — governs sponsor and investigator obligations in clinical trials.
  - Office of the Privacy Commissioner, _A Guide for Businesses and Organizations: Privacy and Your Employees_ and sector-specific health privacy guidance documents.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Academic and Professional Journals">
  - _Journal of Medical Ethics_ — peer-reviewed articles on ethical and legal challenges in clinical and research ethics.
  - _Health Law Review_ (now _Health Law in Canada_) — leading Canadian health law journal covering legislation, case law, and policy.
  - _IRB: Ethics & Human Research_ — practitioner-focused journal covering REB process, consent, and regulatory issues.
  - _Canadian Bar Review_ — for articles on administrative law principles relevant to REB decision-making and appeals.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Online Resources and Communities">
  - CAREB–ACCER member resources portal — templates, guidance, and webinar recordings from the national professional association.
  - Panel on Research Ethics blog and FAQ portal — informal interpretations and practice notes from PRE staff.
  - CanLII — free Canadian case law database; create alerts for "research ethics," "informed consent," and "clinical trial" to track relevant decisions.
  - Global Ethics Observatory (GEObs) — UNESCO-managed database of ethics committee profiles and resources across jurisdictions.
</Accordion>

<Tip>
  Track your REB-related professional development in a dedicated CPD log. Many provincial Law Societies accept REB service and ethics-related training as qualifying CPD hours — but only if you document them contemporaneously with the activity dates and learning outcomes.
</Tip>
