The legal representative role on a Research Ethics Board is distinct from any other legal function in the hospital environment. It is neither equivalent to acting as institutional counsel nor to providing legal advice to researchers. The questions below address the most frequent sources of confusion, uncertainty, and concern raised by lawyers and legal counsel who serve on REBs — drawing on the _Tri-Council Policy Statement_ (TCPS2), Health Canada regulations, provincial legislation, and general principles of administrative and institutional governance.

<Note>
  Nothing in this FAQ constitutes legal advice. Apply these general principles in light of your institution's specific policies, applicable provincial law, and the facts of each situation. When in doubt, consult your institution's general counsel or risk management office.
</Note>

## Voting and Authority

<Accordion title="Is my vote as the legal representative binding, and does the REB have to follow my legal opinion?">
  Your vote carries equal weight to that of any other voting member of the REB — no more and no less. The REB is a collective deliberative body, and decisions are made by the board as a whole, typically by consensus or majority vote, rather than by deference to any single member's expertise. When you provide a legal analysis — for example, identifying a deficiency in a consent form under **Law 5** or the **Civil Code of Québec**, or flagging an indemnification clause that does not adequately protect participants — you are contributing specialist input that the board must consider, but the board is not bound to adopt your conclusion. That said, in practice, REB chairs and coordinators give significant weight to a well-reasoned legal opinion, and other members are rarely in a position to contradict a clear legal analysis with authority. Your most effective tool is a clear, accessible explanation of the legal issue and its practical implications for participants and the institution — not assertion of authority you do not hold.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="What if I disagree with the REB chair's decision?">
  Your first step is to raise your disagreement clearly on the record during the meeting so that your position is documented in the minutes. If the chair's ruling relates to procedure — for example, whether a quorum has been met or whether a conflict of interest should require recusal — you can request that the basis for the ruling be documented. For substantive disagreements about ethics review outcomes, most institutions have an internal appeal or reconsideration process; you can encourage an affected researcher or participant to use it, though you are not typically in a position to appeal on their behalf. If you believe the board's decision creates a material legal risk for the institution or for participants, you should communicate that concern in writing to the REB chair and consider whether the matter should be escalated to the institution's research compliance officer, general counsel, or senior administration. Document your concern and the response you received.
</Accordion>

## Confidentiality and Legal Practice

<Accordion title="Can I use information from REB deliberations in my legal practice?">
  No. You must treat all protocol submissions, deliberations, member opinions, and board decisions as strictly confidential in your capacity as an REB member. Information shared in the REB context is shared for the sole purpose of ethics review — it is not available to you for use in advising other clients, in litigation, or in any context outside the REB. Most institutions formalize this obligation in a confidentiality agreement that members sign at appointment. Beyond the institutional obligation, using confidential REB information in your practice could expose you to disciplinary proceedings by your provincial Law Society for breach of confidentiality duties owed in a fiduciary or quasi-fiduciary capacity. If you become aware of a conflict between information you have obtained in your REB role and a matter arising in your legal practice, disclose the potential conflict to the REB chair and to your firm's conflicts manager immediately.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="How are minutes of REB meetings treated — are they privileged?">
  REB meeting minutes are generally not protected by solicitor-client privilege because they record the deliberations of an institutional committee, not confidential communications between a lawyer and a client. However, they may be subject to other protections depending on the context. REB deliberations may attract a qualified privilege in defamation law given that members exercise a quasi-adjudicative function in good faith. Minutes may also be withheld from disclosure under the Québec _Act Respecting Access to Documents Held by Public Bodies and the Protection of Personal Information_ (Loi sur l'accès) as deliberative records of an advisory body of the MUHC. If the MUHC is served with a legal demand for REB records — whether by litigation subpoena, an access request under the Loi sur l'accès, a **Commission d'accès à l'information (CAI)** proceeding, or a Health Canada inspection — the appropriate response is to route the request through the MUHC's general counsel rather than responding independently. Do not interpret a confidentiality obligation as authorization to withhold records from a lawful demand for production; that is a question for institutional counsel.
</Accordion>

## Personal Liability

<Accordion title="Am I personally liable if an approved study harms a research participant?">
  Personal liability for individual REB members is rare in Canadian practice, but the theoretical risk is not zero. Members who act in good faith, within the scope of their role, in accordance with the TCPS2 and applicable legislation, and without wilful misconduct or gross negligence are generally insulated from personal liability by institutional indemnification provisions and the principles of administrative law. However, if a member acts outside their role — for example, by providing individual legal advice to a researcher, approving a protocol known to violate the law, or suppressing a material concern to avoid institutional embarrassment — the protection may not apply. Before accepting your appointment, confirm in writing that the hospital's directors' and officers' liability insurance, or an equivalent institutional indemnity, covers REB members. If your institution does not provide such coverage, assess whether your own professional liability insurance responds to REB service. Document your reasoning in legally complex reviews so that a contemporaneous record of good-faith analysis exists.
</Accordion>

## Interactions with Researchers

<Accordion title="How do I handle a request from a researcher to speak to me directly about their protocol?">
  Researchers sometimes contact REB members directly — particularly if they have identified a member's professional background and hope for informal guidance. You should decline to engage in substantive ex parte discussions about a protocol that is before or will come before the REB. Explain politely that all communications about a submitted protocol should be directed to the REB coordinator and that your role as a board member requires that deliberations take place in the context of the full board meeting. Direct ex parte communication creates a risk of bias, inconsistent information, and breach of the board's procedural fairness obligations. If you inadvertently receive substantive information from a researcher outside the formal process, disclose it to the REB chair and coordinator at the outset of the relevant meeting so it can be addressed transparently. This approach protects both you and the integrity of the review.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="What is the difference between the legal representative's role and the hospital's legal department?">
  Your role as the legal representative on the REB is to bring legal knowledge to the board's collective ethics review — not to act as legal counsel to the institution, the researcher, or the sponsor. You assess whether a protocol raises legal issues relevant to participant protection, institutional liability, and regulatory compliance, and you contribute that analysis to the board's deliberations. The hospital's legal department, by contrast, acts as institutional counsel: it provides advice to hospital management on contractual, regulatory, and liability matters, reviews and negotiates clinical trial agreements, responds to litigation, and advises on compliance obligations. The two roles are complementary but distinct. When a protocol raises a question that requires a formal institutional legal opinion — for example, a complex indemnification structure, a multi-party data access agreement, or a question about Health Canada's enforcement powers — the appropriate step is to flag it to the REB chair and recommend referral to general counsel rather than attempting to provide an institutional legal opinion in your capacity as an REB member.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="When should a protocol be referred to the hospital's general counsel?">
  Refer a protocol (or a specific legal issue within it) to general counsel whenever the issue requires a formal institutional legal opinion rather than advisory input to the board's ethics deliberation. Common triggers include: a clinical trial agreement that has not been reviewed and approved by the institution's legal and risk management teams before coming to the REB; an indemnification, liability, or insurance provision that creates material institutional risk; a question about the hospital's obligations under a specific statute that requires formal legal advice (e.g., mandatory reporting obligations, privacy breach notification, intellectual property ownership disputes); a concern about potential criminal conduct or regulatory offences; and any situation involving threatened or pending litigation related to a study under review. When making a referral recommendation, document it in the minutes and confirm that the protocol review will be held pending receipt of general counsel's input. This protects the board and ensures that the institutional legal advice is on record.
</Accordion>

## Regulatory and Compliance Questions

<Accordion title="Can an REB approve a study that is potentially illegal?">
  No. The REB's mandate is to determine that research meets ethical and regulatory standards before it proceeds — and approval of an activity that is unlawful is inconsistent with that mandate. If a protocol, as proposed, appears to violate applicable federal or provincial law — whether by collecting personal information without lawful authority, conducting a clinical trial outside Health Canada's regulatory framework, or proposing research activities that exceed what provincial consent legislation permits — the REB should require revisions to bring the protocol into compliance before approval is granted. In rare cases, there may be a genuine legal uncertainty about whether a proposed activity is lawful; in those situations, the appropriate response is to require a legal opinion to resolve the question before proceeding, not to assume the activity is permissible. If an investigator argues that a legal requirement should be waived or that the REB should approve the study despite a legal concern "at the investigator's risk," the REB should decline that approach — ethics approval does not immunize unlawful research, and the board should not be used to legitimize it.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="What happens if a researcher proceeds without REB approval?">
  Conducting research involving human participants without REB approval is a serious violation of the TCPS2 and, in the case of regulated clinical trials, of Health Canada's _Food and Drug Regulations_ or _Medical Devices Regulations_. The consequences for the researcher can include: loss of institutional research privileges; withdrawal of CIHR, NSERC, or SSHRC funding (the three agencies require ongoing compliance with the TCPS2 as a condition of funding); Health Canada regulatory action including refusal to accept data generated in non-compliant trials; and, in serious cases, action by a provincial College or other professional regulatory body. For the institution, proceeding without REB approval can affect accreditation, institutional funding agreements, and reputation. If you become aware that a researcher is conducting or has conducted research without REB approval, you have an obligation to report it to the REB chair and the institution's research compliance officer promptly. The REB may need to review the study retroactively and assess whether any data generated during the unapproved period can be used or whether participants need to be notified.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="How do I stay up to date on regulatory changes?">
  Staying current requires a combination of passive monitoring and active engagement. Subscribe to Health Canada's MedEffect e-Notice service and the Canada Gazette RSS feed to receive regulatory amendments as they are published. Monitor the Panel on Research Ethics website for updates to TCPS2 guidance and interpretation documents, as the Panel periodically issues clarifications that affect REB practice without amending the full policy statement. Set up CanLII alerts for relevant search terms — "clinical trial," "informed consent," "research ethics," and applicable provincial privacy legislation. Attend CAREB–ACCER's annual conference and webinar series, where regulatory updates are discussed in a practical REB context. Review your provincial Law Society's CPD calendar each year for health law and privacy sessions. Finally, build relationships with colleagues at other institutional REBs — peer discussion often surfaces emerging issues before they appear in formal guidance.
</Accordion>

## Obligations Outside Meetings

<Accordion title="What is my obligation if I learn of a serious protocol violation outside a meeting?">
  If you learn of a serious protocol violation — whether from a researcher's disclosure, a participant complaint, a news report, or any other source — you should report it to the REB chair and the REB coordinator without delay, rather than waiting for the next scheduled meeting. Under the TCPS2, REBs have ongoing oversight responsibility for approved research, and serious violations — including unanticipated harms to participants, significant deviations from an approved protocol, or breaches of participant confidentiality — may require the REB to take immediate action, up to and including suspension of the study. You do not need to investigate the violation yourself; your obligation is to ensure that the information reaches the REB chair so that the institution's reporting, oversight, and, if necessary, remediation processes are activated. If the violation involves potential harm to a current participant, the urgency is heightened and the REB chair may need to convene an emergency review or interim oversight meeting. Document when and how you learned of the violation and what steps you took.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="What if the REB chair is the source of a concern I need to report?">
  If your concern relates to conduct by the REB chair — whether a conflict of interest, a procedural irregularity, or a substantive decision you believe is improper — you should not route the concern through the chair. Instead, direct the matter to the institution's senior research officer, vice-president of research, or research integrity officer, depending on your institution's governance structure. Review your institution's research compliance policy and whistleblower protection policy before doing so; most institutions have formal channels for reporting concerns about senior research personnel that provide a degree of protection to the reporter. If the concern potentially involves a legal obligation — such as a mandatory Health Canada notification that the chair has declined to make — consult with the hospital's general counsel. Document the concern and your steps in writing from the outset.
</Accordion>

## Additional Questions

<Accordion title="Can an REB member be asked to recuse themselves, and when is recusal appropriate?">
  Yes. Recusal is required whenever a member has a real or reasonably perceived conflict of interest with respect to a specific protocol. Common triggers include: a financial interest in the study sponsor or the research outcome; a professional or personal relationship with the principal investigator that could compromise independent judgment; being a co-investigator, co-author, or named personnel on the study; and serving in a supervisory or reporting relationship with the investigator. You are responsible for self-identifying conflicts at the outset of each review — do not wait to be asked. When recused, you must leave the meeting room (or virtual meeting) during the review and discussion of the protocol in question and must not participate in the vote. Your absence should be noted in the minutes. If you are uncertain whether a relationship constitutes a conflict, err on the side of recusal and discuss the question with the REB chair before the meeting rather than making the determination yourself during deliberations.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="How should I handle a situation where a sponsor's representative is present at an REB meeting?">
  Sponsor representatives — whether from pharmaceutical companies, medical device manufacturers, or research foundations — are not typically permitted to attend REB deliberations. The TCPS2 requires that REBs operate independently of sponsor influence, and the presence of a sponsor representative during deliberation could compromise that independence. If a sponsor has been invited to address a factual question (which should be rare and carefully managed), they should present only their specific information and then leave before any deliberation or vote. If you become aware that a sponsor representative has been invited to attend a meeting in which their study will be discussed, raise the independence concern with the REB chair before the meeting. Accepting hospitality, gifts, or other benefits from sponsors is inconsistent with your role as an independent REB member and may violate your institution's conflict of interest policy.
</Accordion>
