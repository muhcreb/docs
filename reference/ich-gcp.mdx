The International Council for Harmonisation of Technical Requirements for Pharmaceuticals for Human Use (ICH) Guideline E6(R3) — Good Clinical Practice — is the internationally recognized standard for designing, conducting, recording, and reporting clinical trials that involve human participants. As a legal representative on a hospital REB, you will encounter GCP requirements whenever a protocol involves an investigational drug, biologic, device, or natural health product regulated under Canada's _Food and Drugs Act_. Understanding GCP is not optional: it is the framework within which sponsors, investigators, and your REB operate in parallel.

## GCP in the Canadian Regulatory Context

In Canada, Health Canada incorporates GCP expectations into its clinical trial regulations. The _Food and Drug Regulations_ Part C, Division 5 (for drugs) and the _Medical Devices Regulations_ (for devices) require that clinical trials be conducted in compliance with GCP as a condition of a Clinical Trial Application (CTA) or Investigational Testing Authorization (ITA). Health Canada may inspect trial sites and sponsor facilities for GCP compliance, and deficiencies can result in trial suspension, import alerts, or regulatory action.

<Note>
  **From R2 to R3:** E6(R2) was structured around eight numbered sections (Glossary, Principles, IRB/IEC, Investigator, Sponsor, Protocol, Investigator's Brochure, Essential Documents). E6(R3) replaces that structure with overarching **Principles** and **Annex 1 (Interventional Clinical Trials)**, with **Annex 2** covering non-traditional trial designs still being finalized. Where you or a sponsor still see R2 section numbers cited in older documents (e.g., "Section 3.1.1," "Section 4.8," "Section 5.18"), map them to the corresponding R3 topic before relying on them for review decisions.
</Note>

## GCP Principles Most Relevant to the Legal Representative

E6(R3) is built on a set of overarching principles that carry forward the ethical foundation of earlier GCP guidance while emphasizing quality by design, proportionality, and risk-based decision-making across the trial lifecycle. The following R3 topic areas are most directly implicated in legal review.

<Accordion title="IRB / IEC (REB) Responsibilities">
  **Scope:** E6(R3) sets out the responsibilities of the Independent Review Board (IRB) or Independent Ethics Committee (IEC) — the equivalents of Canada's REB — covering constitution, operations, written procedures, records, and the scope and cadence of review.

  **Key legal considerations:**

  - **REB constitution:** R3 requires that the IRB/IEC be composed of members with the qualifications and experience collectively necessary to review the science, ethics, and other aspects of proposed trials, including non-scientific members and members independent of the institution. Verify your REB's membership list complies with both R3 expectations and TCPS2 Chapter 6.
  - **Written SOPs:** R3 requires the IRB/IEC to establish, follow, and maintain written procedures. Review your institution's REB SOPs annually to ensure they reflect current GCP and TCPS2 requirements. Gaps in SOPs can be cited by Health Canada inspectors as GCP deficiencies.
  - **Scope of review:** The IRB/IEC must review and approve the protocol and any amendments, the informed consent document(s), subject recruitment materials, other written information provided to participants, the investigator's brochure or equivalent, and information about payments and compensation to participants. Confirm that your REB review process addresses each of these document types.
  - **Continuing review:** R3 requires periodic review of ongoing trials at intervals appropriate to the degree of risk — at minimum annually. Ensure your REB's continuing review processes capture safety updates, DSMB reports, and protocol amendments from the sponsor.
  - **GCP non-compliance:** If the REB discovers non-compliance by the investigator or sponsor, it must notify the investigator and institution and, where appropriate, the regulatory authority. Map this to your institution's process for reporting serious protocol deviations to Health Canada.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Informed Consent of Trial Participants">
  **Scope:** E6(R3) sets out detailed expectations for the informed consent process — required elements of information, conditions for obtaining consent from participants who cannot consent for themselves, requirements for witnesses, and provisions for emergency and other special situations.

  **Key legal considerations:**

  - **Required elements:** R3 enumerates the elements of information that must be provided to participants before consent (covering the same substantive ground as R2's fifteen elements, reorganized). Compare these with TCPS2 Article 3.2 and your provincial consent legislation. Any element missing from a consent form is a reviewable deficiency.
  - **Language and readability:** Consent documents must be written in non-technical language that the participant can understand. Your review should assess reading level as well as legal sufficiency. Courts and regulators have found consent forms invalid where they were written at a level beyond the comprehension of the target population.
  - **Impartial witness:** For participants who cannot read, R3 requires an impartial witness present throughout the consent process. Confirm that the protocol's consent procedures address this scenario.
  - **Legally acceptable representative (LAR):** Where a participant is incapable of consenting, consent must be obtained from a LAR in accordance with applicable law. GCP defers to local law on who qualifies as a LAR — cross-reference with provincial legislation (e.g., Québec _Civil Code_ arts. 20–25).
  - **Emergency exception:** R3 preserves a narrow exception permitting enrollment without prior consent in life-threatening situations where the subject is incapable of consenting, no LAR is available, and the emergency consent procedure has been approved by the IRB/IEC in advance. This exception requires explicit REB prospective approval of the emergency consent procedure.
  - **Re-consent:** When new information may affect a participant's willingness to continue, R3 requires that consent be re-obtained. Define what constitutes a "material change" in the protocol and train investigators accordingly.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Investigator Obligations">
  **Scope:** E6(R3) addresses the obligations of the trial investigator — qualifications and resources, medical care of participants, record-keeping, safety reporting, and trial discontinuation. The investigator remains personally responsible for the conduct of the trial at the site.

  **Key legal considerations:**

  - **Investigator qualifications:** R3 requires investigators to be qualified by education, training, and experience to assume responsibility for conducting the trial. The REB should review the investigator's CV and credentials as part of protocol review, not merely rely on the sponsor's assessment.
  - **Medical care of participants:** A qualified physician must be responsible for all trial-related medical decisions. Where the principal investigator is not a physician, confirm that the protocol identifies a responsible physician.
  - **Delegation log:** Investigators must maintain a delegation log identifying all persons to whom trial tasks have been delegated. This is a GCP inspection item and also a key document in establishing institutional liability if an adverse event occurs.
  - **Safety reporting to REB:** R3 requires the investigator to report promptly to the REB all serious adverse events (SAEs) and, where required by applicable regulations, to Health Canada. Confirm that your REB's SAE reporting procedures specify timelines and triage criteria that meet both GCP and TCPS2 requirements.
  - **Premature termination:** If an investigator terminates or suspends a trial, they must promptly notify the REB and the sponsor, provide written reasons, and ensure appropriate participant follow-up. The REB should require notification within a defined timeframe specified in its SOPs.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Sponsor Obligations">
  **Scope:** E6(R3) addresses sponsor obligations across the full trial lifecycle — quality by design, clinical trial oversight, monitoring, auditing, and regulatory submissions. The sponsor is ultimately accountable for the overall conduct of the trial.

  **Key legal considerations:**

  - **Quality by design and risk-based quality management:** R3 elevates quality-by-design and proportionate, risk-based quality management from an addendum concept (as in R2) to a central organizing principle. When reviewing a sponsored trial, ask whether the sponsor has conducted a documented risk assessment, identified critical-to-quality factors, and reflected them in the monitoring plan.
  - **Monitoring:** The sponsor must appoint monitors and conduct monitoring proportionate to the trial's risk, using a documented monitoring strategy. Review the monitoring plan submitted with the protocol — inadequate monitoring is a red flag for both GCP compliance and participant safety.
  - **Clinical trial agreements:** R3 addresses written agreements between sponsors and investigators. Your legal review of the clinical trial agreement (CTA/contract) should confirm that it does not contain provisions that compromise investigator independence, restrict safety reporting, or give the sponsor unilateral rights to suppress unfavourable results.
  - **Audit rights:** R3 authorizes sponsors to conduct audits of investigator sites. The REB is not subject to sponsor audit, but investigators are. Ensure your institution's research agreement addresses what the sponsor may and may not access during an audit.
  - **Regulatory submissions:** Sponsors must submit timely safety reports to regulatory authorities. Where a sponsor's reporting to Health Canada appears inadequate, the REB has authority — and arguably an obligation — to seek assurance of compliance before approving continuation.
</Accordion>

## GCP Compliance and Legal Review of Clinical Trial Protocols

When you review a clinical trial protocol, apply a GCP lens in parallel with TCPS2 and applicable law.

<Steps>
  <Step title="Verify Regulatory Authorization">
    Confirm that a CTA or ITA has been filed with Health Canada (or is not required for this trial type). Approval of an unauthorized trial exposes the institution, investigator, and sponsor to regulatory liability under the _Food and Drugs Act_.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Review the Informed Consent Form Against R3 Consent Requirements">
    Cross-check the full set of GCP consent elements against the submitted form. Note any gaps and require the investigator to address them before the REB issues approval.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Assess Investigator Qualifications and Resources">
    Request and review the investigator's current CV and the site's resource assessment. An inadequately resourced site is both a GCP deficiency and a participant safety risk.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Review the Clinical Trial Agreement">
    Confirm the agreement does not restrict publication rights beyond a 60-day sponsor review period, does not grant the sponsor rights to suppress negative findings, and addresses indemnification of participants and the institution.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Check the Safety Monitoring Plan">
    Confirm the protocol includes a safety monitoring plan, DSMB terms of reference (if applicable), and stopping rules. Absence of a monitoring plan in a higher-risk trial is a mandatory condition to address before approval.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Document GCP Considerations in REB Minutes">
    Your written REB decision should reference the specific E6(R3) topics engaged by any condition or concern. This creates a defensible record in the event of a regulatory inspection or legal proceeding.
  </Step>
</Steps>

## How GCP and TCPS2 Interact

<Tabs>
  <Tab title="Where They Align">
    GCP and TCPS2 share a common ethical foundation in the Declaration of Helsinki. Both require:

    - Free and informed consent as a precondition for enrollment
    - Independent ethics review before research begins
    - Ongoing monitoring proportionate to risk
    - Prompt reporting of unanticipated harm to participants
    - Respect for participant confidentiality

    For most clinical trials, satisfying both frameworks simultaneously is straightforward because they address the same concerns from different but complementary angles.
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Where They Diverge">
    Differences between GCP and TCPS2 are most apparent in:

    - **Emergency consent:** E6(R3) and TCPS2 Article 3.8 both address emergency research but use slightly different criteria. Apply the more stringent standard.
    - **Placebo controls:** TCPS2 Article 11.4 and the Declaration of Helsinki (referenced by GCP) take a stricter position on placebo use than the FDA-aligned interpretation of GCP sometimes allows. In Canada, favour the more protective TCPS2 standard.
    - **Record retention:** E6(R3) sets essential-document retention expectations by trial type (generally at least 2 years after last regulatory submission or the trial's formal discontinuation, and substantially longer — commonly 15 years or more — for trials supporting a marketing authorization). TCPS2 does not specify retention periods — defer to GCP and your institution's records management policy for trial records.
    - **Regulatory reporting:** GCP creates sponsor/investigator duties to report to Health Canada that go beyond TCPS2's REB reporting requirements. Ensure your REB's SAE procedures capture both sets of obligations.
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Hierarchy of Standards">
    When GCP, TCPS2, provincial law, and institutional policy appear to conflict, apply the following hierarchy:

    1. **Applicable legislation** (federal and provincial statutes and regulations) — always paramount
    2. **The more protective ethical standard** — where TCPS2 and GCP differ on participant protection, apply the higher standard
    3. **TCPS2** — governing the REB's process and decisions
    4. **GCP** — governing the conduct of the trial and sponsor/investigator obligations
    5. **Institutional policy** — may be more restrictive than any of the above but cannot be less protective

    Document your analysis whenever you identify a divergence between standards.
  </Tab>
</Tabs>

<Warning>
  GCP compliance is a regulatory requirement, not merely a best practice. Health Canada may inspect your institution's clinical trial operations — including REB records — for GCP compliance. Maintain complete, accurate, and contemporaneous records of all REB decisions, deliberations, and correspondence related to clinical trials. Inspection-ready records are your institution's best defence in the event of a regulatory audit.
</Warning>
