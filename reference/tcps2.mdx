The Tri-Council Policy Statement: Ethical Conduct for Research Involving Humans, Second Edition (TCPS2) is the foundational ethics framework for human-subjects research in Canada. As a legal representative on a Research Ethics Board, you will encounter TCPS2 in virtually every protocol review, every consent document scrutiny, and every dispute about REB jurisdiction. Understanding its structure and authority is essential to your role.

## What Is TCPS2?

TCPS2 is a joint policy of Canada's three federal research funding agencies — the Canadian Institutes of Health Research (CIHR), the Natural Sciences and Engineering Research Council (NSERC), and the Social Sciences and Humanities Research Council (SSHRC), collectively known as the Tri-Councils. It was first published in 1998, substantially revised in 2010 (the "2nd edition"), and has been updated through amendments since then.

<Info>
  The authoritative, current text of TCPS2 — including all amendments — is published and maintained by the Panel on Research Ethics (PRE). You can access it through the Government of Canada's Panel on Research Ethics website at **pre.ethics.gc.ca**. The site also hosts the TCPS2 CORE tutorial, interpretive guidance, and a searchable HTML version of the full policy.
</Info>

### Who Must Comply?

<CardGroup cols={2}>
  <Card title="Institutions" icon="building-columns">
    Any Canadian institution that receives funding from CIHR, NSERC, or SSHRC — including hospitals, universities, and affiliated research institutes — must comply with TCPS2 as a condition of that funding.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Researchers" icon="flask">
    All researchers conducting human-subjects research at a compliant institution must follow TCPS2, regardless of whether their specific project is Tri-Council funded.
  </Card>

  <Card title="REBs" icon="shield-check">
    The institution's REB must be constituted, operate, and make decisions in accordance with TCPS2. Its composition, mandate, review processes, and records all fall under TCPS2 requirements.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Legal Representatives" icon="scale-balanced">
    As a legal rep on the REB, you must understand TCPS2 not only as ethics policy but as a contractual obligation embedded in the institution's funding agreements with the Tri-Councils.
  </Card>
</CardGroup>

## The Three Core Principles

TCPS2 is built on three foundational principles that inform every article and every REB decision. You should be able to identify when a protocol or consent document raises questions under each principle.

<CardGroup cols={3}>
  <Card title="Respect for Persons" icon="person">
    Individuals have inherent dignity and the right to make informed decisions about their own participation. This principle underpins consent requirements, protections for vulnerable populations, and the right to withdraw without penalty.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Concern for Welfare" icon="heart-pulse">
    Researchers and REBs must protect the physical, mental, spiritual, and social welfare of participants. Welfare extends beyond harm avoidance to include the interests of communities and third parties who may be affected.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Justice" icon="balance-scale">
    Benefits and burdens of research must be distributed fairly. No group should bear a disproportionate share of research risks, and no group should be arbitrarily excluded from research benefits.
  </Card>
</CardGroup>

## Key Chapters for Legal Review

The following chapters of TCPS2 are most directly implicated in the legal representative's work on an REB. Use the accordions below to review each chapter's scope and the specific legal questions it raises.

<Accordion title="Chapter 3 — Free and Informed Consent">
  **Scope:** Chapter 3 sets out the requirements for valid consent to research participation. It covers the essential elements of information disclosure, the conditions under which consent is free (absent coercion or undue influence), capacity and competence, consent for incapable adults and minors, and waivers of consent.

  **Key legal considerations:**

  - **Voluntariness:** Consent must be free from undue influence. As legal counsel, scrutinize any consent process where the researcher holds a position of authority over the participant (e.g., physician-patient, employer-employee, professor-student).
  - **Capacity:** TCPS 2 distinguishes between capable and incapable adults and sets rules for consent by authorized third parties. In Québec, cross-reference with the **Civil Code of Québec**, in particular arts. 11–25 (consent to care and to research, including consent for minors and adults incapable of consent) and art. 15 (hierarchy of persons who may consent on behalf of an incapable adult).
  - **Waivers of consent:** Article 3.7 allows the REB to approve a waiver or alteration of consent under strictly defined conditions. Your role is to assess whether the legal standard for waiver is met and whether the waiver is consistent with applicable privacy legislation.
  - **Ongoing consent:** Consent is a process, not a one-time event. Protocols must describe how researchers will manage material changes and how participants will be re-consented.
  - **Documentation:** Article 3.12 addresses when written consent is required and when it may be waived. Written waivers have evidentiary implications for the institution.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Chapter 5 — Privacy and Confidentiality">
  **Scope:** Chapter 5 addresses the collection, use, and disclosure of information about research participants. It establishes the concept of "identifiable information," degrees of identifiability, and the conditions under which researchers may collect, use, link, and disclose such information.

  **Key legal considerations:**

  - **Relationship to privacy law:** TCPS 2 Chapter 5 operates alongside — not in place of — Québec's health-information regime. For the MUHC REB the primary statute is **Law 5** (_Act Respecting Health and Social Services Information_), supplemented by **Law 25** for residual holdings and, for federally regulated data flows, by **PIPEDA**. Where standards differ, the more protective standard applies.
  - **Identifiability spectrum:** TCPS2 distinguishes directly identifying, indirectly identifying, coded, anonymized, and anonymous information. The legal obligations shift along this spectrum; understand where a given dataset falls.
  - **Secondary use of data:** Articles 5.5–5.7 address research using data collected for other purposes. Secondary use without consent requires REB approval and must satisfy the conditions in Article 5.5(a)–(e).
  - **Data linkage:** Linking datasets from different sources raises both privacy and jurisdictional issues. Ensure the protocol addresses data residency, access controls, and any cross-border transfer of personal health information.
  - **Certificates of confidentiality:** TCPS2 does not provide legal protection equivalent to a court order. Advise researchers that promises of confidentiality may be overridden by mandatory reporting laws, court orders, or coroner's investigations.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Chapter 7 — Conflicts of Interest">
  **Scope:** Chapter 7 addresses real, perceived, and potential conflicts of interest (COI) involving researchers, REB members, and the institution itself. It covers financial interests, personal relationships, and institutional pressures that may compromise the integrity of research or its review.

  **Key legal considerations:**

  - **REB member conflicts:** Article 7.3 requires REB members who have a conflict to declare it and absent themselves from related discussions and votes. As legal counsel, you have an obligation to declare conflicts and to assist the REB Chair in identifying undisclosed conflicts.
  - **Researcher conflicts:** The REB must assess whether a researcher's financial or personal interests could affect the design, conduct, or reporting of a study. Review COI disclosure forms critically; vague or incomplete disclosures should be flagged.
  - **Institutional conflicts:** Where the institution itself has a financial interest in a study (e.g., equity in a sponsor company, licensing revenue), TCPS2 requires additional safeguards. Assess whether the institutional COI policy adequately addresses these situations.
  - **Legal liability dimensions:** COI that goes unmanaged exposes the institution to regulatory scrutiny, civil liability, and reputational harm. Your role is to ensure the REB's process meets the standard of a reasonable, independent ethics review.
  - **Sponsored research agreements:** Review whether sponsor agreements contain clauses that could compromise researcher independence — including publication restrictions, data ownership provisions, or sponsor rights to terminate studies.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Chapter 11 — Clinical Trials">
  **Scope:** Chapter 11 applies to research involving the prospective assignment of participants to experimental and control conditions — i.e., clinical trials. It imposes requirements beyond those applicable to other research, including trial registration, safety monitoring, stopping rules, and data and safety monitoring boards (DSMBs).

  **Key legal considerations:**

  - **Relationship to Health Canada:** Clinical trials involving drugs, devices, or natural health products are also regulated under the _Food and Drugs Act_ and its regulations (CTA, Part C Division 5). TCPS2 Chapter 11 and Health Canada requirements operate concurrently. The REB must be satisfied that both sets of requirements are met.
  - **Trial registration:** Article 11.3 requires REB approval to be conditional on registration in an acceptable public registry. Unregistered trials raise both ethical and regulatory compliance concerns.
  - **Stopping rules and safety monitoring:** The protocol must include pre-specified stopping rules and a plan for safety monitoring. Assess whether the DSMB charter — if one exists — is constituted independently of the sponsor.
  - **Randomization and placebo controls:** TCPS2 requires that participants in control arms receive the best available treatment unless there is no proven treatment and the placebo is ethically acceptable. Align this with the Declaration of Helsinki standard if the study is internationally sponsored.
  - **Protocol amendments:** Any material change to an approved protocol requires prospective REB review. "Material" includes changes to eligibility criteria, dosing, safety monitoring, or the consent process. Your legal review should flag whether a proposed amendment is truly minor or substantive.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Chapter 12 — Human Biological Materials, Including Materials Related to Human Reproduction">
  **Scope:** Chapter 12 governs research involving human biological materials (HBM) — including tissues, cells, DNA, blood, and organs — whether collected prospectively for research or used secondarily from clinical or archival sources. It also addresses reproductive materials and embryos.

  **Key legal considerations:**

  - **Consent for HBM:** Separate consent may be required for the collection and storage of HBM, for specific research uses, and for future unspecified uses. Broad consent frameworks are permitted under Article 12.3 but must meet specific disclosure requirements.
  - **Biobanks and repositories:** If the protocol involves contributing to or drawing from a biobank, assess whether the biobank's own consent and governance framework satisfies TCPS2 and applicable property and privacy law.
  - **Property law:** Canadian courts have not definitively recognized property rights in excised human tissue. Nonetheless, institutional agreements with biobanks, sponsors, or commercial entities may create contractual rights in HBM. Review material transfer agreements carefully.
  - **Incidental findings:** Research use of genetic material may generate clinically significant incidental findings. The protocol must describe how incidental findings will be managed and whether participants will be informed. Consider the interaction with duty-to-warn principles in provincial law.
  - **Reproductive materials and embryos:** Research involving human embryos is additionally governed by the _Assisted Human Reproduction Act_ (AHRA) and, where applicable, provincial legislation. Confirm regulatory compliance before recommending REB approval.
</Accordion>

## How Compliance Is Assessed

<Steps>
  <Step title="Institutional Agreement">
    The institution's agreement with the Tri-Councils (the Memorandum of Agreement or institutional policy) requires TCPS2 compliance. Non-compliance can trigger funding suspension or termination.
  </Step>
  <Step title="REB Review">
    The REB is the primary mechanism for assessing compliance. Every protocol subject to TCPS2 must receive REB review proportionate to the level of risk before research begins.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Ongoing Monitoring">
    The REB monitors approved research through annual renewals, adverse event reporting, protocol amendment review, and — where warranted — audits or site visits.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Panel on Research Ethics Guidance">
    The Panel on Research Ethics (PRE) provides interpretive guidance, case studies, and FAQs. While PRE guidance is not binding, it reflects the authoritative interpretation of the policy and should inform your legal analysis.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Institutional Accountability">
    The institution's research office or Vice-President Research typically oversees systemic TCPS2 compliance. The REB reports to institutional leadership and is accountable for its review processes and decisions.
  </Step>
</Steps>

<Warning>
  TCPS2 is a policy condition, not a statute. However, its requirements are incorporated by reference into the institution's funding agreements, making compliance a contractual obligation enforceable by the Tri-Councils. Additionally, TCPS2 standards may be referenced in civil litigation as evidence of the applicable standard of care for research involving humans. Treat TCPS2 obligations with the same seriousness as regulatory requirements.
</Warning>
