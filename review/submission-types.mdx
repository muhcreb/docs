Not every submission that lands in your review queue looks the same. A first-in-human clinical trial sponsored by a multinational pharmaceutical company carries very different legal weight than a single-site anonymous survey of hospital staff. Recognizing the submission type early — before you open a single document — tells you which legal frameworks apply, which documents deserve the closest scrutiny, and how much time to allocate. This page walks through each major submission category you will encounter as a legal representative on a Canadian hospital REB.

<Note>
  Regardless of submission type, any study that presents **more than minimal risk** to participants, involves a **vulnerable population**, raises a **novel legal or ethical question**, or includes a **conflict of interest** that cannot be managed at the delegated level must be escalated to full board review. See [Review Levels](/review/review-levels) for the full escalation criteria.
</Note>

## Submission Categories

<Tabs>
  <Tab title="New Study Applications">
    A new study application is a first-time request for REB approval before any research activity involving human participants begins. It is the most document-intensive submission type and the one where thorough legal review has the greatest impact.

    ### What the Package Typically Contains

    - Research protocol (full version with version number and date)
    - Participant consent form(s) and, where applicable, assent forms for minors
    - Recruitment materials (advertisements, scripts, letters of invitation)
    - Investigator's Brochure or device description (for clinical trials)
    - Data management and privacy plan
    - Questionnaires, interview guides, or data collection instruments
    - Sponsor or funder agreement (CTA, grant agreement, data-use agreement)
    - Conflict-of-interest declarations for the PI and co-investigators
    - Health Canada regulatory filing confirmation (for CTAs/IND-exempt studies)
    - Institutional authorizations or site agreements (for multi-site studies)

    ### Subtypes and Legal Focus Areas

    <Accordion title="Clinical Trials (Drug, Device, Natural Health Products)">
      Clinical trials are the highest-risk, most heavily regulated new study subtype. They are governed by the _Food and Drug Regulations_ (Parts C.05 and C.08), _Medical Devices Regulations_, ICH E6(R3) Good Clinical Practice guidelines, and TCPS2 Chapter 11.

      **Legal review focus:**

      - **Clinical Trial Agreement (CTA):** Review against your institution's model CTA. Flag clauses that: restrict the investigator's ability to publish results; limit the REB's oversight authority; indemnify the sponsor at the institution's expense without cap; or purport to bind future REB decisions.
      - **Consent form:** Confirm all ICH E6(R3) mandatory consent elements are present, including the right to withdraw, compensation for injury language consistent with provincial tort law, and accurate description of Health Canada regulatory oversight.
      - **Regulatory status:** Confirm that a No Objection Letter (NOL) or equivalent has been issued by Health Canada, or that the study qualifies for an IND exemption, before the board issues approval.
      - **Insurance and indemnification:** Verify that sponsor indemnity and insurance provisions meet institutional minimums and that the institution is not accepting unlimited liability.
    </Accordion>

    <Accordion title="Observational Studies and Epidemiological Research">
      Observational studies involve no experimental intervention — researchers collect data by watching, measuring, or surveying without altering participants' care or behaviour. Risk is typically lower, but privacy and data governance issues are central.

      **Legal review focus:**

      - **Privacy legislation compliance:** Confirm that data collection, storage, and communication practices comply with the applicable Québec framework — primarily **Law 5** (_Act Respecting Health and Social Services Information_) for information held by the establishment, together with **Law 25** (residually) and **PIPEDA** (where applicable to specific inter-provincial or federally regulated flows).
      - **Data-use or data-sharing agreements:** If data are obtained from a third party (e.g., ICES, provincial registry, another institution), review the data-sharing agreement for permissible use limitations and re-identification risk provisions.
      - **Secondary use of identifiable information:** Where the study involves secondary use of health records without consent, confirm that the conditions for a consent waiver under TCPS2 Article 5.5A are met and documented.
    </Accordion>

    <Accordion title="Survey and Interview Studies">
      Survey and qualitative interview studies typically present minimal risk, but they can raise consent, confidentiality, and coercion issues — particularly when conducted in a dependent relationship (e.g., employer-employee, clinician-patient).

      **Legal review focus:**

      - **Voluntary participation and coercion risk:** Assess whether the recruitment relationship creates undue influence. Studies where a supervisor recruits their own employees, or a clinician recruits their own patients, require careful consent language and, potentially, a third-party recruiter.
      - **Confidentiality limits and mandatory reporting:** If interview topics could elicit disclosures that trigger mandatory reporting obligations (e.g., child abuse, imminent risk of harm), verify that the consent form discloses these limits accurately and that the protocol includes a referral and safety plan.
      - **Audio/video recording:** Confirm that consent language clearly describes recording, storage, access controls, and destruction timelines.
    </Accordion>

    <Accordion title="Chart Reviews and Secondary Use of Health Records">
      Retrospective chart reviews and database studies involve accessing existing health information, often without direct participant contact. They are common in hospital research and raise significant privacy law questions.

      **Legal review focus:**

      - **Privacy custodian authorization:** Confirm that the PI has obtained the necessary authorization from the health information custodian (e.g., the hospital's Privacy Officer) in addition to REB approval, as required by provincial legislation.
      - **Minimum necessary data:** Verify that the data fields requested are limited to what is necessary for the stated research purpose — a proportionality requirement under PIPEDA and most provincial health privacy statutes.
      - **Data linkage:** If the study links datasets from multiple sources, assess re-identification risk and confirm that the linkage methodology is described and that appropriate data governance safeguards are in place.
    </Accordion>

    <Accordion title="Tissue, Biobank, and Genetic Studies">
      Studies involving human biological materials — including prospective tissue collection, use of archived specimens, and genetic analysis — engage a distinct legal and ethical framework under TCPS2 Chapter 12.

      **Legal review focus:**

      - **Consent for future use:** Verify that consent forms for biobank contributions clearly describe the scope of future permitted uses, re-contact procedures, and the participant's right to withdraw their sample.
      - **Genetic information disclosure:** Assess the protocol's plan for handling incidental findings and unsolicited genetic information. Confirm that the consent form's disclosure language aligns with provincial genetic non-discrimination obligations under the _Genetic Non-Discrimination Act_.
      - **Material Transfer Agreements (MTAs):** Review MTAs for intellectual property provisions that could restrict the institution's rights over discoveries made using participant samples, and for provisions that limit re-export or secondary transfer of materials.
    </Accordion>
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Amendments">
    An amendment is a request to change a previously approved protocol, consent document, or study procedure. Amendments range from minor administrative corrections to substantial modifications that alter the risk-benefit profile of the study.

    ### What the Package Typically Contains

    - Completed amendment request form identifying each proposed change
    - Track-changed version of every affected document (protocol, consent form, recruitment materials, etc.)
    - Clean final version of every affected document
    - Justification for each change
    - Updated regulatory filings (if applicable)
    - Updated sponsor agreement or budget amendment (for industry-sponsored studies)

    ### Legal Review Focus

    <CardGroup cols={2}>
      <Card title="Scope of Change" icon="magnifying-glass" href="/review/amendments">
        Determine whether the amendment is **minor** (e.g., correcting a typographical error, adding a co-investigator with no competing interests) or **substantial** (e.g., adding a new study arm, increasing risk, broadening the participant population, or changing the data-sharing arrangements). Substantial amendments require the same depth of review as a new study application.
      </Card>

      <Card title="Consent Re-Contact" icon="users" href="/review/informed-consent">
        Assess whether the amendment requires re-consent of already-enrolled participants. Changes that increase risk, add new procedures, alter confidentiality protections, or modify compensation require re-contact and re-consent under TCPS2 Article 3.3. Verify that the PI's re-consent plan is legally sound.
      </Card>

      <Card title="Contractual Alignment" icon="handshake" href="/review/liability-and-indemnity">
        When the amendment modifies study procedures, confirm that the sponsor agreement is updated correspondingly. Amendments that add costs, extend timelines, or change the scope of the investigator's obligations may require a formal contract amendment before the REB approves the protocol change.
      </Card>

      <Card title="Regulatory Notification" icon="bell" href="/review/reb-decisions">
        For clinical trials, certain protocol amendments require sponsor notification to Health Canada. Confirm that the PI has acknowledged this obligation and has described the regulatory notification plan, particularly for amendments that affect participant safety.
      </Card>
    </CardGroup>
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Renewals / Continuing Reviews">
    REB approval is time-limited. Studies must undergo continuing review at intervals determined by the board — typically annually, though higher-risk studies may be reviewed more frequently. The renewal submission demonstrates that the study is ongoing, that no new risks have emerged, and that the approved protocol is being followed.

    ### What the Package Typically Contains

    - Annual renewal or continuing review form
    - Enrollment and accrual summary
    - Summary of any adverse events, unanticipated problems, or protocol deviations since the last review
    - Updated investigator's brochure or safety report (for clinical trials)
    - Current approved consent form (confirming no unapproved changes have been made)
    - Statement of expected study completion date

    ### Legal Review Focus

    <Accordion title="Deviation and Non-Compliance Review">
      Review the summary of protocol deviations and non-compliance events. Assess whether any deviation has legal significance — for example, a consent process violation, an unauthorized data disclosure, or a procedure performed without proper approval. Flag deviations that may require reporting to Health Canada, the institution's Privacy Officer, or institutional leadership.
    </Accordion>

    <Accordion title="Adverse Event and Safety Signal Review">
      For clinical trials, confirm that all serious adverse events (SAEs) and serious unexpected suspected adverse reactions (SUSARs) have been reported to Health Canada within the regulatory timelines (7 days for fatal/life-threatening SUSARs; 15 days for others) under _Food and Drug Regulations_ C.05.014. Assess whether the accumulation of safety signals since the last review warrants consent form updates or escalation to full board.
    </Accordion>

    <Accordion title="Consent Currency">
      Confirm that the consent form used with participants throughout the approval period is the most recently approved version, and that any required re-consent events (triggered by prior amendments) have been documented. A consent form that has drifted from the approved version is a significant legal and regulatory liability.
    </Accordion>

    <Accordion title="Contractual Status">
      For industry-sponsored studies, confirm that the sponsor agreement remains current, that all amendments to the agreement have been executed, and that any regulatory commitments made by the sponsor (e.g., indemnity, insurance certificates) remain in force.
    </Accordion>
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Adverse Event Reports">
    Adverse event (AE) reports notify the REB of unexpected or serious harms that have occurred in the course of a study. They are among the most time-sensitive submissions in the REB queue.

    ### What the Package Typically Contains

    - Completed adverse event / unanticipated problem report form
    - Description of the event, date, severity, and relationship to study procedures
    - Actions taken by the investigator (including any immediate safety measures)
    - Assessment of impact on ongoing participant safety
    - Proposed protocol or consent form changes (if any)
    - Health Canada or sponsor safety report (for clinical trials)

    ### Legal Review Focus

    <Warning>
      Serious adverse events in clinical trials have **mandatory Health Canada reporting timelines**. If the REB receives an AE report and you identify that the required regulatory reporting has not occurred or is late, escalate immediately to the REB Chair and the institution's research compliance office. Failure to report is a regulatory offence under the _Food and Drug Regulations_.
    </Warning>

    <CardGroup cols={2}>
      <Card title="Causality Assessment" icon="link" href="/review/adverse-events">
        Review the investigator's causality assessment critically. If the event is plausibly related to the study intervention and was not anticipated in the protocol, it likely triggers consent form update obligations and may require immediate participant notification.
      </Card>

      <Card title="Institutional Liability" icon="building-columns" href="/review/liability-and-indemnity">
        Assess whether the adverse event creates institutional exposure — for example, if the event involved a deviation from the approved protocol, inadequate participant monitoring, or a failure of a safety mechanism that the institution warranted in a sponsor agreement.
      </Card>

      <Card title="Mandatory Reporting" icon="triangle-exclamation" href="/review/adverse-events">
        Determine whether the event triggers any mandatory reporting obligations beyond the REB — to Health Canada, the Office of the Privacy Commissioner (if a data breach is involved), the hospital's Chief Risk Officer, or the institution's insurer.
      </Card>

      <Card title="Participant Notification" icon="envelope" href="/review/informed-consent">
        Assess whether enrolled participants must be re-contacted and re-consented based on the new safety information. The threshold for re-consent is whether the new information would reasonably affect a participant's decision to continue.
      </Card>
    </CardGroup>
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Study Closures">
    A study closure submission notifies the REB that a study has concluded — either as planned, early (due to safety concerns, funding loss, investigator departure, or sponsor decision), or following a regulatory hold.

    ### What the Package Typically Contains

    - Study closure or final report form
    - Final enrollment numbers and completion status
    - Summary of any outstanding adverse events or safety issues
    - Data destruction or archival plan
    - Confirmation of regulatory filing closure (for clinical trials)
    - Return or destruction of investigational product (for drug/device trials)

    ### Legal Review Focus

    <Accordion title="Early Termination Reasons">
      If the study is closing early, determine the reason. Early termination for safety reasons has specific regulatory reporting obligations. Early termination due to sponsor withdrawal may have contractual implications — confirm whether the PI has obligations under the CTA to notify Health Canada and whether data ownership and publication rights provisions in the sponsor agreement have been triggered.
    </Accordion>

    <Accordion title="Data Retention and Destruction">
      Review the data archival and destruction plan against applicable legal requirements. For clinical trials, _Food and Drug Regulations_ and ICH E6(R3) require that essential documents be retained for the periods specified by the current guideline — typically at least 2 years after formal discontinuation or the last regulatory submission, and substantially longer (commonly 15 years or more) for trials supporting a marketing authorization. Confirm that the plan aligns with provincial health privacy legislation requirements for data retention of health records.
    </Accordion>

    <Accordion title="Outstanding Obligations">
      Confirm that all conditions attached to the original or continuing approval have been addressed, that all adverse event reports have been resolved, and that there are no outstanding regulatory correspondence items with Health Canada. A study should not be closed with unresolved material regulatory issues.
    </Accordion>

    <Accordion title="Participant Notification">
      Determine whether enrolled or recently completed participants must be notified of the study closure — particularly if final results are available, if the study closed early for safety reasons, or if the data will be used in a manner not contemplated in the original consent form.
    </Accordion>
  </Tab>
</Tabs>
