As a legal representative on a Research Ethics Board, you are one of the final safeguards ensuring that research conducted at your institution meets both ethical and legal standards. The REB protocol review process is a structured, multi-stage cycle that begins the moment a researcher submits an application and ends only when the board issues a final decision and the researcher has addressed every outstanding condition. Understanding each stage — and knowing precisely where your expertise is required — lets you intervene effectively rather than reactively.

## The End-to-End Review Cycle

<Steps>
  <Step title="Researcher Submission">
    The principal investigator (PI) assembles the complete application package and submits it through the institution's electronic submission platform (e.g., Research Ethics Manager, IRIS, or a similar system). The package typically includes the protocol, consent documents, recruitment materials, data management plan, sponsor agreements, and any conflict-of-interest declarations.

    **Your role at this stage:** None directly. However, you should be familiar with the submission requirements so that you can flag incomplete packages efficiently during screening review.
  </Step>
  <Step title="REB Office Administrative Screening">
    The REB Coordinator reviews the submission for administrative completeness — confirming that all required forms are present, signatures are in place, and supporting documents match the protocol version. Incomplete submissions are returned to the PI before they enter the formal review queue.

    **Your role at this stage:** The Coordinator may flag legal documents for your preliminary review (e.g., a Clinical Trial Agreement, a data-sharing agreement, or a consent form involving a third-party sponsor). Respond to these requests promptly so as not to delay the queue.

    <Note>
      Administrative screening is not an ethical or legal assessment. A submission that passes screening has met the **form** requirements, not the **substance** requirements. Legal issues often surface for the first time during full reviewer assessment.
    </Note>
  </Step>
  <Step title="Risk Classification and Reviewer Assignment">
    The REB Chair (or designate) reviews the screened submission and assigns it to the appropriate review pathway — **full board**, **delegated**, or **administrative only** — based on the risk level and study type under the _Tri-Council Policy Statement: Ethical Conduct for Research Involving Humans_ (TCPS2).

    The Chair then assigns primary and secondary reviewers. Legal representatives are assigned as either:

    - A **primary reviewer** when the submission contains complex legal documents (multi-site agreements, commercial sponsor contracts, First Nations data sovereignty clauses, or health information privacy issues), or
    - A **resource reviewer** consulted by the primary reviewer on specific clauses.

    <Tip>
      When you receive an assignment, check the study type and sponsor type immediately. Industry-sponsored clinical trials almost always require a review of the Clinical Trial Agreement against institutional policy and _Food and Drug Regulations_ C.05.001 requirements before consent review begins.
    </Tip>
  </Step>
  <Step title="Reviewer Assessment">
    Assigned reviewers independently assess the protocol against the TCPS2 criteria, institutional SOPs, and applicable law (PIPEDA/provincial health privacy legislation, _Food and Drug Act_, _Medical Devices Regulations_, ICH E6(R3) GCP guidelines, etc.). Each reviewer produces a written report or annotated checklist submitted to the REB Coordinator before the deadline.

    **As a legal reviewer, your assessment should address:**

    <CardGroup cols={2}>
      <Card title="Consent Document Review" icon="file-signature" href="/review/informed-consent">
        Verify that consent documents meet provincial capacity legislation, include all mandatory disclosure elements, and are written at an accessible reading level. Flag any clauses that limit participant rights or waive liability improperly.
      </Card>

      <Card title="Contractual & Regulatory Compliance" icon="scale-balanced" href="/review/liability-and-indemnity">
        Review sponsor agreements, data-use agreements, and site agreements for compliance with institutional policy, TCPS2 Article 11 and 12 obligations, and applicable federal and provincial law.
      </Card>

      <Card title="Privacy & Data Governance" icon="shield-halved" href="/review/privacy-and-data">
        Confirm that the data management plan satisfies provincial health information protection legislation and, where applicable, PIPEDA or sector-specific statutes (e.g., _Mental Health Act_ requirements on record disclosure).
      </Card>

      <Card title="Regulatory Filings" icon="clipboard-list" href="/review/submission-types">
        For clinical trials, confirm that Health Canada regulatory filing obligations are acknowledged and that the protocol version matches the filed Investigational New Drug (IND) or Clinical Trial Application (CTA) documentation.
      </Card>
    </CardGroup>
  </Step>
  <Step title="Full Board or Delegated Review Meeting">
    Reviewer reports are circulated to board members prior to the meeting. At the meeting, the primary reviewer presents a summary of findings. For **full board review**, the entire board deliberates and votes. For **delegated review**, the assigned delegated reviewer(s) issue a decision on behalf of the board.

    **Your role at this stage:**

    - Present legal findings clearly and concisely — avoid legal jargon that non-lawyer board members may not understand.
    - Flag issues that meet the threshold for escalation from delegated to full board review (see [Review Levels](/review/review-levels)).
    - Abstain from voting on matters where you have a conflict of interest, but remain available to answer legal questions from other board members.

    <Info>
      Under TCPS2 Article 6.4, the REB operates as a committee, not as a collection of individual experts. Your legal opinion informs the board's deliberation, but the **decision belongs to the board collectively**.
    </Info>
  </Step>
  <Step title="REB Decision Issued">
    After deliberation, the REB issues one of four standard decisions:

    | Decision | Meaning |
    | --- | --- |
    | **Approved** | The protocol may proceed as submitted. |
    | **Approved with Conditions** | Approval is contingent on the researcher satisfying specified conditions (often consent revisions or clarifications). |
    | **Deferred** | Substantial concerns require major revisions before a decision can be made; the revised submission will return for review. |
    | **Rejected** | The protocol cannot be approved in its current form; the researcher may appeal under institutional policy. |

    The REB Coordinator drafts the decision letter, incorporating the legal reviewer's written conditions verbatim where applicable. Review the draft decision letter before it is issued if your conditions are included — small drafting errors can create ambiguity.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Researcher Response and Condition Clearance">
    For **Approved with Conditions** decisions, the PI submits a written response addressing each condition. The Coordinator routes legal conditions back to you for clearance. You must:

    1. Confirm that the revised document fully satisfies the stated condition.
    2. If the response is inadequate, issue a clear written explanation of what remains outstanding.
    3. Once all conditions are cleared, confirm clearance in writing to the Coordinator.

    <Warning>
      Do not provide verbal clearance. All condition clearances must be documented in the REB file to create a complete, auditable record. Undocumented clearances can create liability exposure during Health Canada inspections or litigation.
    </Warning>
  </Step>
  <Step title="Ongoing Oversight and Continuing Review">
    REB approval is not a one-time event. Approved studies are subject to continuing oversight, including annual renewals, amendment reviews, and adverse event reporting. Legal issues can arise at any point in the study lifecycle — contract renegotiations, privacy breaches, protocol deviations with legal implications, or changes in applicable law.

    Monitor your institution's active-study portfolio for flagged legal issues and respond promptly to REB Coordinator requests throughout the study's lifespan.
  </Step>
</Steps>
