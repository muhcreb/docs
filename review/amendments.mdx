Protocol amendments are a routine feature of active research, but they carry real legal risk if misclassified or inadequately reviewed. As a legal representative on the REB, your role is not to evaluate the science of a proposed change but to identify whether it introduces new legal exposure, alters the risk-benefit calculus for participants, or triggers fresh regulatory obligations. A well-structured amendment review prevents legal problems downstream — at the point of adverse event litigation, regulatory audit, or participant complaint.

## What Triggers an Amendment?

Any change to an approved protocol, consent form, or study operations that was not described in the original approved submission requires a formal amendment request. Common triggers include:

<CardGroup cols={2}>
  <Card title="Protocol Changes" icon="flask">
    Modifications to study design, dosing, procedures, visit schedule, inclusion/exclusion criteria, or primary/secondary endpoints
  </Card>

  <Card title="Consent Form Updates" icon="file-signature">
    New risk information, updated contact details, changes to compensation, revised data-use language, or corrections to errors in the approved form
  </Card>

  <Card title="Site and Investigator Changes" icon="hospital">
    Addition of new study sites, change of principal investigator, addition of co-investigators, or transfer of sponsor responsibilities
  </Card>

  <Card title="Risk Level Changes" icon="triangle-exclamation">
    New safety signals from interim analyses, updated investigator brochures, Health Canada safety advisories, or regulatory authority requests for protocol modification
  </Card>

  <Card title="Data and Privacy Changes" icon="database">
    New data collection elements, changes to data storage locations, additions to secondary data use, or new data sharing with third parties
  </Card>

  <Card title="Funding and Sponsorship Changes" icon="building-columns">
    Change of sponsor, new funding source with different data-ownership terms, or material changes to the Clinical Trial Agreement
  </Card>
</CardGroup>

## Amendment Classification: Administrative vs. Full Review

Not every change warrants a convened full board review. Correctly classifying an amendment is the first and most consequential step, because misclassification in either direction creates risk — under-review exposes participants; over-review wastes resources and delays important safety updates.

<Steps>
  <Step title="Receive the Amendment Request">
    The REB Coordinator receives the amendment form and supporting documents. Confirm that the submission includes: a description of the change, a rationale, redlined versions of any modified documents, and the PI's attestation that the change does not constitute a new study.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Apply the Administrative Amendment Test">
    Ask: Does this change affect **participant safety, risk level, consent adequacy, or study integrity**? If the honest answer to all of these is **No**, the amendment is likely administrative.

    **Administrative amendments** (chair or delegate review only) typically include:

    - Correction of typographical errors that do not affect meaning
    - Administrative contact information updates
    - Changes to the study title that do not affect protocol content
    - Addition of a co-investigator with no new procedures
    - Minor formatting changes to consent forms that do not alter substance
  </Step>
  <Step title="Apply the Expedited Review Test">
    If the change is more than purely administrative but does not substantially alter the risk profile, it may qualify for **expedited (chair-delegate) review**:

    - Minor consent form language clarifications that improve but do not substantively change disclosure
    - Addition of a sub-investigator with clearly defined and limited responsibilities
    - Administrative site additions where all study procedures are identical
    - Updates that reduce participant burden (e.g., elimination of a non-critical study visit)
  </Step>
  <Step title="Identify Changes Requiring Full Board Review">
    Route the amendment to the full board whenever it:

    - Increases the risk level for current or future participants
    - Substantially modifies the consent process or disclosure of material risks
    - Adds new invasive procedures or biological sample collection
    - Expands data collection in a way that triggers additional privacy law obligations
    - Changes the study population (age range, capacity status, vulnerable populations)
    - Arises from a safety signal or regulatory authority directive
    - Alters indemnification, liability, or data-ownership terms in the CTA
  </Step>
  <Step title="Legal Representative Review">
    For any amendment reaching full board or expedited review, conduct your legal assessment as described in the section below before the board meeting.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Issue the Decision">
    The board approves, approves with conditions, or requires modifications. Issue an amended approval letter with an updated expiry date. Confirm that the PI understands which version of each document is now the approved version.
  </Step>
</Steps>

## Decision Tree: Which Review Pathway?

<Tabs>
  <Tab title="Step-by-Step Questions">
    Use these questions in sequence. Stop at the first **Yes** and follow the indicated pathway.

    | Question | If Yes → |
    | --- | --- |
    | Does the change affect participant safety or risk level? | **Full Board Review** |
    | Does the change alter what participants are told in the consent process? | **Full Board Review** |
    | Does the change add new procedures, biological samples, or data collection? | **Full Board Review** |
    | Does the change involve a new vulnerable population or expand the age range? | **Full Board Review** |
    | Does the change arise from a safety signal, SAE, or regulatory directive? | **Full Board Review** |
    | Does the change affect a material legal term (indemnification, IP, data ownership)? | **Full Board Review** |
    | Does the change involve a new investigator with new study responsibilities? | **Expedited Review** |
    | Is the change a minor consent form clarification with no new risk disclosure? | **Expedited Review** |
    | Is the change purely administrative (contact info, typo, formatting)? | **Administrative — Chair Sign-off** |
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Key Legal Thresholds">
    **Threshold 1 — Consent Adequacy** Any change that adds, modifies, or removes information that a reasonable participant would consider material to their decision to participate requires full board review and re-consent of currently enrolled participants.

    **Threshold 2 — Regulatory Footprint** Changes that add a new drug, device, or biologic to the protocol, or that expand the indication under study, likely require a CTA amendment or new Health Canada submission. The REB cannot approve the amendment until the regulatory pathway is confirmed.

    **Threshold 3 — Privacy Law Triggers** New data elements, new data custodians, or new destinations for data communication may engage Law 5, Law 25, or PIPEDA obligations. In particular, any change that introduces a **communication of personal information outside Québec** requires a Law 25 ÉFVP before implementation. Confirm with the establishment's _responsable de l'accès à l'information_ before the board meeting.

    **Threshold 4 — Contractual Change** Amendments that alter any term of the CTA, Data Sharing Agreement, or Material Transfer Agreement require sponsor concurrence and legal sign-off before REB approval is finalised.
  </Tab>
</Tabs>

## What the Legal Representative Looks for in an Amendment

<Accordion title="New Legal Risks and Liability Exposure">
  Review whether the proposed change:

  - Introduces a procedure with a higher adverse event profile that existing indemnification language may not cover
  - Adds a new sponsor or contract research organisation (CRO) not covered by the current CTA
  - Changes the PI in a way that transfers regulatory responsibility without a corresponding contractual assignment
  - Removes a safeguard that was itself a condition of the original REB approval

  **Action:** If new liability exposure is identified, require evidence of updated indemnification or institutional risk-management sign-off before approving the amendment.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Changes to Consent and Participant Rights">
  Review whether:

  - The amendment adds new risks or benefits that participants have not been informed of
  - Currently enrolled participants must be re-consented (and if so, whether the re-consent process itself is adequate)
  - The amendment affects participants who have completed active study procedures but remain in follow-up
  - The change affects participants who lack capacity, requiring substitute decision-maker notification

  **Action:** Require the PI to provide a re-consent plan for currently enrolled participants whenever material new information is added, consistent with TCPS2 Article 3.2.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Privacy Implications of New Data Collection">
  Review whether:

  - New data elements are identifiable or potentially re-identifiable in combination with existing data
  - Data sharing with new third parties requires a new or amended Privacy Impact Assessment (PIA)
  - Data storage in a new jurisdiction (e.g., a US-based cloud service) triggers extra-Québec communication obligations under Law 25 (ÉFVP \+ information-sharing agreement) and, where applicable, PIPEDA
  - The original consent form authorised the expanded data use, or whether re-consent is required for data use changes

  **Action:** Require a PIA update or privacy counsel attestation for any amendment that materially expands data collection or sharing.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Regulatory Compliance Confirmation">
  Review whether:

  - The amendment to a drug or device trial requires a CTA amendment filed with Health Canada
  - The amended protocol aligns with any Investigational Testing Authorization (ITA) conditions
  - The amendment is consistent with the current approved product monograph or investigator brochure
  - Any Québec regulatory body (e.g., the MSSS for designation-relevant changes, the FRQ for funded trials, or the CAI for confidentiality-related changes) must be notified where required

  **Action:** Do not approve amendments that expand regulated activities until written confirmation of regulatory compliance is provided.
</Accordion>

## Common Pitfalls

<Tip>
  **Watch for scope creep.** Investigators sometimes submit a series of individually minor amendments that collectively transform the original protocol into a fundamentally different study. Review amendments not just in isolation but against the **original approved protocol**. If cumulative changes have altered the study's design, population, risk level, or purpose, the board should require a fresh full submission rather than approving another incremental amendment. This protects the integrity of the original ethical review and ensures participants consented to the study they are actually participating in.
</Tip>

<Warning>
  A PI who implements a protocol change **before** receiving REB amendment approval has committed a protocol deviation — and potentially a regulatory violation under the _Food and Drug Regulations_ if the study is subject to Health Canada oversight. As legal representative, advise the board that unauthorized changes must be documented as deviations, reported to the sponsor, and reviewed for participant safety impact, separate from the amendment review process.
</Warning>

<Note>
  Under TCPS2 Article 6.15, the REB must be **promptly notified** of any change that affects participant safety, even if the PI believes the change is minor. Emphasise to the board that the classification of amendments must be made by the REB — not unilaterally by the investigator.
</Note>

## Reviewing Amendments Involving Vulnerable Populations

<CardGroup cols={2}>
  <Card title="Minors and Assent" icon="child">
    Amendments that lower the minimum age of eligibility, or that add procedures to be conducted on minors, must include an updated assent process appropriate to the developmental stage of newly included participants.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Persons Lacking Capacity" icon="user-shield">
    Any amendment affecting participants who lack decision-making capacity must confirm that substitute-consent procedures remain adequate and compliant with **arts. 15 and 21 of the _Civil Code of Québec_** (as applicable to consent to care and consent to research).
  </Card>

  <Card title="Indigenous Communities" icon="people-group">
    Amendments that expand data collection from or about Indigenous communities, or that change data governance arrangements, require renewed engagement with community leadership consistent with TCPS2 Chapter 9.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Prisoners and Detainees" icon="building">
    Any amendment that adds incarcerated individuals to the study population triggers heightened scrutiny of voluntariness and undue influence, and may require consultation with correctional authorities.
  </Card>
</CardGroup>
