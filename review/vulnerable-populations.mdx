Research involving vulnerable populations demands the most careful legal scrutiny you will apply at the REB. Vulnerability in the research context is not simply a clinical characteristic — it is a legal and ethical concept that describes any condition that reduces a person's ability to give free, informed, and genuinely voluntary consent, or that heightens their exposure to harm from research participation. The TCPS2 does not provide a single exhaustive definition of "vulnerable," but it identifies categories and principles that should guide your assessment of each protocol on its specific facts.

## Who Counts as a Vulnerable Population?

The TCPS2 (Chapter 4) identifies several population categories that warrant heightened ethical and legal scrutiny. Vulnerability may arise from compromised decision-making capacity, situational power imbalances, institutional dependency, or particular susceptibility to harm.

<CardGroup cols={3}>
  <Card title="Children and Youth" icon="child">
    Individuals under 18 (the age of majority in Québec) who lack legal capacity to consent independently. Protections under art. 21 CCQ are discussed in detail below.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Cognitively Impaired Adults" icon="brain">
    Adults whose decision-making capacity is impaired by dementia, acquired brain injury, intellectual disabilities, acute psychiatric illness, or other conditions affecting comprehension or judgment.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Persons in Custody" icon="building">
    Individuals in correctional institutions, immigration detention, or other custodial settings where the coercive environment may compromise voluntariness.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Economic Dependents" icon="hand-holding-dollar">
    Individuals whose financial circumstances may make compensation for research participation unduly influential, potentially overriding legitimate risk concerns.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Institutional Dependents" icon="user-group">
    Patients, students, employees, military personnel, or others in relationships of institutional authority with researchers, where refusal may be perceived as carrying consequences.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Terminally Ill Patients" icon="heart-pulse">
    Individuals who may be especially vulnerable to therapeutic misconception or to accepting elevated risk in the hope of benefit when no other options remain.
  </Card>
</CardGroup>

<Info>
  Vulnerability is context-dependent and may be temporary. A patient experiencing acute delirium may regain capacity after treatment. A student enrolled in a researcher's course is only in a dependent relationship during that enrollment. The protocol must account for this variability — static categorization of participants as "vulnerable" without a plan for capacity assessment and re-consent is a legal deficiency.
</Info>

## Population-Specific Legal Frameworks

<Tabs>
  <Tab title="Children">
    ### Consent and Assent for Minor Participants

    Research involving a minor (under 18 in Québec) is governed by **art. 21 CCQ**: the research must be approved by a competent research ethics committee (an MSSS-designated REB where applicable), the risk must be proportionate to the expected benefit, and consent must be given by the person having parental authority or the tutor. The minor's own assent must be sought where they are capable of understanding, and their refusal must be respected (art. 21 CCQ).

    **Parental/guardian consent requirements:**

    - Both parents must generally consent unless one parent has sole legal custody, is unavailable after reasonable effort, or provincial legislation permits single-parent consent.
    - Guardians appointed under provincial child welfare legislation may consent on behalf of children in state care, but only if the research is consistent with the guardian's statutory obligations to act in the child's best interests.
    - Review whether the protocol adequately identifies the consent authority for children who are ward of the Crown, in foster care, or in other non-parental custody arrangements.

    **Assent of the minor:**

    - Children as young as 7 may be capable of expressing a meaningful preference about research participation. TCPS2 Article 4.6 requires assent be sought from minors where they are capable of assenting.
    - An assent form must be written in plain, age-appropriate language. A form written for adults re-titled "Assent Form" is not adequate.
    - A minor's **dissent** (refusal to participate or continue) must be respected unless the study offers direct therapeutic benefit unavailable outside the study and both the parents and the REB have determined participation is in the child's best interests.

    **Mature minor doctrine:** The common law recognizes that some minors demonstrate sufficient understanding and judgment to make their own medical and research decisions. In Quebec, the Civil Code (art. 14) permits consent by minors 14 years and older for care not presenting serious risk. The protocol must describe:

    - How mature minor status will be assessed,
    - Who makes the determination, and
    - What documentation will be kept.

    **Acceptable study designs involving minors:**

    - Studies that offer reasonable prospect of direct benefit to the individual minor participant
    - Studies involving no more than minimal risk that generate knowledge directly applicable to the health of children
    - Studies with greater than minimal risk where the potential benefit justifies the risk and is at least as favorable as available alternatives

    **Unacceptable study designs:**

    - Trials that expose minors to more than minimal risk with no prospect of direct benefit, solely to obtain generalizable knowledge
    - Studies that use minors as a convenience population when the research question could be answered using adult participants
    - Studies in which parental consent is treated as sufficient without any consideration of child assent

    <Warning>
      A protocol that proposes to enroll children without providing separate age-appropriate assent forms, or that treats parental consent as automatically determinative of the child's participation regardless of the child's expressed preferences, fails to meet TCPS2 and common law standards. Flag this as requiring revision before approval.
    </Warning>
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Cognitively Impaired Adults">
    ### Decision-Making Capacity and Substitute Consent

    Research involving adults with conditions affecting cognitive function — including dementia, acquired brain injury, intellectual disabilities, severe mental illness, and acute medical conditions producing delirium or altered consciousness — requires careful attention to both the legal framework for substitute decision-making and the ongoing assessment of individual capacity.

    **Capacity assessment:**

    - Capacity to consent to research is decision-specific and time-specific. A person may have capacity to consent to a low-complexity questionnaire study but lack capacity to consent to an invasive clinical trial.
    - The protocol must describe how and by whom capacity will be assessed. Clinician judgment alone is insufficient for complex research decisions — a structured capacity assessment tool (e.g., MacArthur Competence Assessment Tool for Clinical Research, MacCAT-CR) is best practice.
    - Capacity must be reassessed at key decision points, including when material changes to the protocol occur or when a previously incapacitated participant shows signs of recovered capacity.

    **Substitute-consent framework:** In Québec, consent to research on an adult incapable of consent is governed by **art. 21 CCQ**, with substitute consent given by the **mandatary** (under a protection mandate confirmed by the court), the **tutor**, or the **curator**. For consent to care generally, art. 15 CCQ sets out the hierarchy (mandatary, tutor, curator, then spouse, then close relative or person showing special interest). Confirm the protocol identifies the correct substitute for the research context and the legal standard governing the substitute's decision.

    | SDM Standard | Description |
    | --- | --- |
    | **Prior capable wish** | If the person expressed a relevant wish while capable, the SDM must follow it, even if the SDM disagrees. |
    | **Best interests** | If no relevant prior capable wish exists, the SDM must act in the person's best interests, considering their values, beliefs, and current well-being. |

    **Research-specific SDM considerations:**

    - SDM consent for research is more legally complex than SDM consent for treatment. Unlike treatment, research does not necessarily offer direct benefit to the individual participant. The SDM's "best interests" analysis must account for this.
    - Article 21 CCQ permits substitute consent to research on an incapable adult even where the research is not expected to produce results directly beneficial to the participant, but only if the research **could not be carried out** on capable persons yielding the same knowledge, the risk is proportionate to the expected benefit, and the REB is MSSS-designated. This is a stricter test than the standard for consent to care (art. 15 CCQ), and it must be documented in the REB decision.
    - The consent form must include a separate SDM signature block that documents the SDM's relationship to the participant and the basis for their authority.

    **Ongoing assent:** Even where valid SDM consent exists, participants who are capable of expressing a preference should be asked for their assent at each study contact. A participant's clear expression of unwillingness to continue — verbal or behavioral — must be respected regardless of the SDM's consent.

    <Note>
      If the protocol proposes to enroll incapacitated adults without providing for SDM consent and without meeting the criteria for waiver of consent under TCPS2 Article 3.7, it cannot be approved. There is no legal mechanism under Canadian law to conduct research on incapacitated adults without either their own valid consent, valid SDM consent, or an REB-authorized consent waiver meeting all applicable conditions.
    </Note>
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Persons in Custody">
    ### Research in Correctional and Custodial Settings

    Individuals in custody — including federal and provincial correctional institutions, immigration detention centres, and youth custody facilities — are presumed to be in a coercive environment that may compromise the voluntariness of research consent.

    **The voluntariness problem:** Incarcerated individuals may consent to research participation because they perceive it as a way to secure benefits (e.g., improved conditions, positive attention from staff), avoid negative consequences (e.g., appearing uncooperative), or simply relieve boredom. None of these are freely autonomous motivations. Your role is to scrutinize the consent process and the study design for measures that genuinely address these pressures.

    **Legal review checklist:**

    - Does the protocol describe how the research team will be clearly distinguished from correctional or custodial staff?
    - Is it clearly stated in the consent form and during recruitment that participation will have no effect on the participant's custodial status, parole eligibility, release date, or institutional privileges?
    - Are compensation amounts modest enough that they do not create an undue inducement given the limited access to income in custody?
    - Does the study design require participation during institutional hours or activities that could create implicit pressure to participate?
    - Is withdrawal truly without consequence, including without any notation in the participant's institutional file?

    **Acceptable research in custodial settings:** Research in correctional settings is ethically defensible when: (a) it could not reasonably be conducted outside the custodial setting, (b) it offers direct benefit to the participant or addresses health needs specific to the incarcerated population, and (c) robust safeguards against coercion are documented in the protocol.

    **Unacceptable research:** Research that treats the custodial population as a convenient captive pool for studies that have no particular relevance to their health needs, or that offers compensation structured in ways that exploit economic deprivation in custody, must be rejected.

    <Warning>
      **Coercive Recruitment in Custodial Settings**

      Never approve a protocol in which correctional officers, parole officers, immigration officials, or institutional staff are involved in recruiting participants, distributing consent forms, or collecting consents. The inherent authority of these roles over the participants' daily lives and future prospects renders consent obtained through their involvement inherently suspect. Recruitment must be conducted by independent research personnel with no custodial authority.
    </Warning>
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Students & Employees">
    ### Dependent Relationships and Institutional Power

    Students enrolled in courses taught by researchers, employees of institutions where research is being conducted, and patients of clinicians who are also investigators present a distinct form of vulnerability rooted in institutional power imbalances rather than impaired capacity.

    **The coercion risk:** Even without explicit coercion, potential participants who are students, employees, or patients of a researcher may reasonably fear that declining to participate could affect their grades, employment, or clinical care. This perception — whether accurate or not — compromises the voluntariness of consent.

    **Legal and ethical requirements:**

    - The protocol must include explicit, written assurances in the consent form that refusal to participate or withdrawal at any time will have absolutely no effect on the participant's academic standing, employment status, or access to clinical care.
    - Recruitment of students by their own course instructors in studies where participation might influence grades is presumptively coercive. The protocol must describe how this conflict is managed (e.g., recruitment through a neutral third party, course credit offered by a non-involved faculty member, opt-in rather than opt-out framing).
    - Employer-sponsored research recruiting employees as participants must include an explicit statement that participation is entirely voluntary, that the employer will not have access to individual results, and that non-participation carries no workplace consequences.

    **Acceptable design features:**

    - Recruitment through neutral channels (posters, institutional email lists) rather than direct solicitation by the researcher-authority figure
    - Extra precautions to separate the researcher's dual roles (clinician/investigator, instructor/researcher)
    - Anonymous or confidential data collection to reduce fear of identification

    <Warning>
      **Coercive Recruitment in Dependent Relationships**

      Any protocol in which a researcher directly recruits their own students, patients, or direct reports as research participants — without structural safeguards that genuinely separate the research relationship from the authority relationship — must be flagged for major revision. The appearance of coercion is itself a legal and ethical problem, regardless of the researcher's subjective intent. The REB may require that recruitment be delegated to an independent team member with no authority over the potential participants.
    </Warning>

    **Employees in occupational health research:** Studies investigating workplace health, exposures, or practices present additional complexity because: (a) the employer's interest in the data may conflict with individual employee privacy interests, and (b) collective agreement provisions may govern employee participation in workplace studies. Confirm the protocol addresses both.
  </Tab>
</Tabs>

## Heightened Legal Protections — Across All Vulnerable Populations

Regardless of which specific category of vulnerability applies, the following legal safeguards must be present in every protocol involving a vulnerable population.

<Steps>
  <Step title="Justification for Including the Vulnerable Population">
    The protocol must explicitly justify why members of the vulnerable population are included as participants. Research that includes vulnerable participants merely because they are accessible (e.g., hospitalized patients, institutionalized individuals) without a specific justification related to the research question does not meet TCPS2 standards. Confirm this justification is documented.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Proportionate Risk to Anticipated Benefit">
    Apply a stricter risk-benefit analysis than you would for a general adult population. For non-therapeutic research involving vulnerable participants (research with no direct benefit to the individual), the risk must be truly minimal. Greater-than-minimal risk is only acceptable where the research offers direct therapeutic benefit that is at least as favorable as available alternatives.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Enhanced Consent Safeguards">
    The protocol must describe additional consent safeguards appropriate to the population: capacity assessment processes, SDM identification and training, cooling-off periods before enrollment, independent consent witnesses, or the involvement of an independent patient advocate who is not a member of the research team.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Independent Monitoring">
    Protocols involving vulnerable populations benefit from — and in some cases require — enhanced monitoring of the consent process by an independent monitor or Data Safety Monitoring Board (DSMB). Look for this in the protocol for any study that enrolls participants with impaired capacity or in coercive settings.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Withdrawal Protections">
    The withdrawal rights described in the consent documentation must be operationally realistic for the population. For a cognitively impaired participant, this means the SDM must also be informed of and able to exercise withdrawal rights. For an incarcerated participant, withdrawal must carry no institutional consequences.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Privacy and Confidentiality — Enhanced Requirements">
    Vulnerable populations often face heightened consequences from a privacy breach — stigma, loss of custody, immigration consequences, employment consequences. The data security and confidentiality provisions of the protocol must be assessed with these specific harms in mind, not merely against a general privacy standard.
  </Step>
</Steps>

## Acceptable vs. Unacceptable Study Designs — Quick Reference

<CardGroup cols={2}>
  <Card title="✅ Acceptable Designs" icon="circle-check">
    - Research that cannot be answered without including the specific vulnerable population and addresses health issues specific to that population
    - Minimal-risk observational studies with enhanced consent safeguards
    - Clinical trials offering potential direct therapeutic benefit where risk is proportionate and informed consent (or valid SDM consent) is obtained
    - Studies with independent consent monitors, DSMB oversight, and documented capacity assessment protocols
    - Longitudinal studies with built-in re-consent at each phase
  </Card>

  <Card title="🚩 Unacceptable Designs" icon="circle-xmark">
    - Research using vulnerable populations as a convenient enrollment pool for questions that could be answered using non-vulnerable participants
    - Studies with greater than minimal risk, no direct benefit, and no enhanced safeguards
    - Protocols where the researcher holds authority over participants and no structural safeguards separate the authority relationship from the research relationship
    - Consent processes that rely on a single signed form obtained at a moment of institutional admission without independent capacity assessment
    - Studies where withdrawal carries — or appears to carry — institutional, academic, employment, or clinical consequences
  </Card>
</CardGroup>

<Tip>
  When you identify a protocol involving a vulnerable population that raises legal concerns, document your specific concerns in writing to the REB chair before the full REB review meeting. Vulnerable population protocols benefit from advance legal analysis that the full REB can consider alongside the scientific and ethics review. Verbal observations at the meeting are less effective and less auditable than a written memorandum.
</Tip>
