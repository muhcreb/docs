Under the *Tri-Council Policy Statement: Ethical Conduct for Research Involving Humans* (TCPS2), every research submission involving human participants must be assigned to one of three review levels before any review work begins. The review level determines who reviews the submission, what procedural rules apply, and what authority is needed to issue a binding REB decision. As a legal representative, you must understand these levels not just to fulfil your assigned review role, but because you will sometimes be the person who identifies that a submission has been misclassified and that escalation is warranted.

<Warning>
  The **default** position under TCPS2 is that research involving human participants receives **full board review** unless the REB Chair (or designate) affirmatively determines that the submission qualifies for a lower level. If you are uncertain whether a submission meets the criteria for delegated or administrative-only review, apply the more protective standard and flag it for Chair review. Misclassifying a study as delegated when it warrants full board review is a TCPS2 compliance failure and can expose the institution to regulatory and reputational risk.
</Warning>

## The Three TCPS2 Review Levels

<Tabs>
  <Tab title="Full Board Review">
    ### What It Is

    Full board review is the highest level of REB scrutiny. The entire convened board — including the legal representative, scientific members, community members, and the chair — deliberates collectively and reaches a decision by consensus or, where consensus is not achievable, by recorded vote. A quorum of board members must be present, and the board must include at least one member with no affiliation to the institution.

    Full board review is conducted at regularly scheduled board meetings, or at a specially convened meeting when urgency demands it.

    ### When Full Board Review Is Required

    Under TCPS2 Article 6.11 and your institution's SOPs, the following study characteristics **require** full board review:

    | Characteristic | Why Full Board Is Required |
    |---|---|
    | **More than minimal risk** | Elevated potential for physical, psychological, social, or economic harm to participants requires collective board judgment, not a single reviewer. |
    | **Vulnerable populations** | Studies involving children and minors, individuals with diminished decision-making capacity, prisoners, persons in dependent relationships, or Indigenous communities require the board's full deliberative oversight. |
    | **Deception or incomplete disclosure** | Any study that withholds information from participants or uses deceptive procedures requires full board consideration of the justification and the debriefing plan. |
    | **Waiver or alteration of consent** | Requests to waive or substantially alter the standard consent process require full board authorization under TCPS2 Article 3.7A. |
    | **Novel, unresolved ethical or legal issues** | Research at the frontier of established ethics norms — first-in-human studies, germline editing, studies implicating constitutional rights — warrants collective deliberation. |
    | **Significant conflicts of interest** | When a PI or sponsor relationship raises conflicts that cannot be managed procedurally, the full board must assess whether the research should proceed. |
    | **Multi-jurisdictional or international studies** | Research involving participants or data across multiple legal jurisdictions introduces regulatory complexity requiring full board review. |

    ### Your Role as Legal Representative

    At full board review, you function as a full voting member of the deliberating body. Your responsibilities include:

    <Steps>
      <Step title="Pre-Meeting Written Report">
        Submit a written legal review report to the REB Coordinator before the meeting deadline. Your report should identify specific legal concerns, reference the applicable statute or regulatory provision, and propose concrete conditions or questions for the PI. Vague observations ("some privacy issues may exist") do not create a useful record.
      </Step>
      <Step title="Oral Presentation at the Meeting">
        Present your findings to the board clearly and without legal jargon. Translate legal requirements into plain language so that community members and scientific reviewers can meaningfully engage with the issues. Distinguish between issues that are legal requirements (non-negotiable) and issues where you are offering best-practice guidance (advisable but negotiable).
      </Step>
      <Step title="Deliberation and Voting">
        Participate in board deliberation. You may vote on the overall study approval, not merely on the legal components. Declare and manage any conflict of interest before deliberation begins, and document your declaration in the meeting minutes.
      </Step>
      <Step title="Decision Letter Review">
        Before the formal decision letter is sent to the PI, review any conditions that originate from your legal analysis to ensure they are drafted with sufficient precision. Ambiguous conditions generate unnecessary back-and-forth and create documentation gaps.
      </Step>
    </Steps>
  </Tab>

  <Tab title="Delegated Review">
    ### What It Is

    Delegated review authorizes one or more individual board members — designated by the REB Chair — to conduct the review and issue an REB decision on behalf of the full board, without convening a meeting. The delegated reviewer has the same authority as the full board to approve, impose conditions, or request clarifications, but **cannot reject** a study outright; a study that cannot be approved must be referred to the full board.

    Delegated review is faster than full board review and is appropriate for lower-risk submissions where a comprehensive individual review is sufficient to protect participant interests.

    ### When Delegated Review Is Permitted

    Under TCPS2 Article 6.12, delegated review is permitted for studies that present **no more than minimal risk** to participants. A study presents minimal risk when the probability and magnitude of possible harms implied by participation are not greater than those encountered in ordinary daily life or routine physical or psychological examinations.

    Common categories eligible for delegated review include:

    <CardGroup cols={2}>
      <Card title="Amendments to Approved Studies" icon="file-pen" href="/review/amendments">
        Minor amendments that do not increase risk, do not affect vulnerable populations, and do not change the consent process are routinely handled by delegated review.
      </Card>
      <Card title="Annual Renewals (Minimal Risk Studies)" icon="calendar-check" href="/review/overview">
        Continuing review of studies originally approved as minimal risk, where no new safety signals or adverse events are outstanding.
      </Card>
      <Card title="Surveys and Interviews (Non-Sensitive)" icon="clipboard-question" href="/review/submission-types">
        Anonymous or confidential surveys and qualitative interviews with non-vulnerable adult populations on non-sensitive topics.
      </Card>
      <Card title="Secondary Use of Anonymized Data" icon="database" href="/review/privacy-and-data">
        Studies involving anonymized or de-identified datasets where re-identification risk has been assessed and addressed, and where no new data collection from participants occurs.
      </Card>
    </CardGroup>

    ### Your Role as Legal Representative

    When you are assigned as a delegated reviewer, you act on the full board's behalf. Your responsibilities are the same in substance as in full board review — written assessment, identification of conditions, and condition clearance — but the procedural context is different:

    - You issue the decision directly (subject to the Chair's confirmation under institutional SOPs) rather than contributing to a collective vote.
    - You must recognize the **limits of your delegated authority**: if your review surfaces issues that warrant full board consideration, stop and refer the submission upward rather than proceeding to a decision.
    - Document your review rationale as if the full board will audit it — because it may.

    <Tip>
      Many institutions assign a legal representative as a **second delegated reviewer** on submissions that contain legal documents (e.g., a minimal-risk survey study sponsored by a commercial entity that includes a data-use agreement). Even if the primary delegated reviewer is a scientific member, you review the contractual and privacy components independently and your clearance is required before the decision issues.
    </Tip>

    ### Escalation from Delegated to Full Board Review

    A delegated reviewer **must** refer a submission to the full board when they encounter any of the following during their review:

    <Accordion title="Risk Level Is Higher Than Initially Assessed">
      The submission was classified as minimal risk at screening, but your review reveals that the actual risk to participants — physical, psychological, privacy-related, or economic — is greater than minimal. Trust your assessment over the classification label.
    </Accordion>

    <Accordion title="Consent Waiver or Alteration Is Requested">
      Any explicit or implicit request to waive or alter the standard consent process — even if framed as a minor amendment — requires full board authority under TCPS2 Article 3.7A.
    </Accordion>

    <Accordion title="A Vulnerable Population Is Identified">
      Screening may not have recognized that a study involves a vulnerable group. If your review reveals that participants are minors, persons with diminished capacity, prisoners, or individuals in a dependent relationship with the investigator, escalate immediately.
    </Accordion>

    <Accordion title="Novel Legal or Ethical Issue Arises">
      The submission raises a legal or ethical question for which there is no established institutional precedent — for example, a new type of data-sharing arrangement, a study design implicating Indigenous data sovereignty, or a consent issue arising from a novel digital health technology.
    </Accordion>

    <Accordion title="Significant Conflict of Interest Is Identified">
      Review of sponsor agreements or co-investigator disclosures reveals a conflict of interest that cannot be managed through standard procedural controls. The full board must assess whether the research can proceed.
    </Accordion>

    <Accordion title="The Delegated Reviewer Cannot Approve the Study">
      If your review leads you to conclude that the study should not be approved — as opposed to approved with conditions or approved after clarification — you must refer the submission to the full board. Delegated reviewers cannot issue a rejection.
    </Accordion>
  </Tab>

  <Tab title="Administrative Review Only">
    ### What It Is

    Some activities that superficially resemble research do not require REB review because they do not meet the TCPS2 definition of research involving human participants, or because they are explicitly excluded from TCPS2 scope. These activities receive an **administrative determination** — sometimes called a "no REB review required" determination — issued by the REB Chair or REB Coordinator under authority delegated by the Chair.

    An administrative determination is not an approval of the activity; it is a confirmation that the activity falls outside the REB's jurisdiction under TCPS2.

    ### Activities That Typically Fall in This Category

    | Activity | Basis for Exclusion |
    |---|---|
    | **Quality improvement (QI) projects** | QI projects designed solely to improve local service delivery, with no intent to generalize findings beyond the institution, are excluded from TCPS2 scope under Article 2.5. |
    | **Program evaluation** | Evaluations of existing institutional programs, where data are not intended for publication or generalization, typically fall outside TCPS2 scope. |
    | **Journalistic, literary, or artistic inquiry** | Activities that constitute journalism, biography, or artistic creation using publicly available information are excluded. |
    | **Use of truly anonymous existing data** | Secondary use of data that was collected anonymously and can never be linked back to individuals does not constitute research involving human participants. |
    | **Case reports (with consent)** | Single case reports published with the explicit consent of the subject are handled by institutional policy and do not typically require full REB review. |

    <Info>
      The QI/research boundary is one of the most frequently litigated questions in Canadian research governance. An activity that begins as a QI project but generates findings intended for publication, generalization, or policy change crosses into research and requires REB review. If a submission is presented as QI but includes a prospective data collection protocol, a comparator group, or an intention to publish, flag it for a formal TCPS2 scoping determination by the REB Chair.
    </Info>

    ### Your Role as Legal Representative

    Your direct involvement in administrative-level determinations is typically limited. However, you should be engaged when:

    - The activity involves collection of personal health information, even if the PI asserts it falls outside research scope — provincial health privacy legislation applies regardless of TCPS2 classification.
    - A QI or program evaluation project is sponsored by a commercial entity or involves a contractual arrangement that creates institutional obligations.
    - The determination is being sought in connection with a publication or regulatory submission, and the institution's "no REB review required" letter will be cited externally.

    <Note>
      A "no REB review required" determination does not release the PI from obligations under provincial privacy legislation, institutional data governance policies, or professional regulatory standards. If the PI appears to be using the administrative determination as a shield against compliance obligations, escalate to the REB Chair.
    </Note>
  </Tab>
</Tabs>

## Review Level Decision Framework

Use this decision table to quickly assess the appropriate review level for an incoming submission. The table summarizes the key criteria; always defer to TCPS2, your institutional SOP, and the REB Chair's classification authority.

| Criterion | Full Board | Delegated | Administrative Only |
|---|:---:|:---:|:---:|
| More than minimal risk to participants | ✅ Required | ✗ | ✗ |
| Vulnerable population involved | ✅ Required | ✗ | ✗ |
| Waiver or alteration of consent requested | ✅ Required | ✗ | ✗ |
| Deception or incomplete disclosure | ✅ Required | ✗ | ✗ |
| Novel legal or ethical question | ✅ Required | ✗ | ✗ |
| Significant, unmanageable conflict of interest | ✅ Required | ✗ | ✗ |
| Minimal risk, no vulnerable populations | Permissible | ✅ Appropriate | ✗ |
| Minor amendment to approved minimal-risk study | Permissible | ✅ Appropriate | ✗ |
| Annual renewal, no outstanding safety issues | Permissible | ✅ Appropriate | ✗ |
| QI / program evaluation, no publication intent | ✗ | ✗ | ✅ Appropriate |
| Truly anonymous secondary data use | ✗ | ✗ | ✅ Appropriate |
| Delegated reviewer cannot approve the study | ✅ Required (referral) | — | ✗ |

<Info>
  This table is a decision-support aid, not a definitive classification algorithm. Every submission presents unique facts. When in doubt, apply the more protective standard and seek the REB Chair's guidance on classification.
</Info>
