As a legal representative on a Research Ethics Board (REB), your primary obligation during consent review is to determine whether a proposed consent process and its accompanying documents meet the legal threshold for valid, enforceable consent under Canadian law and the Tri-Council Policy Statement: Ethical Conduct for Research Involving Humans (TCPS2). A technically accurate document is not enough — the process and language must genuinely enable autonomous, informed decision-making by every prospective participant.

## The Four Pillars of Valid Consent

Canadian law and the TCPS2 recognize four conditions that must be simultaneously satisfied for research consent to be legally valid.

<CardGroup cols={2}>
  <Card title="Capacity" icon="brain">
    The participant must have the legal and cognitive capacity to make a research decision at the time of consent. Capacity is decision-specific and time-specific — it is not an all-or-nothing status.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Voluntariness" icon="hand">
    Consent must be given free from manipulation, coercion, undue influence, or inducement. This is particularly sensitive when researchers hold authority over participants (e.g., clinicians, employers, teachers).
  </Card>

  <Card title="Disclosure" icon="file-lines">
    Participants must receive all information a reasonable person in their circumstances would need to make a decision, including risks, benefits, alternatives, and the right to withdraw.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Comprehension" icon="magnifying-glass">
    Information must be presented in plain language at an accessible reading level, free from technical jargon, and offered with an opportunity to ask questions before signing.
  </Card>
</CardGroup>

<Note>
  The TCPS2 (Chapter 3) treats ongoing consent as a process, not a one-time signature event. Legally, consent can be withdrawn at any time without penalty, and participants must be informed of this right clearly and prominently in the consent form itself.
</Note>

## TCPS2 Documentation Requirements

The TCPS2 and most provincial REB policies require that consent documentation include specific elements. As a legal reviewer, verify that every consent form contains all of the following before approving it.

<Steps>
  <Step title="Study Purpose and Nature">
    Confirm the form clearly describes the research objectives, the experimental nature of any interventions, and explicitly states this is a research study — not standard clinical care. Look for language that blurs the line between treatment and research, which creates therapeutic misconception risk.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Identity of Researchers and Sponsor">
    The consent form must identify the principal investigator, the institution, any sponsor or funder, and a contact person for research-related questions. Verify that the REB contact for complaints is distinct from the research team contact — regulatory guidance and TCPS2 Article 3.2(h) require this separation.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Description of Procedures">
    All research procedures must be clearly distinguished from standard-of-care procedures. The form must state which procedures are experimental, their duration, and their frequency. Vague or generic descriptions are a legal deficiency.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Risks, Discomforts, and Benefits">
    Foreseeable risks and discomforts — physical, psychological, financial, social, and legal — must be disclosed with sufficient detail. Benefits must not be overstated. Confirm there is no language that guarantees clinical benefit from participation.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Alternatives to Participation">
    Participants must be told about alternatives to participation, particularly in clinical trials where an approved therapy exists. Omitting available alternatives is a common legal deficiency with significant liability implications.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Confidentiality and Privacy">
    The form must describe how data will be collected, stored, used, and who will have access to it, including sponsors, Health Canada, and foreign regulatory authorities if applicable. See the Privacy & Data page for full detail.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Compensation and Costs">
    Any payments or reimbursements for participation must be disclosed. The form must also identify whether any costs will be incurred by the participant. Compensation must not be so substantial as to constitute undue inducement.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Voluntary Participation and Right to Withdraw">
    The form must explicitly state that participation is voluntary, that refusal will have no consequences, and that withdrawal at any time will not affect the participant's access to care. This language must be unambiguous — see the Warning below.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Contact Information">
    Provide the research team contact for questions about the study, and a separate, independent REB contact for concerns or complaints. Both must be current and accessible.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Signature Block">
    The signature block must include space for the participant's name, signature, and date. For studies using ongoing consent processes, document how re-consent will be sought if material protocol changes occur.
  </Step>
</Steps>

<Warning>
  **Legally Invalid Consent Language — Flag Immediately**

  The following types of language render a consent form legally deficient and must be flagged to the REB for correction before approval:

  - **Liability waivers**: Any language stating the participant waives the right to seek compensation for research-related injury (e.g., "I release the hospital from all claims…") is void and unenforceable in Canada, and its inclusion may invalidate the entire consent form.
  - **Coercive phrasing**: Language such as "You must complete all study visits" or "Withdrawal may affect your medical care" constitutes coercion and undermines voluntary consent.
  - **Guarantees of benefit**: Statements such as "This treatment will improve your condition" or "You will benefit from participation" are legally problematic misrepresentations.
  - **Excessive inducement**: Compensation structured to make withdrawal economically irrational (e.g., full payment only upon study completion) may compromise voluntariness.
  - **All-caps or boilerplate disclaimers**: Standardized legal release language imported from commercial contracts has no place in a research consent form and must be removed.
</Warning>

## Legal Review Checklist

Use this checklist when formally reviewing any consent document submitted to the REB.

<Steps>
  <Step title="Plain Language Assessment">
    Read the form as a layperson. Target a Grade 6–8 reading level. Flag passive constructions, technical acronyms, and medical jargon that are not explained. Request a readability score if the study involves a vulnerable population.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Scan for Prohibited Language">
    Search specifically for liability waivers, coercive phrasing, benefit guarantees, and overly legalistic boilerplate. Cross-reference against the Warning above.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Verify Structural Completeness">
    Confirm all ten TCPS2 documentation elements are present (see the Steps section above). A single missing element is sufficient grounds to request revisions.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Check Sponsor and Regulatory Disclosures">
    For industry-sponsored trials, verify that the sponsor's right to access data and the role of regulatory authorities (Health Canada, FDA if applicable) are clearly disclosed.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Assess Consistency with Protocol">
    Compare the consent form against the study protocol. Inconsistencies between stated procedures, risks, or exclusion criteria in the protocol versus the consent form are a significant legal and ethical concern.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Confirm Translation and Accessibility">
    If the target population includes non-English or non-French speakers, verify that certified translations have been provided or are planned. Confirm that oral consent procedures are documented where written consent is not feasible.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Review the Re-Consent Plan">
    Ensure the protocol includes a mechanism for re-consenting participants if the protocol is amended in ways that materially affect risk or procedures.
  </Step>
</Steps>

## Special Cases

<Accordion title="Consent by Substitute Decision Makers (SDMs)">
  When a prospective participant lacks capacity, a substitute may consent on their behalf, subject to the applicable statutory framework. In Québec, consent to research on a person incapable of giving consent is governed by **art. 21 of the _Civil Code of Québec_**, which requires REB approval by an MSSS-designated REB, proportionality of risk to expected benefit, and consent by the mandatary (under a protection mandate), tutor, or curator — with the participant's own assent where they are able to understand.

  **Key legal requirements for SDM consent:**

  - The SDM must be authorized under applicable provincial legislation. Confirm the protocol specifies which hierarchy of decision makers is acceptable (spouse/partner, adult child, parent, sibling, or other authorized person).
  - The SDM must consent in the participant's **best interests**, taking into account the participant's prior expressed wishes, values, and beliefs.
  - Even where an SDM provides consent, the participant should provide **assent** (agreement without formal legal consent) if they are able to express a meaningful preference. The protocol must describe how assent will be sought.
  - The consent form must include a **parallel SDM signature block** — not merely an adaptation of the participant's block. The SDM's relationship to the participant and their authority must be documented.
  - If a previously incapacitated participant **regains capacity**, they must be re-consented directly.

  <Warning>
    A consent form that only provides for the participant's own signature, without an SDM provision, is legally insufficient for any study that enrolls individuals who may lack capacity. This includes studies involving dementia, acquired brain injury, delirium, or acute psychiatric conditions.
  </Warning>
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Assent and Parental Consent for Minors">
  Research involving individuals under 16 years of age (or 18 in some provinces) requires parental or guardian consent and, where developmentally appropriate, the minor's assent.

  **Legal framework:**

  - **Parental consent**: Both parents typically must consent unless one parent has sole custody, is unavailable, or provincial legislation permits single-parent consent. The consent form must include a parental signature block.
  - **Assent of the minor**: TCPS2 Article 4.6 requires that researchers obtain assent from minors where they are capable of expressing a meaningful preference, even if they cannot legally consent. The assent form must be written in age-appropriate language.
  - **Dissent must be respected**: A minor's refusal to participate or to continue in a study must be respected unless the research offers direct therapeutic benefit unavailable outside the study and the parents and REB concur that participation is in the minor's best interests.
  - **Mature minors**: Under the common law doctrine of the "mature minor," a minor who demonstrates sufficient understanding and judgment to make a particular health decision may consent independently. The protocol should address how mature minor status will be assessed. In Quebec, the Civil Code provisions apply (generally age 14\+).

  <Tip>
    Request that the research team provide both a **parent/guardian consent form** and a separate age-stratified **assent form** (often one for ages 7–12 and one for ages 13–17). A single document that attempts to serve all purposes is generally inadequate.
  </Tip>
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Waiver of Consent — Legal Criteria and Risks">
  Under TCPS2 Article 3.7, an REB may authorize a waiver or alteration of the consent requirement only if all of the following conditions are met:

  1. The research involves no more than minimal risk.
  2. The waiver is unlikely to adversely affect the welfare of the participants.
  3. It is impossible or impracticable to conduct the research and to obtain consent.
  4. Wherever possible and appropriate, participants will be debriefed after the study.

  **Legal review responsibilities:**

  - Assess whether the researcher has genuinely demonstrated impracticability — a claim that obtaining consent is merely "inconvenient" or "would reduce sample size" does not meet this threshold.
  - Determine whether the study qualifies as involving **minimal risk**. Research involving access to identifiable health records, even without direct contact with participants, may carry meaningful privacy risk that disqualifies it from minimal-risk status.
  - Confirm that a **debriefing plan** is included where feasible, including a mechanism through which participants may opt out of data use after learning of the study.
  - Verify that the waiver has been documented as a formal REB decision, not merely approved by omission.

  <Note>
    A waiver of **written** consent (i.e., permitting oral or electronic consent) is distinct from a waiver of the **consent process** itself. Ensure the researcher's request clearly identifies which type of waiver they are seeking.
  </Note>
</Accordion>

<Info>
  **Ongoing Consent Obligations**: For longitudinal studies, studies involving genetic material, or studies where new risks emerge over time, remind the REB that the consent process must include a clear re-consent mechanism. A single signed consent form does not create perpetual authority to use a participant's data or biological samples for purposes not originally disclosed.
</Info>
