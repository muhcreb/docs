When an REB completes its review of a research protocol, it issues one of several formal decisions. As a legal representative on the board, you play a critical role both in shaping deliberations and in ensuring that the decision and any attached conditions are legally sound, clearly worded, and enforceable. Understanding each decision type — and its downstream legal consequences — is essential to fulfilling that role effectively.

## Decision Outcomes at a Glance

The table below summarises the six standard decision outcomes, their typical triggers, and the immediate effect on research activity.

| Decision | Research May Proceed? | Researcher Action Required | REB Follow-up Required |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Approved** | Yes, immediately | None (maintain compliance) | Continuing review at set interval |
| **Approved with Conditions** | Only after conditions met | Satisfy conditions; provide evidence | Confirm condition satisfaction |
| **Modifications Required (Minor)** | No | Revise and resubmit within set timeframe | Chair or delegate review |
| **Modifications Required (Major)** | No | Substantial revision; full resubmission | Full board re-review |
| **Deferred** | No | Respond to outstanding questions | Full board re-review |
| **Rejected / Disapproved** | No | Appeal or withdraw | Appeal committee review (if triggered) |

## Decision Types in Detail

<Tabs>
  <Tab title="Approved">
    ### Approved

    An **Approved** decision means the REB is satisfied that the protocol meets all ethical requirements under TCPS2, applicable provincial legislation, and institutional policy. Research may commence immediately upon issuance of the written approval letter.

    **What triggers this outcome:**

    - Risks to participants are minimised and reasonable in relation to anticipated benefits
    - Informed consent processes are free, informed, and ongoing
    - Participant selection is equitable and justified
    - Privacy and data security measures are adequate
    - No unresolved legal, regulatory, or liability concerns

    **Your legal contribution:**

    - Confirm that contractual documents (Clinical Trial Agreements, Data Sharing Agreements) are either already executed or will be executed before the first participant is enrolled
    - Verify that sponsor indemnification provisions are adequate
    - Ensure that any jurisdiction-specific regulatory authorisations (e.g., Health Canada CTA for Phase I–III drug trials) are in place or appropriately conditioned

    <Note>
      Approval is time-limited. Under TCPS2 Article 6.14, every approved study requires **continuing review** at intervals proportionate to the level of risk. Record the renewal date in your legal calendar.
    </Note>
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Approved with Conditions">
    ### Approved with Conditions

    The board approves the study in principle but identifies discrete issues that must be resolved before or during the conduct of research. Conditions must be **specific, verifiable, and proportionate** to the concern that prompted them.

    **What triggers this outcome:**

    - Consent form language requires minor clarification
    - A data-sharing or confidentiality clause needs tightening
    - Sponsor indemnification language is ambiguous but correctable
    - A regulatory submission is pending and must be confirmed before enrolment

    **Your legal contribution:**

    - Draft conditions in precise legal language that leaves no room for ambiguous compliance
    - Distinguish between **pre-enrolment conditions** (must be met before any participant is recruited) and **ongoing conditions** (must be maintained throughout the study)
    - Advise the board on how evidence of satisfaction will be assessed — e.g., a revised consent form bearing the REB stamp, a countersigned amendment to the CTA

    **Satisfying conditions:** The principal investigator (PI) responds in writing. The board chair or a delegate reviews the response. If conditions are met, an updated approval letter issues. If not, the file returns to the full board.

    <Warning>
      A study must not enrol participants until all **pre-enrolment conditions** are documented as satisfied in writing by the REB. Premature enrolment may constitute a protocol deviation with regulatory and liability consequences.
    </Warning>
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Modifications Required">
    ### Modifications Required

    This decision signals that the protocol cannot be approved as submitted but does not constitute a rejection. The distinction between **minor** and **major** modifications determines the review pathway.

    #### Minor Modifications

    - Confined to specific sections (e.g., consent form wording, a single recruitment procedure)
    - Do not alter the fundamental design, risk profile, or participant population
    - Typically reviewed by the chair or a designated reviewer after resubmission
    - Turnaround: usually 10–15 business days

    #### Major Modifications

    - Affect the study design, statistical plan, risk-benefit profile, or study population
    - May introduce new legal obligations (e.g., expanded data collection triggering additional privacy obligations under Law 5, Law 25, or PIPEDA)
    - Require full board re-review at the next convened meeting
    - Turnaround: tied to the board's meeting schedule

    **Your legal contribution:**

    - Identify whether any proposed modification expands the study's regulatory footprint (e.g., addition of a new drug arm triggers a CTA amendment with Health Canada)
    - Flag modifications that affect indemnification or liability allocation between sponsor and institution
    - Ensure that the written decision letter clearly distinguishes which modifications are minor vs. major so the PI understands the applicable review pathway

    <Tip>
      When modifications touch consent forms, confirm that the revised form will be **re-stamped** with the new REB approval date before distribution to participants. Consent obtained on an outdated form version is legally deficient.
    </Tip>
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Deferred">
    ### Deferred

    A **Deferred** decision is issued when the board requires additional information before it can reach a substantive decision. Unlike a modifications-required decision, deferral signals an information gap rather than an identified deficiency.

    **What triggers this outcome:**

    - The application is incomplete (missing regulatory authorisations, unsigned agreements)
    - The board requires external expert input (e.g., a biostatistician's independent assessment)
    - Material questions about legal jurisdiction or applicable law remain unresolved
    - A conflict-of-interest issue requires further investigation before the board can deliberate

    **Your legal contribution:**

    - Help the board articulate precisely what information is needed so the PI's response is targeted and complete
    - Advise whether any identified information gap has legal significance (e.g., absence of a Health Canada No Objection Letter for a Phase I drug trial)
    - Where a conflict-of-interest concern has been raised, advise on the institution's COI policy and whether the file must be referred to a separate committee before REB review resumes

    <Info>
      A deferred decision is not a negative finding on the ethics of the protocol. Communicate this clearly in the decision letter to avoid unnecessary harm to the research program or the PI's reputation.
    </Info>
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Rejected / Disapproved">
    ### Rejected / Disapproved

    Rejection is the most serious REB outcome. It means the board has determined that the study, as submitted and considered, cannot be ethically justified. Research may not proceed.

    **What triggers this outcome:**

    - Risks to participants are unreasonable and cannot be mitigated
    - The study design is scientifically invalid, rendering any risk unjustifiable
    - Consent is structurally impossible to obtain in a meaningful way
    - The study involves prohibited research under law or institutional policy
    - Repeated or material non-compliance with previous conditions

    **Your legal contribution:**

    - Ensure the rejection letter articulates specific, factual grounds — vague reasoning creates grounds for a successful appeal
    - Advise on the institution's duty to document the decision and retain records in accordance with TCPS2 and applicable legislation
    - Where the rejection touches on regulatory non-compliance (e.g., an unapproved device or drug), advise whether a separate regulatory referral is required

    <Warning>
      A rejected study may not be re-submitted as a new protocol in an attempt to circumvent the board's findings. Such a submission would constitute a breach of research integrity obligations and may trigger institutional disciplinary proceedings.
    </Warning>
  </Tab>
</Tabs>

## The Legal Representative's Role in Deliberations

Before the board votes on a decision, you contribute at several points in the deliberation:

<Steps>
  <Step title="Pre-Meeting Review">
    Read the full submission package, including the protocol, consent forms, investigator brochure, and all agreements. Flag legal issues in writing to the REB Coordinator before the meeting so they can be added to the discussion agenda.
  </Step>
  <Step title="During Deliberation">
    Raise legal risk points clearly and succinctly. Distinguish between issues that are **determinative** (the board cannot approve without resolution) and those that are **advisory** (conditions that reduce risk but do not block approval). Avoid dominating clinical or scientific deliberations outside your expertise.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Formulating Conditions">
    When the board moves toward an "Approved with Conditions" outcome, offer to draft or refine the condition language. Precise legal drafting reduces the likelihood of a PI satisfying a condition in a way that does not actually address the underlying concern.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Decision Letter Review">
    Before the decision letter is issued, review the final text. Confirm that the stated grounds are accurate, the conditions are enforceable, and the researcher's response options are clearly described.
  </Step>
</Steps>

## Researcher Response Options After a Non-Approval Decision

<CardGroup cols={2}>
  <Card title="Respond and Resubmit" icon="rotate-right">
    The PI addresses the board's concerns and resubmits. This is the most common pathway and does not require the PI to agree with the board's findings — only to satisfy them. No formal appeal is triggered.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Request Reconsideration" icon="magnifying-glass">
    Before lodging a formal appeal, the PI may request that the board reconsider a specific finding based on new information or a perceived factual error. This is an informal step available at most institutions.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Formal Appeal" icon="scale-balanced">
    Under TCPS2 Article 6.20, researchers have the right to appeal an REB decision to an independent body. The appeal body must include members who were not part of the original review. Legal reps typically do not serve on appeal panels for decisions in which they participated.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Withdraw the Application" icon="xmark">
    The PI may withdraw the application entirely, particularly if the study will not be pursued or will be substantially redesigned before any future submission. Withdrawals should be acknowledged in writing by the REB.
  </Card>
</CardGroup>

## Timelines

<Accordion title="Standard Decision Communication Timelines">
  Most institutional REBs operate under internal policies that set timelines for communicating decisions. Typical benchmarks are:

  - **Written decision letter:** issued within **5–10 business days** of the meeting at which the decision was made
  - **Condition response review:** chair/delegate review within **10 business days** of the PI's response
  - **Major modification re-review:** at the **next scheduled full board meeting** (typically monthly)
  - **Appeal initiation deadline:** PI must lodge an appeal within **30 days** of receiving the written decision letter (verify against your institution's SOP)
  - **Appeal decision:** within **60 days** of receipt of the appeal, per most institutional policies

  Review your establishment's SOP and any applicable Québec legislation (e.g., the _Civil Code of Québec_ for capacity-related decisions and the MSSS framework for designated REBs) for binding timelines that may differ from these benchmarks.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Continuing Review Intervals by Risk Level">
  TCPS2 Article 6.14 requires the REB to set a continuing review interval at the time of initial approval. Typical intervals are:

  | Risk Level | Recommended Interval |
  | --- | --- |
  | Greater than minimal risk | Annually or more frequently |
  | Minimal risk | Up to every two years |
  | No participant contact (secondary data only) | Up to every three years, or as determined by the board |

  As legal representative, note that lapsed approval is a protocol deviation and may have regulatory consequences for sponsor-investigators under the _Food and Drug Regulations_.
</Accordion>

## Appeal Rights Under TCPS2

<Note>
  **TCPS2 Article 6.20** explicitly protects a researcher's right to appeal an REB decision. Key legal points for the board to observe:

  1. The appeal body must be **independent** of the REB that issued the original decision
  2. Appeal panel members must not have **participated** in the original review
  3. The appeal process must be **documented** in the institution's REB SOP and communicated to researchers at the time of a non-approval decision
  4. The existence and outcome of an appeal must be **recorded** in the research file

  Failure to provide a compliant appeal pathway may expose the institution to procedural fairness challenges and could undermine the enforceability of the original REB decision. Advise your institution's REB office to review its appeal procedures against TCPS2 requirements at least every three years.
</Note>
