Adverse event (AE) reporting sits at the intersection of participant safety, regulatory compliance, and institutional liability. When an AE report lands before the REB, the board is not conducting a clinical investigation — that function belongs to the sponsor and Health Canada. The REB's task is narrower but legally significant: to determine whether the reported event, or a pattern of events, requires a change to the approved protocol, the consent form, or the conduct of the study. As a legal representative, you contribute a layer of analysis that clinicians may overlook — examining whether regulatory obligations have been met, whether the institution's liability exposure has changed, and whether the consent process remains adequate in light of new safety information.

## Types of Adverse Events

<Accordion title="Adverse Event (AE)">
  Any **untoward medical occurrence** in a participant administered a study intervention, regardless of whether there is a causal relationship with that intervention. AEs include:

  - Signs, symptoms, or laboratory abnormalities that are temporally associated with study participation
  - Worsening of a pre-existing condition during the study
  - Intercurrent illness

  Not all AEs require expedited reporting to the REB. Many are collected in the study's safety database and reported in aggregate at continuing review.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Serious Adverse Event (SAE)">
  An AE is **serious** if it results in any of the following:

  - **Death**
  - **Life-threatening** condition (places the participant at immediate risk of death)
  - **Inpatient hospitalisation** or prolongation of existing hospitalisation
  - **Persistent or significant disability or incapacity**
  - **Congenital anomaly or birth defect**
  - A **medically important event** that may jeopardise the participant and may require medical or surgical intervention to prevent one of the above outcomes

  SAEs require expedited reporting to the REB and — for regulated trials — to Health Canada.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Unexpected Adverse Drug Reaction (UADR) / Suspected Unexpected Serious Adverse Reaction (SUSAR)">
  An SAE is **unexpected** when its nature or severity is **not consistent** with the current approved product information (e.g., the investigator brochure or product monograph). A SUSAR is a UADR that is both serious and unexpected, and for which there is a **reasonable causal relationship** to the study drug.

  SUSARs carry the most stringent regulatory reporting timelines. For **fatal or life-threatening** SUSARs, Health Canada must be notified within **7 calendar days** of the sponsor's first awareness, with a full report due within **15 calendar days**. For all other SUSARs, the full report is due within **15 calendar days**.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Unexpected Adverse Device Effect (UADE)">
  For medical device studies under the *Food and Drug Regulations* and the *Medical Devices Regulations*, a UADE is any adverse effect on health or safety that is **not identified in nature, severity, or rate** in the current risk assessment for the device. UADEs must be reported to Health Canada on the same timelines as SUSARs for drug trials.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Unanticipated Problem Involving Risk (UPIR)">
  A broader category used in many institutional policies: any incident, experience, or outcome that is:

  1. **Unexpected** — not described in the protocol, consent form, or risk assessment
  2. **Related or possibly related** to participation in the research
  3. **Suggests** that participants or others are at greater risk than previously recognised

  UPIRs include non-AE incidents such as a data breach, a confidentiality violation, or a coercion complaint.
</Accordion>

## Health Canada Reporting Requirements

<Warning>
  The following timelines are **mandatory** under the *Food and Drug Regulations* (C.05.014) for sponsor-investigators and sponsors of clinical trials involving drugs. Failure to meet them constitutes a regulatory violation and may trigger a Health Canada inspection, clinical hold, or licence suspension. As legal representative, confirm at each AE review whether the sponsor has met these deadlines.

  | Event Type | Initial Report | Full (Follow-up) Report |
  |---|---|---|
  | Fatal or life-threatening SUSAR | **7 calendar days** from first awareness | **15 calendar days** from first awareness |
  | All other SUSARs | Not applicable | **15 calendar days** from first awareness |
  | Fatal or serious UADE (device) | **10 calendar days** from first awareness | **30 calendar days** from first awareness |
  | Annual Safety Report (drug) | Not applicable | Within **60 days** of the anniversary of the CTA issuance |

  For studies that are also subject to ICH E2A guidelines (most international trials), these timelines apply globally. The sponsor's regulatory affairs team should confirm which reporting obligations apply to the specific study.
</Warning>

<Note>
  The REB reporting obligation is **separate** from the Health Canada obligation. Even if the sponsor has already filed with Health Canada, the investigator (and sponsor-investigator) must also notify the REB in accordance with the institutional SOP. The REB may impose additional safeguards beyond what Health Canada requires.
</Note>

## The AE Review Process: Legal Representative's Steps

<Steps>
  <Step title="Receive and Triage the Report">
    The REB Coordinator forwards the AE or SAE report to board members. As legal representative, first confirm:

    - **Is this an SAE, SUSAR, UADE, or UPIR?** The classification determines the urgency of your review.
    - **Has the Health Canada reporting deadline been met?** Check the date of the sponsor's first awareness against the report date.
    - **Has the institutional risk management office been notified?** For SAEs involving hospitalisation or death, confirm internal notification per your hospital's policy.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Assess Causality">
    Causality is a clinical determination, but its legal implications are yours to analyse. The sponsor will assign a causality category (unrelated, unlikely, possible, probable, definite). Review whether:

    - The causality assessment is **internally consistent** with the clinical narrative
    - The assigned causality affects whether the event triggers **regulatory reporting thresholds**
    - A lower-than-warranted causality assessment appears to minimise the sponsor's reporting obligations (a red flag for regulatory non-compliance)
    - Multiple "possible" or "unrelated" events with a similar profile, taken together, suggest a pattern that could shift the overall causal assessment
  </Step>
  <Step title="Evaluate Consent Implications">
    Determine whether the reported event discloses a new or materially different risk that participants have not been warned about:

    - Is the event **unexpected** relative to the risks disclosed in the current approved consent form?
    - Would a reasonable participant consider this new risk **material** to their decision to continue participating?
    - If yes, is a **consent form amendment** required, and when must currently enrolled participants be re-consented?
    - Are there participants **currently enrolled** who may be at increased risk based on this new information?
  </Step>
  <Step title="Conduct Pattern Analysis">
    Individual AE reports are reviewed by the board as they are received, but the legal risk picture often emerges from patterns rather than isolated events. At each review:

    - Compare the new report to **prior SAEs and AEs** reported for this study
    - Consider whether the **rate** of a particular event type has increased
    - Assess whether the **severity** of events is escalating over time
    - Note any cluster by site, investigator, participant sub-group, or dose level
    - Raise the pattern concern in writing at the board meeting even if no single event alone would require action
  </Step>
  <Step title="Assess Regulatory Compliance">
    Confirm that the investigator and sponsor have met their obligations:

    - Health Canada reporting timelines (see table above)
    - Notification to all participating sites (for multi-site trials)
    - Update of the investigator brochure, if required
    - Notification to institutional risk management and legal counsel, if required by internal policy
    - Documentation in the study's Case Report Forms and regulatory binders

    If any obligation has not been met, the board should note the deficiency in its written record and communicate it to the PI as part of the REB's response.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Formulate the REB Response">
    Based on your analysis, advise the board on the appropriate response. The options range from **acknowledge and file** (for expected, non-serious events consistent with prior disclosures) to **immediate suspension** (see below). Document your legal reasoning in the meeting minutes.
  </Step>
</Steps>

## AE Review Checklist for the Legal Representative

Use this checklist for every SAE, SUSAR, UADE, or UPIR reviewed at the board level.

<Accordion title="Regulatory Compliance Checks">
  - [ ] Health Canada reporting deadline met (7/15/30 days as applicable)
  - [ ] Report submitted by the correct party (sponsor vs. sponsor-investigator)
  - [ ] Annual Safety Report filing is current
  - [ ] Investigator Brochure version referenced in the report is the current approved version
  - [ ] Any required CTA amendment has been filed
  - [ ] All participating sites have been notified (multi-site trials)
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Causality and Clinical Consistency">
  - [ ] Causality assessment is consistent with the clinical narrative
  - [ ] No apparent under-assignment of causality to avoid reporting thresholds
  - [ ] Seriousness criteria correctly applied (death, hospitalisation, life-threatening, etc.)
  - [ ] "Unexpected" determination made against the correct reference document (current IB or product monograph)
  - [ ] Pattern across prior reports considered and documented
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Consent Adequacy">
  - [ ] Event type and severity compared to risks disclosed in the current approved consent form
  - [ ] Determination made: is a consent form amendment required?
  - [ ] If amendment required: is immediate re-consent of enrolled participants necessary?
  - [ ] Re-consent plan assessed for adequacy (timeline, process, substitute decision-maker notification)
  - [ ] Participants who have completed active procedures but remain in follow-up considered
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Institutional and Liability Considerations">
  - [ ] Hospital risk management / legal department notified (as applicable per internal policy)
  - [ ] Sponsor indemnification coverage confirmed to extend to the reported event type
  - [ ] Investigator's duty to report to the REB met within the institutional SOP timeframe
  - [ ] Any potential regulatory inspection risk identified and flagged to institutional legal counsel
  - [ ] Event documented in the REB study file with date of receipt and board response
</Accordion>

## When to Recommend Suspension

<Warning>
  Recommend **immediate suspension** of study activities — or, in urgent cases, advise the REB Chair to exercise emergency suspension authority — when any of the following conditions are present:

  - **Death or life-threatening SAE** with a credible causal relationship to the study intervention, where the event type was not disclosed to participants
  - A **cluster of SAEs** suggesting a previously unrecognised systemic safety signal
  - **Repeated regulatory non-compliance** by the sponsor or investigator (missed Health Canada reporting, failure to notify the REB)
  - Evidence that **consent is being obtained under duress** or that participants are not being informed of new risks
  - A Health Canada **clinical hold** or request to suspend the trial

  Suspension is not a final determination of wrongdoing. It is a protective measure that preserves participant safety while the board conducts a full review. Document the grounds for suspension clearly. The PI must receive written notice of the suspension and the conditions required for reinstatement.
</Warning>

<Tip>
  When you recommend suspension, also advise the board on the **operational steps** participants who are mid-treatment should take. Abrupt discontinuation of some investigational treatments can itself cause harm. The suspension decision should include instructions to the PI on managing participants currently receiving the intervention.
</Tip>

## Post-Review Follow-up

<CardGroup cols={2}>
  <Card title="Protocol Amendment" icon="pen-to-square">
    If the AE discloses a new risk, require a protocol amendment addressing the risk mitigation strategy — whether new monitoring procedures, modified eligibility criteria, or additional safety assessments.
  </Card>
  <Card title="Consent Form Update" icon="file-signature">
    Require an updated consent form disclosing the new risk, with a timeline for re-consenting enrolled participants. Approve the updated form before re-consent begins.
  </Card>
  <Card title="Investigator Communication" icon="envelope">
    Issue a written REB response to the PI stating whether the board requires action, has taken note, or has concerns about regulatory compliance. Retain a copy in the study file.
  </Card>
  <Card title="Continuing Review Acceleration" icon="clock-rotate-left">
    If the AE raises concerns about ongoing participant safety, bring the continuing review date forward rather than waiting for the scheduled renewal. Document the rationale for early review.
  </Card>
</CardGroup>
