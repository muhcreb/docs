Liability and indemnification review is a core legal function that distinguishes a legally trained REB member from a clinical or scientific reviewer. When a research agreement or consent form contains deficient liability provisions, the consequences can extend beyond the participant to the institution, the investigator, and the REB itself. Your task is to identify arrangements that expose the hospital to unacceptable legal risk, that improperly restrict participant rights, or that fail to meet regulatory requirements — and to bring these clearly to the REB's attention before approval.

<Note>
  **Sponsor-Initiated vs. Investigator-Initiated Studies**

  The liability landscape differs significantly depending on who initiates the research:

  - **Sponsor-initiated studies** (SIS): A commercial sponsor (typically a pharmaceutical or medical device company) designs the protocol, holds the IND/IDE or clinical trial application, and is primarily responsible for the product under investigation. The sponsor should indemnify the hospital and investigators against claims arising from the sponsor's negligence, the study drug/device, or protocol design defects. The hospital's exposure is generally limited to investigator conduct.
  - **Investigator-initiated studies** (IIS): A hospital researcher designs and conducts the study, often without a commercial sponsor. There is no external sponsor to provide indemnification. The hospital's own liability exposure is substantially broader, and the investigator's institutional indemnification coverage must be confirmed. Insurance arrangements require particular scrutiny for IIS protocols.
</Note>

## What to Look for in Research Agreements

Research agreements — including Clinical Trial Agreements (CTAs), Data Use Agreements, and Material Transfer Agreements — contain liability provisions that can significantly affect the hospital's legal position. Review each of the following elements.

<Steps>
  <Step title="Indemnification Scope and Direction">
    Confirm that the sponsor agrees to indemnify and hold harmless the hospital, its affiliates, and the investigator from third-party claims arising from: (a) the sponsor's negligence or willful misconduct; (b) defects in the study drug, device, or product; and (c) defects in the protocol design. The indemnification should not be limited to claims arising from the sponsor's "proven" negligence — this effectively shifts the burden of proof in a way that may leave the hospital unprotected pending litigation.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Carve-outs and Exclusions">
    Review the exceptions to indemnification carefully. Standard carve-outs for the hospital's own negligence or protocol deviations by the investigator are acceptable. Flag any carve-outs that are overly broad, ambiguous, or that attempt to exclude the sponsor's liability for adverse events that were foreseeable from the investigational product's known risk profile.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Insurance Requirements">
    Confirm that the sponsor has agreed to maintain adequate clinical trial liability insurance throughout the study and for a defined period after completion. For clinical trials governed by Health Canada's Food and Drug Regulations Division 5, refer to the specific insurance expectations described below. Request certificates of insurance as a condition of REB approval where appropriate.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Limitation of Liability Clauses">
    Identify any clauses that cap the sponsor's total liability to the hospital at an amount that may be insufficient given the nature of the study. A liability cap of \$1 million may be adequate for a low-risk observational study but is plainly inadequate for a Phase I oncology trial. Flag disproportionate caps for renegotiation.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Compensation for Research-Related Injury">
    Confirm that the agreement addresses compensation for participants who suffer research-related injuries. Health Canada's guidance and the TCPS2 (Article 3.1) require that participants are not denied compensation they may be entitled to. Review whether the agreement provides a clear pathway for compensation or medical treatment of injuries caused by the investigational product.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Governing Law and Dispute Resolution">
    Identify the governing law clause. If the agreement designates a foreign jurisdiction's law as governing (e.g., Delaware or a European jurisdiction), flag this for institutional legal counsel. Canadian law — specifically the law of the province where the study is conducted — should govern claims arising from research conducted in Canada.
  </Step>
</Steps>

## Insurance Requirements for Sponsor-Initiated Clinical Trials

<CardGroup cols={2}>
  <Card title="Health Canada / Division 5" icon="shield-halved">
    Health Canada's Division 5 of the Food and Drug Regulations does not prescribe specific insurance dollar amounts, but the Guidance Document for Clinical Trials (GUI-0100) expects that sponsors maintain adequate liability insurance. Industry standard in Canada is typically a minimum of \$10–20 million per occurrence for Phase II–III trials, with clinical trial specific coverage (not general commercial liability).
  </Card>

  <Card title="ICH E6(R3) (GCP)" icon="file-contract">
    The International Council for Harmonisation's Good Clinical Practice guideline, Annex 1, Section 3.14 ("Insurance/Indemnification/Compensation to Participants and Investigators") requires the sponsor to provide insurance or indemnification covering compensation for trial-related injury or death, and this obligation is incorporated into Health Canada's GCP expectations for clinical trials. (This was Section 5.8 under the prior E6(R2); Health Canada adopted E6(R3) effective April 1, 2026.)
  </Card>

  <Card title="CTA vs. Letter of Indemnification" icon="pen-to-square">
    Some sponsors provide indemnification through a standalone Letter of Indemnification rather than a CTA clause. Either format is acceptable, but confirm the letter is current, executed by an authorized officer of the sponsor, and covers all participating sites. A letter limited to the U.S. sponsor entity may not provide effective coverage in Canadian courts.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Hospital's Own Insurance" icon="hospital">
    Confirm that the study falls within the scope of the hospital's existing clinical trial liability or professional liability insurance. Some hospital policies exclude investigator-initiated studies or studies conducted outside the institution's physical premises. Escalate any coverage gaps to institutional risk management before REB approval.
  </Card>
</CardGroup>

## Liability Scenarios

<Accordion title="Research-Related Injuries in Clinical Trials">
  When a participant suffers an injury that is causally related to their participation in a clinical trial, the legal question of who bears responsibility — and who pays for treatment and compensation — must be resolved before the study begins, not after.

  **What to look for:**

  - Does the consent form clearly describe the nature and scope of available compensation for research-related injury? Vague language such as "emergency medical treatment will be provided" without specifying who pays is inadequate.
  - Does the CTA or sponsor agreement commit the sponsor to covering the cost of medical treatment for study-related adverse events? Confirm this obligation is not limited to "emergencies" and does not exclude events that were foreseeable based on the known risk profile of the investigational product.
  - Is there a distinction between injuries caused by the investigational product and injuries caused by study procedures mandated by the protocol (e.g., additional biopsies, frequent blood draws)? Both should be covered by the sponsor's indemnification, but some CTAs limit coverage to product-related injuries only.
  - For Phase I trials in healthy volunteers, confirm that the compensation and medical coverage provisions are especially robust — healthy volunteers assume risk with no prospect of direct therapeutic benefit.

  <Tip>
    Request a copy of the sponsor's standard No-Fault Compensation policy or equivalent if the study is a Phase I or Phase II trial involving healthy volunteers or high-risk interventions. These policies provide direct compensation to injured participants without requiring proof of negligence and represent best practice in Canadian research.
  </Tip>
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Investigator-Initiated Studies — No Sponsor Indemnification">
  When there is no commercial sponsor, the hospital and investigator bear the full legal and financial exposure for harm arising from the study. This significantly elevates the standard of scrutiny you should apply.

  **Key review points:**

  - Confirm that the investigator is covered under the hospital's professional liability or clinical trial insurance policy for the study activities described in the protocol.
  - If the study involves use of a licensed product outside its approved indications (off-label use in a trial context), confirm that the hospital's insurer is aware and that coverage is confirmed.
  - If the study uses an investigational device or novel procedure, confirm that the appropriate pre-study regulatory filings have been made with Health Canada and that coverage for the experimental activity is confirmed.
  - Review the consent form to ensure it accurately describes the absence of a sponsor and the available recourse for research-related injury. Participants should understand that there is no pharmaceutical company indemnification supporting the study.
  - Confirm that the investigator's institutional contract does not contain personal indemnification obligations that could expose them to personal liability outside the institution's coverage.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Compensation and Inducement — The Legal Line">
  Compensation for research participation occupies an uncomfortable middle ground in Canadian research law — participants should be able to recover costs and receive reasonable recognition for their time, but compensation must not be so substantial that it impairs free and voluntary consent.

  **Legal review considerations:**

  - **Reimbursement of out-of-pocket expenses**: Always permissible. Confirm that the consent form accurately reflects the amounts and timing.
  - **Honoraria for time and inconvenience**: Permissible within reasonable limits. There is no fixed statutory maximum in Canada, but the REB should assess whether the amount could constitute undue inducement given the target population's socioeconomic circumstances.
  - **Completion bonuses**: A single payment contingent on completing the entire study creates a financial incentive to remain in the study even after the participant would otherwise wish to withdraw. This may compromise voluntariness. Flag completion bonuses that represent a substantial proportion of total compensation.
  - **Payment to healthcare providers**: Finders' fees or payments to clinicians who recruit participants are ethically prohibited under TCPS2 and the CMA Code of Ethics. Any agreement that provides per-capita recruitment payments to investigators or clinical staff must be flagged.

  <Warning>
    **Participants Cannot Waive Their Right to Compensation for Injury**

    Any provision in a consent form or research agreement that purports to have participants waive their right to seek compensation for harm caused by the research is legally unenforceable in Canada and may constitute a deceptive practice. This includes language such as:

    - "I agree not to hold the hospital responsible for any complications resulting from participation."
    - "I release all claims against the sponsor arising from my participation in this study."
    - "By signing this form, I waive any right to pursue legal action against the investigators."

    Such language must be struck from the consent form as a condition of approval. Its presence is not merely a technical deficiency — it signals a fundamental misunderstanding of participant rights and may indicate other problematic provisions in the broader agreement. Escalate to institutional legal counsel if the sponsor resists removing this language.
  </Warning>
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Multi-Site and International Studies">
  Liability and indemnification arrangements in multi-site and international trials require additional scrutiny because the allocation of responsibility across sites may be unclear, and foreign governing law provisions may reduce the hospital's effective protections.

  **Review checklist for multi-site studies:**

  - Confirm that the CTA applies to and covers all Canadian participating sites, not merely the lead site or the sponsor's home jurisdiction.
  - Where a Canadian hospital is a sub-site under a lead site CTA, review whether the indemnification flows through from the sponsor to all sub-sites or whether sub-sites must rely on the lead institution for coverage.
  - For international studies involving a U.S. FDA IND or IDE, confirm that Canadian participants and the Canadian site are expressly covered — IND applications are U.S. documents and may not automatically establish liability coverage for Canadian operations.
  - Review whether the study has a single global CTA or whether site-specific addenda exist. Conflicts between the global agreement and local addenda can create coverage gaps.
  - Confirm the governing law clause designates Canadian (provincial) law for claims arising from Canadian participants.
</Accordion>

## Red-Flag Clauses — Reference Table

Review every research agreement and consent form for the following specific language patterns. Each represents a material legal concern that must be addressed before REB approval.

<CardGroup cols={2}>
  <Card title="🚩 Unilateral Amendment Rights" icon="triangle-exclamation">
    Language allowing the sponsor to amend the protocol, consent form, or agreement unilaterally without REB notification or re-approval. Example: _"Sponsor may modify the protocol at any time without prior notice."_
  </Card>

  <Card title="🚩 Data Publication Restrictions" icon="triangle-exclamation">
    Clauses giving the sponsor the right to suppress or delay publication of study results indefinitely, or requiring investigator approval before reporting adverse events to regulatory authorities.
  </Card>

  <Card title="🚩 Confidentiality Over Safety" icon="triangle-exclamation">
    Provisions requiring that safety-related findings or adverse events be kept confidential before reporting to Health Canada or the REB. These conflict with mandatory reporting obligations and are void to the extent of the conflict.
  </Card>

  <Card title="🚩 Indemnification Running Only One Way" icon="triangle-exclamation">
    Agreements that require the hospital to indemnify the sponsor but contain no reciprocal indemnification of the hospital by the sponsor. This reverses the expected allocation of risk.
  </Card>

  <Card title="🚩 IP Ownership of Participant Data" icon="triangle-exclamation">
    Provisions claiming that all data, including participant health data, becomes the exclusive intellectual property of the sponsor with no right retained by the hospital or participants.
  </Card>

  <Card title="🚩 Post-Study Access Waivers" icon="triangle-exclamation">
    Language in the consent form indicating that participants who benefit from an investigational product during the trial will have no right of access to it after the study ends, without any sponsor commitment to provide continued access or transition to approved therapy.
  </Card>
</CardGroup>
