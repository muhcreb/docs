The consent form is the single most important document you will review. It is what a real participant — possibly worried, possibly unwell, possibly overwhelmed — will actually hold in their hands and be asked to sign. Whether the study is ethical, in practice, comes down largely to whether that document does its job.

As a community representative, you are usually the best-qualified person in the room to judge whether a consent form works. You approach it the way a real reader would, without the researcher's assumptions and without the clinician's shorthand.

## What a Good Consent Form Does

A consent form has to do several things at once:

<CardGroup cols={2}>
  <Card title="Explain the study honestly" icon="circle-info">
    What is being studied? Why? Who is running it? Who is paying for it?
  </Card>

  <Card title="Describe procedures" icon="list-ol">
    What will actually happen to the participant? How many visits, how long, what tests, what samples?
  </Card>

  <Card title="Name the risks" icon="triangle-exclamation">
    Not just the physical ones — emotional, social, privacy, and financial risks matter too.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Describe the benefits (or lack thereof)" icon="circle-check">
    What might participants gain? If the answer is "nothing direct," the form must say so.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Explain alternatives" icon="code-branch">
    What are participants' options if they don't join the study? Standard care must be available.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Confirm voluntariness" icon="hand">
    Participation is a free choice. Withdrawal is possible at any time, without penalty.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Address confidentiality" icon="lock">
    Who will see the data? How is it stored? For how long? Who owns samples?
  </Card>

  <Card title="Provide contact info" icon="phone">
    Who to call with questions? Who to call to complain? Who to call in a medical emergency?
  </Card>
</CardGroup>

## How to Read a Consent Form

Read it the way a stressed patient would. Do not skim.

<Steps>
  <Step title="Read it out loud, or read it slowly">
    Skimming hides problems. If a sentence trips you up, mark it. That is a real problem for a real reader.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Watch for jargon">
    "Randomized double-blind placebo-controlled trial." "Pharmacokinetics." "Biomarker." "Adverse event." Each one is a small barrier. Every unexplained term is a place where the participant is going to guess — or give up.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Time yourself">
    Consent forms should generally be readable in 15–20 minutes. If it takes you an hour, it is too long. If it takes 5 minutes because it is too vague, that is also a problem.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Compare the risks section to the protocol">
    Every risk described in the protocol should appear in the consent form, in language the participant will understand. If the protocol mentions a serious side effect and the consent form does not, that is a major flag.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Look at the order">
    Are risks buried at the end and benefits played up at the top? Is the withdrawal-at-any-time reassurance hidden on page 8? Order matters — it signals what the researcher thinks is important.
  </Step>
</Steps>

## Warning Signs to Flag

<Warning>
  Any one of these should prompt you to raise a concern at the meeting.
</Warning>

<Accordion title="Jargon without explanation">
  "You will undergo venipuncture." (You will have blood drawn.) "The intervention is administered subcutaneously." (You will get an injection under the skin.) Every unexplained technical term is a barrier. If a term must be used, it should be defined the first time.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Vague risks">
  "Some participants may experience side effects." What side effects? How common? How serious? "Side effects include nausea (about 1 in 3 participants), rash (about 1 in 10), and rarely, liver problems (about 1 in 500) that may require stopping the drug" is honest. The vague version is not.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Overselling benefits">
  "This study may help you." Might, might not. "This study is investigating whether Drug X can shrink tumours. It is not known to work, and most participants in early-phase studies do not benefit personally. The purpose is to learn whether the treatment might help future patients." That is honest.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Confusing randomization language">
  "You will be assigned to one of two groups by a computer." What are the groups? Is one of them a placebo? Will the participant be told which they are in? Randomization needs to be described plainly, including what "placebo" means.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Coercive language">
  "By participating, you help science." "Your doctor recommends this study." These pressure participants into feeling obligated. The consent form should say the opposite: that saying no is completely fine and their care will not be affected.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Buried withdrawal rights">
  Participants must be told they can withdraw at any time, for any reason, without giving a reason, and without losing access to standard care. If this is a small paragraph on page 9, ask that it be moved up.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Missing or unclear privacy explanation">
  Under Québec's Law 5 and TCPS 2 Chapter 5, participants must be told how their information will be used and protected. Vague statements like "your information will be kept confidential" are not enough. Who sees it? Where is it stored? For how long? Is it shared outside the hospital? Outside the province? Outside the country?
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Unrealistic estimates of time or burden">
  "Each visit takes about 30 minutes." But the protocol calls for blood work, ECG, questionnaires, and physical exam — which is a two-hour visit, minimum. If the consent form's time estimate is unrealistic, participants will feel misled when the study actually starts.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Compensation described in a way that could pressure people">
  Compensation should reimburse costs (parking, meals, lost wages) and acknowledge time. It should not be so high that it pressures people who need money into risks they would otherwise refuse. If the amount seems out of proportion to the ask, raise it.
</Accordion>

## Special Situations

<Tabs>
  <Tab title="Consent for children">
    Under Québec's Civil Code (article 21), children generally need a parent or guardian to consent on their behalf — and the child themselves must also agree ("assent") at whatever level they can. A separate, age-appropriate assent document is often required. As a community rep: is the child's version actually written for a child? Or is it just the adult version with smaller words?
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Consent for adults who cannot consent for themselves">
    A substitute decision-maker (often a family member or legal representative) must consent, and the participant must not object. The consent form for the substitute must acknowledge that they are consenting on behalf of someone else, and describe the participant's own indication of agreement or disagreement.
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Emergency research">
    Very occasionally, studies enrol participants who cannot consent because of the emergency (unconscious trauma patients, for example). These studies have special rules under TCPS 2 (Article 3.8). They require heightened scrutiny — do not let them slide.
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Consent for future use of samples or data">
    Some studies ask participants to consent not just to this study, but to future, unspecified uses of their samples or data. That must be crystal clear in the form, and participants must be told they can consent to the current study without consenting to future uses.
  </Tab>
</Tabs>

<Tip>
  When you present your comments at the meeting, be specific: page number, section, exact sentence, and a suggested rewrite where possible. That turns a concern into an actionable revision request. "On page 4, the sentence about randomization is confusing. Could it be rewritten as: 'Everyone in the study will get either the study drug or a placebo (a pill that looks the same but has no active medication). A computer will decide which one you get, and no one — including your doctor — will know until the study is over.' That is much clearer to a general reader."
</Tip>
