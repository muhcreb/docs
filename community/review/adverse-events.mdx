An adverse event (AE) is any unwanted medical or psychological occurrence in a participant during a study. A **serious adverse event (SAE)** is one that is life-threatening, results in hospitalization, causes lasting disability, or leads to death.

Studies generate adverse event reports — sometimes many of them. Not every AE means the study is unsafe. But every SAE requires the board to ask serious questions.

## The Basic Vocabulary

<CardGroup cols={2}>
  <Card title="Adverse event (AE)" icon="pill">
    Any unwanted medical or psychological occurrence in a participant, whether or not related to the study.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Serious adverse event (SAE)" icon="triangle-exclamation">
    An AE that is life-threatening, requires hospitalization, causes lasting harm, or results in death.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Expected AE" icon="circle-info">
    An event of a type and frequency that was anticipated and disclosed in the consent form.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Unexpected AE" icon="circle-exclamation">
    An event that was not anticipated, or that occurs more often or more severely than expected.
  </Card>
</CardGroup>

<Info>
  Not every AE is caused by the study. Someone in a diabetes trial who breaks their arm skiing has had an AE, but not one caused by the study drug. Researchers must report it anyway; the board's job is to think about what the pattern of AEs is telling us.
</Info>

## What Community Reps Should Ask

When an AE or SAE report comes to the board, the questions to ask are:

<Steps>
  <Step title="What happened?">
    In plain language, what did the participant experience? What was the outcome?
  </Step>
  <Step title="Was it caused by the study?">
    The researcher's report will include a causality assessment ("related," "possibly related," "unlikely related," "not related"). Is that assessment convincing?
  </Step>
  <Step title="Was it in the consent form?">
    Was this risk disclosed? If yes, at what stated frequency? If no, the consent form probably needs updating.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Is it a pattern?">
    One SAE is a data point. Three SAEs of the same type is a pattern. Patterns change what the board should do.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Do current participants need to be told?">
    Especially for SAEs that are unexpected or that raise the known risk. Participants deserve to know.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Should the study continue?">
    Usually yes. Sometimes no. The board can pause enrollment, require changes, or in serious cases stop the study.
  </Step>
</Steps>

## The Community Rep Focus: The Participant Behind the Report

AE reports are often written in clinical, detached language. Your job is to imagine the person behind the report.

- Someone in a cancer trial developed severe liver toxicity and was hospitalized for three weeks. What was that like for them, and for their family?
- Someone in a mental health study became so distressed by the interview questions that they needed emergency psychiatric care afterward. Did the study team provide adequate support? Are safeguards adequate for the next participant?
- Someone in a device trial had a device malfunction that required an unplanned second surgery. What are we telling future participants about that risk?

Asking these questions is not about second-guessing the medical team. It is about making sure the story of what happens to participants stays visible.

## What the Board Can Do

<Tabs>
  <Tab title="No action">
    If the AE was expected, appropriately disclosed, and consistent with previous experience — acknowledge and move on.
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Update the consent form">
    If the risk is now known to be higher, more frequent, or different than the current consent form describes — require an update. Future participants deserve the current picture.
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Notify current participants">
    If the new information is significant, require the researcher to tell everyone still in the study, so they can decide whether to continue.
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Pause enrollment">
    If the pattern is concerning but not conclusive, the board can pause new enrollment while the researcher gathers more data.
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Suspend or stop the study">
    In the most serious cases, the board can require the study to stop enrolling and, if necessary, stop treating current participants — with a plan for their safe transition.
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Refer to Health Canada">
    For drug and device trials, serious findings are also reported to Health Canada under Division 5. The REB does not usually make that call — the sponsor does — but the board can raise concerns about whether reporting is happening appropriately.
  </Tab>
</Tabs>

## Warning Signs

<Warning>
  Any of these should trigger deeper board discussion.
</Warning>

<Accordion title="Multiple SAEs of the same type">
  A single serious event may be a fluke. Three of the same type is a signal. The pattern deserves careful analysis before the study continues.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="SAE that was not disclosed in the consent form">
  A serious risk that participants were not warned about is a major problem. The consent form must be updated, existing participants must be told, and the board should ask why the risk was not anticipated in the original design.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Late reporting">
  SAEs have short reporting timelines under Division 5 (typically 15 days for non-fatal, 7 days for fatal or life-threatening). If reports come in late, that itself is a problem — it suggests the study team is not monitoring participants carefully.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Vague reports">
  "Participant experienced an SAE." What kind? What outcome? Was there a hospital stay? Were they still in the study? Missing detail suggests either sloppy documentation or something the researcher would rather not say.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Researcher pushback on updates">
  Sometimes researchers resist updating the consent form because it complicates recruitment or requires re-consenting existing participants. That resistance itself is worth noting. The consent form must reflect the study as it is now, not as it was when the study started.
</Accordion>

## The Community Voice in Adverse Event Review

AE review can become highly technical — clinical members will focus on causality mechanisms, dose relationships, and comparison to historical data. That is their job. Your job, alongside them, is:

1. To keep the focus on the participant experience.
2. To push for consent form updates that participants would actually understand.
3. To ask whether current participants are being properly informed.
4. To speak up when a pattern looks concerning, even if the technical members are still weighing evidence.

<Tip>
  "Even if the causality is still uncertain, would a reasonable person want to know about this before agreeing to join the study?" is often the right question to ask. If the answer is yes, the consent form probably needs an update, no matter what the technical debate settles on.
</Tip>
