Once a study is approved, it does not stay frozen. Researchers often change protocols during the course of a study — adding sites, updating drugs, changing procedures. Every meaningful change must go back to the REB as an **amendment**. Amendments are one of the highest-volume things the board reviews.

Your job is to look at each amendment and ask: **is this still the study participants agreed to?**

## What Amendments Look Like

<CardGroup cols={2}>
  <Card title="Minor amendments" icon="pencil">
    Typo fixes, updated contact info, adding a co-investigator to the team, small administrative updates.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Substantive amendments" icon="pen-to-square">
    New sites, revised drug doses, expanded eligibility, new procedures, extended follow-up.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Major amendments" icon="file-pen">
    New drug arm, redesigned procedures, significant scope change, new population.
  </Card>
</CardGroup>

The categories matter because they determine how the amendment gets reviewed:

- **Minor amendments** may go through delegated or administrative review.
- **Substantive amendments** typically require delegated or full board review depending on the impact.
- **Major amendments** should go to the full board — and may effectively require a new consent form and re-consenting of existing participants.

## The Key Question: Does the Consent Form Still Reflect Reality?

This is the single most important question for a community rep on an amendment.

<Steps>
  <Step title="What has changed about what participants will experience?">
    New procedures? Longer visits? More visits? Different drug or dose? Longer study duration?
  </Step>
  <Step title="Are those changes reflected in the consent form?">
    A common failure: the protocol is amended, but the consent form is not updated to match. Read both.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Do current participants need to be told?">
    If the change affects them — new risks, new burdens, new procedures they haven't had yet — they should be re-consented, or at least formally notified.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Do future participants need a new consent form?">
    If the study is meaningfully different from what was originally approved, the consent form for new participants needs to be updated.
  </Step>
</Steps>

## Re-consent vs. Notification

Not every amendment requires participants to sign a new consent form. TCPS 2 (Article 6.14) recognizes different levels of response:

<Tabs>
  <Tab title="Full re-consent">
    Required when the change is significant: new risks, new procedures the participant hasn't yet undergone, meaningful change to the study's aim. Participants receive an updated consent form and are asked to sign it. If they refuse, they can withdraw without penalty.
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Formal notification">
    Used for changes that affect participants but don't rise to the level of full re-consent. For example, a new safety finding is disclosed via a letter, and participants confirm they still want to continue.
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="No action needed">
    For minor administrative changes (correcting a typo, updating a team member's title), no participant notification is needed.
  </Tab>
</Tabs>

<Info>
  When the board is unsure whether re-consent is needed, err toward doing it. Participants who receive an updated consent form and choose to continue are exercising informed choice. Participants who continue based on outdated information are not.
</Info>

## Types of Amendments and What to Watch For

<Accordion title="New study sites">
  Straightforward on its face, but ask: does the new site have the resources to run the study safely? Is the local REB approval in place? Does the consent form need site-specific updates (contact info, location details)?
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Expanded eligibility">
  Opening a study to a new population is a bigger deal than it looks. Are the new participants at meaningfully different risk? Are the recruitment materials appropriate for them? If the new population is more vulnerable, are additional protections needed?
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Changed drug dose or new drug arm">
  For clinical trials, this is major. New dose may change the risk profile. New drug arm effectively adds a new sub-study. Consent form must be updated; existing participants may need re-consent depending on which arm they're on.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Added procedures">
  "We'd like to add one more blood draw per visit." How much more? Every added procedure is added burden. Is the burden still reasonable? Is the consent form updated?
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Extended follow-up">
  "We'd like to extend the follow-up period from one year to five years." Participants signed up for one year. Five is a very different commitment. Re-consent likely required.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="New sponsor or funding">
  Not visible to participants directly, but potentially relevant. New sponsor may want access to data. Financial conflicts of interest may shift. Ask.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Updated safety information">
  New known side effect discovered? Must be added to the consent form. Existing participants must be notified. Failure to do this is one of the most common problems in ongoing studies.
</Accordion>

## When an Amendment Is Really a New Study

<Warning>
  Sometimes an amendment is so large that it effectively creates a new study. A drug trial that adds a second, unrelated drug arm; a survey that changes its topic from one issue to another; a study that swaps its target population. When the amendment fundamentally changes what the study is, the board should treat it as a new submission — not an amendment on top of the existing approval.
</Warning>

If an amendment feels like it is really a new study wearing an amendment's clothes, say so at the meeting. This is a legitimate call for the board to make.

## Community Rep's Amendment Checklist

<Steps>
  <Step title="What is changing?">
    Summarize in one sentence what is different from before.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Does this affect participants' experience?">
    If yes, does the consent form reflect it?
  </Step>
  <Step title="Do current participants need to be told or re-consented?">
    If yes, is that plan in the amendment package?
  </Step>
  <Step title="Do future participants need a different consent form?">
    If yes, is the updated form in the package and does it make sense?
  </Step>
  <Step title="Is this really an amendment, or is it a new study?">
    If the latter, say so.
  </Step>
</Steps>

<Tip>
  When the consent form is updated as part of an amendment, review the tracked changes version if available — that shows you exactly what changed, not just what the new document says. It is much easier to catch a missing update or a poorly worded addition when you can see it in context.
</Tip>
