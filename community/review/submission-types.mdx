The REB reviews many kinds of submissions, not just brand-new studies. Knowing the categories helps you understand what is being asked of the board at each meeting and where your input will matter most.

## The Main Categories

<CardGroup cols={2}>
  <Card title="Initial submissions" icon="file-plus">
    A brand-new study, seeking first-time approval before it can begin. The heaviest read.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Amendments" icon="pen-to-square">
    Changes to a previously approved study. Range from tiny (a typo fix) to major (adding a new drug arm).
  </Card>

  <Card title="Continuing review" icon="arrows-rotate">
    Annual (or more frequent) check-ins on ongoing studies. Confirms the study is still ethical and still going as planned.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Adverse event reports" icon="triangle-exclamation">
    Notifications of serious problems that occurred during the study. Triggers a targeted review.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Protocol deviations" icon="route">
    Reports of when the study did not follow its own protocol. Assesses whether participants were affected.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Closure reports" icon="flag-checkered">
    Final report at the end of a study. Confirms it wrapped up appropriately.
  </Card>
</CardGroup>

## Initial Submissions

**What it is:** A researcher's first application to the REB for a new study.

**What is in the package:**

- The protocol (the full study plan).
- The consent form (and any assent forms for children).
- Recruitment materials (posters, letters, ads, scripts).
- Investigator brochures (for drug or device trials) — background on the product being tested.
- Sponsor agreements (for industry-funded studies).
- Data collection tools (questionnaires, surveys).
- Any special protocols (e.g., for research with Indigenous communities, community engagement plans).

**Where community reps focus:**

1. **Consent form** — the single most important document. Is it understandable? Honest about risks? Free of pressure?
2. **Recruitment materials** — fair and non-coercive?
3. **Participant procedures** — reasonable burden?
4. **Vulnerable-group protections** — present and adequate?
5. **Compensation and reimbursement** — fair, not coercive?

## Amendments

**What it is:** A change to a previously approved study. Researchers must get REB approval before implementing most changes.

**Common types of amendments:**

- Adding or removing study sites.
- Changing the study drug dose or schedule.
- Adding new tests or procedures.
- Changing eligibility criteria.
- Updating the consent form to reflect any of the above.
- Extending the study timeline.
- Adding new researchers to the team.

**Where community reps focus:**

- **Does the change alter the participant experience?** If yes, does the consent form need to be updated?
- **Do already-enrolled participants need to be re-consented?** If the change affects them (new risk, new procedure, longer commitment), yes.
- **Is the amendment consistent with why participants originally agreed?** A minor tweak to eligibility is one thing. A major shift in the study's aim is another — that may effectively be a new study.

## Continuing Review

**What it is:** A required check-in on ongoing studies, usually annually. TCPS 2 (Article 6.14) requires the REB to do this.

**What is in the package:**

- Number of participants enrolled to date.
- Number of withdrawals, and reasons.
- Adverse events over the past year.
- Protocol deviations.
- Any complaints from participants.
- Progress toward the study's aims.

**Where community reps focus:**

- **Withdrawal patterns**: Is anyone dropping out at an unusual rate? Are the reasons cause for concern?
- **Complaints**: What did participants complain about? Was it addressed?
- **Any pattern that would change your original review**: If you would not have approved the study as it is now being run, say so.

## Adverse Event (AE) and Serious Adverse Event (SAE) Reports

**What it is:** A report that something bad happened to a participant — a side effect, a hospitalization, or worse.

**Where community reps focus:**

- **Does the consent form need to be updated?** If a new risk has emerged, or a known risk is happening more often than the consent form says, future participants deserve to know.
- **Do current participants need to be told?** If the risk landscape has meaningfully shifted, participants who are still enrolled may need a follow-up conversation.
- **Should the study continue?** Sometimes the pattern of AEs is severe enough that the board must consider pausing or stopping the study.

  See the [Adverse Events](/community/review/adverse-events) page for a deeper dive.
</Info>

## Protocol Deviations

**What it is:** A report that the study did not follow its own protocol — for example, a participant was enrolled who did not meet eligibility criteria, or a test was not done at the required time.

**Where community reps focus:**

- **Was any participant harmed or put at risk by the deviation?**
- **Was it a one-time mistake, or a pattern?** Patterns suggest a problem with how the study is being run.
- **Was the deviation reported promptly?** Late reporting is itself a red flag.

## Closure Reports

**What it is:** The final report submitted when a study ends.

**Where community reps focus:**

- **How will results be shared with participants?** If the study has none of this planned, raise it.
- **What happens to participant samples and data?** Are they destroyed, retained, or used for future research (in which case, did participants consent to that use)?
- **Are there any lessons for future studies?** Sometimes a closure report reveals things that should inform how similar studies are reviewed in the future.

<Tip>
  If you are unsure what category a submission falls into, ask the Coordinator. Categories matter because they determine the level of review, and mislabelling a major amendment as a minor one is one of the more common ways studies slip past the checks that should catch them.
</Tip>
