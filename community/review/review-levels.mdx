Not every study needs the whole board sitting around a table for two hours. The TCPS 2 lets the REB assign studies to different levels of review based on how much risk they involve. This page explains how that works and what your role is at each level.

## The Levels

## How the Level Is Chosen

The TCPS 2 (Article 6.12) defines **minimal risk** as risk that is "no greater than that encountered by the participant in those aspects of his or her everyday life that relate to the research." That is a mouthful, but the practical translation is: if the study is unlikely to cause any harm beyond what people encounter in daily life, it may qualify for delegated review.

What pushes a study into full board:

- More than minimal risk (any invasive procedure, any experimental drug, meaningful chance of harm).
- Involvement of a vulnerable population (children, older adults with cognitive impairment, Indigenous communities, prisoners, and others).
- Sensitive subject matter (trauma, addiction, sexual behaviour, illegal activity, stigmatized conditions).
- Novel methods that the board has not evaluated before.
- Significant public interest or controversy.
- International or multi-site complexity.

The REB Coordinator, in consultation with the Chair, usually makes the initial call on which level applies. If any reviewer thinks a delegated file should go to full board, they can escalate it — which is the community rep's important right.

## Full Board Review

**How it works:** The study appears on the agenda for a scheduled meeting. Primary reviewers (typically one scientific member, and often a community and/or legal representative) present their comments. The researcher may be invited to present and answer questions. The board deliberates and votes.

**Your role:**

- Read the whole file if you are a primary reviewer; skim the summaries and the consent forms otherwise.
- Come prepared to speak. This is where community perspective has the most visibility.
- Vote. Every vote counts equally.

**Typical files:**

- Drug trials.
- Studies of new medical devices.
- Studies involving vulnerable populations.
- Studies involving deception or non-disclosure at consent.
- Studies asking to waive consent for data access.
- Anything the Coordinator or Chair flagged as sensitive.

## Delegated Review

**How it works:** A smaller subset of the board — often the Chair, one or two designated reviewers, and sometimes a community representative — reviews the study without pulling the whole board together. A decision is reached by consensus among the delegated reviewers.

**Your role:**

- If you are one of the delegated reviewers, read the file and share your comments with the Chair or Coordinator.
- If you have serious concerns, you can push the file up to full board review — your judgement alone is enough to trigger that.

**Typical files:**

- Minimal-risk survey studies.
- Chart reviews with strong privacy safeguards.
- Interview studies on non-sensitive topics.
- Minor amendments to previously approved studies.
- Continuing reviews with no new issues.

<Info>
  Your right to escalate a delegated file to full board is a real check. If something about a study feels wrong — even if you cannot fully articulate why — you can request full board review. The Chair will almost always honour that request. Never worry that you are "overreacting" by asking for a fuller look.
</Info>

## Administrative Review

**How it works:** The REB office handles very minor updates without member deliberation. Examples: a corrected typo in a consent form, updated contact information for the research team, closure reports on studies that ended without incident.

**Your role:**

- None on most files. The Coordinator will flag anything unusual for member attention.

## A Simple Rule of Thumb

<Tip>
  If the file involves any real chance of harm to a participant, any vulnerable population, or any novel or sensitive element — it should be full board. If in doubt, err toward more review, not less. That is the direction of caution.
</Tip>

## What Community Reps Should Watch For

<Warning>
  Some studies get labelled "minimal risk" because the physical risk is low, even though the emotional, social, or privacy risks are significant. A survey about experiences of workplace harassment is a good example — no needles involved, but real potential for distress and re-traumatization. If a study's non-physical risks look meaningful and it has been assigned to delegated review, ask for it to be escalated.
</Warning>

<Accordion title="'Chart review' studies">
  These are studies that access patient records to answer a research question without contacting participants directly. They are often assigned to delegated review because there is no physical risk. But privacy risk is real, and consent is often waived. Ask: is the data properly protected? Is the waiver justified? Is this the kind of study your community would be comfortable with?
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Studies of vulnerable groups">
  Even minimal-risk studies involving vulnerable groups often need full-board review under TCPS 2. If you see a delegated file involving children, Indigenous communities, or others in a vulnerable position, ask the Chair whether it should be escalated.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Amendments that grow beyond 'minor'">
  Amendments start as delegated-review items by default. But some amendments meaningfully change the study — for example, adding a new drug arm, extending follow-up by two years, or opening enrollment to a new population. Those deserve full-board review, not a rubber stamp.
</Accordion>
