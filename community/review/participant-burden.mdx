One of the clearest contributions a community representative makes is asking a deceptively simple question: **is what this study is asking of participants actually reasonable?** Researchers, understandably, focus on the science. You focus on the person.

This page walks through how to evaluate participant burden and recruitment practices.

## What Counts as "Burden"

Burden is not just the medical procedures. Think broadly.

<CardGroup cols={2}>
  <Card title="Time" icon="clock">
    How many visits? How long each? How much travel? How long is the total study — weeks, months, years?
  </Card>

  <Card title="Physical" icon="stethoscope">
    Blood draws, biopsies, imaging, physical exams, medication side effects, procedures.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Emotional" icon="face-frown">
    Answering questions about trauma, difficult experiences, or stigmatized conditions. Waiting for results. Being reminded of illness.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Social" icon="users">
    Awkward conversations with family. Being identified as a participant in a sensitive study. Disruption to work, childcare, and social life.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Financial" icon="dollar-sign">
    Time off work. Travel. Parking. Meals. Childcare. Many participants are out of pocket even when the study covers medical costs.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Practical" icon="road">
    Getting to the hospital repeatedly. Language barriers. Accessibility for people with disabilities. Fitting the study around chemo, dialysis, or existing appointments.
  </Card>
</CardGroup>

## Questions to Ask About Burden

<Steps>
  <Step title="Total time commitment">
    Add up the visits and duration. How many hours over how many months? Would you agree to that yourself?
  </Step>
  <Step title="Fit into life">
    Are visits scheduled during business hours only? That excludes people with jobs. Are they only at the main hospital? That excludes people who live far away. Is childcare available or covered?
  </Step>
  <Step title="Cumulative effect">
    Any single visit might be reasonable. But six blood draws in eight weeks is more than the sum of its parts — especially for someone already dealing with an illness.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Withdrawal-friendly design">
    Can participants easily withdraw partway through? Or does the design (long follow-up, embedded procedures) subtly discourage it?
  </Step>
  <Step title="Compensation vs. burden">
    Is compensation proportional to what is being asked? Under-compensation exploits participants. Over-compensation can pressure them.
  </Step>
</Steps>

<Info>
  Reasonable burden is not the same as "no burden." Some studies genuinely require significant participation. The question is whether the burden is proportionate to what the study is trying to learn, and whether participants are given the information they need to decide clearly.
</Info>

## Recruitment: Fair and Non-Coercive

How participants are approached matters as much as how the study is designed.

<Tabs>
  <Tab title="Who does the recruiting">
    If a participant's own treating doctor asks them to join a study, they may feel they can't say no without damaging the relationship. Best practice is that recruitment be done by someone other than the treating clinician, or that a clear firewall be described in the consent form.
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="How the study is described">
    Recruitment posters, letters, and ads should describe the study honestly. "Volunteer for cutting-edge cancer treatment" is misleading if the study is a placebo-controlled trial where half the participants will get no active drug.
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Where recruitment happens">
    A study recruiting only from a fancy downtown clinic will get a very different sample than one recruiting from community health centres. If a study is meant to help a broad population, but only recruits from a narrow one, that is a justice issue.
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Compensation as recruitment lure">
    High compensation can attract people who cannot afford to turn it down. That is a subtle form of coercion. On the other hand, no compensation excludes people who cannot afford to take time off. Reasonable compensation — covering costs plus a modest acknowledgement of time — is usually right.
  </Tab>
</Tabs>

## Recruitment Materials to Look At

- **Posters and flyers**: Are claims accurate? Is the language non-coercive?
- **Recruitment letters**: Does the letter make clear that this is optional and unrelated to care?
- **Advertisements (online, radio, print)**: Are they honest? Do they name the sponsor and the researcher?
- **Scripts for phone or in-person approaches**: Does the script give the person an easy way to say no?
- **Social media outreach**: Is targeting appropriate? Is privacy respected?

## Warning Signs

<Warning>
  Any of these should trigger a discussion at the meeting.
</Warning>

<Accordion title="Doctor-of-record does the recruiting">
  Even with the best intentions, this creates pressure. Best practice: someone other than the treating clinician makes the initial approach, or a clear firewall is described ("your doctor is telling you about this study — they will not know if you say no, and your care will not be affected either way").
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Recruitment materials that oversell">
  "Breakthrough treatment." "Access to cutting-edge therapy." These framings misrepresent the nature of research. Research is not care. If the study drug worked well enough to be a "breakthrough," it would be approved and available.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Compensation levels that feel off">
  Very high compensation for a modest study, or very low compensation for an intensive one, both raise questions. Ask what the reasoning is.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Vulnerable populations recruited disproportionately">
  A study recruiting mostly from shelters, addiction clinics, or low-income neighbourhoods needs to justify why. Sometimes it makes sense (the study is about that population). Sometimes it means the researchers are picking easy targets.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Burden that doesn't match the consent form">
  If the consent form says "visits take 30 minutes" but the actual schedule adds up to 3 hours per visit, that is a problem. Reality has to match description.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="No accessibility considerations">
  If a study requires in-person visits with no telehealth or remote options, no evening or weekend availability, and no language support, the participant pool is going to be skewed toward people who can afford that flexibility. Is that intentional? Is it fair?
</Accordion>

## Making Your Point Constructively

<Tip>
  When you flag a burden or recruitment issue, suggest a fix where you can. "Six visits in eight weeks is a lot for someone in active cancer treatment. Could visits 3 and 5 be combined, or done by phone?" "The compensation of \$500 per visit is much higher than similar studies — could that inadvertently pressure people who need money?" Constructive suggestions are more likely to lead to real change than pure critique.
</Tip>
