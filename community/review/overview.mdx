Studies do not just appear on the meeting agenda — they move through a defined process, from initial submission by the researcher to final closure years later. Knowing that journey will help you understand where your input fits, what has already been checked before something reaches you, and what will happen after the board votes.

## The Life Cycle of a Study

<Steps>
  <Step title="Researcher submits">
    A researcher (the "principal investigator" or PI) uploads a full protocol package to the REB's electronic submission system: the protocol itself, consent forms, recruitment materials, sponsor agreements, investigator brochures, and any other required documents.
  </Step>
  <Step title="REB office screens">
    The REB Coordinator does an initial completeness check — is everything there? Is the study within the REB's mandate? — and assigns it to a review pathway (full board, delegated, or administrative).
  </Step>
  <Step title="Primary reviewers are assigned">
    For full-board studies, the Coordinator assigns primary reviewers — typically one scientific member and often a community and/or legal representative. As a community rep, this may be you.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Primary reviewers read and write comments">
    You read the file, take notes, and submit written comments to the Coordinator before the meeting deadline.
  </Step>
  <Step title="The board meets and deliberates">
    At the meeting, primary reviewers present their comments, the researcher may present and answer questions, and then the board discusses the study without the researcher present.
  </Step>
  <Step title="The board decides">
    The board votes. The outcome is one of: approved, approved with conditions, deferred (sent back for changes), or rejected.
  </Step>
  <Step title="The Coordinator communicates the decision">
    The Chair signs a decision letter. The Coordinator sends it to the researcher, listing any conditions that must be met before the study can start.
  </Step>
  <Step title="The researcher responds">
    If the study was approved with conditions or deferred, the researcher revises the protocol and consent form and resubmits. The reviewers who raised the concerns are usually asked to check the revisions.
  </Step>
  <Step title="The study runs">
    Once fully approved, the study begins enrolling participants. The board continues to monitor it through annual continuing review, amendments, and adverse event reports.
  </Step>
  <Step title="The study closes">
    When the study ends, the researcher files a closure report. The REB reviews it and closes the file.
  </Step>
</Steps>

## Where the Community Representative Adds Value

<CardGroup cols={2}>
  <Card title="Initial review" icon="file-magnifying-glass" href="/community/review/submission-types">
    This is the biggest, most visible role. Your plain-language reading of the consent form and recruitment materials often catches problems no one else does.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Condition-checking" icon="list-check" href="/community/review/reb-decisions">
    When the board asks for revisions, you may be asked to confirm that the revised consent form is now understandable and appropriate.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Amendment review" icon="pen-to-square" href="/community/review/amendments">
    When the researcher wants to change the study, you apply the same lens — is this still fair, clear, and reasonable for participants?
  </Card>

  <Card title="Adverse event review" icon="triangle-exclamation" href="/community/review/adverse-events">
    When problems occur, the board looks at whether the consent form should be updated so future participants know about the new risk. You are essential to that judgement.
  </Card>
</CardGroup>

## What Community Reps Should Focus On at Each Stage

<Tabs>
  <Tab title="Initial submission">
    Focus on consent, recruitment, and participant burden. Ask: what is being asked of participants, how are they being told about it, and does the ask feel fair? Also think about vulnerable groups: does the study need extra protections it doesn't currently have?
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Amendments">
    Ask what has changed and whether the change requires an update to the consent form. If side effects have grown, the number of visits has increased, or the follow-up period has lengthened, participants who joined under the old terms may need to be re-consented.
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Continuing review (annual)">
    Ask what happened over the past year. How many participants enrolled? How many withdrew, and why? Were there complaints? If enrollment or withdrawal patterns look unusual, dig in.
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Adverse events">
    Ask whether the risk described in the consent form is still accurate. If a new side effect has emerged, or if a known one is happening more often than expected, current and future participants deserve to know.
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Closure">
    Ask how the researchers plan to share results with participants. Under TCPS 2, participants who want to know how the study turned out should have a way to find out. This is often overlooked — you can raise it.
  </Tab>
</Tabs>

## The Review Pathways

Not every submission goes through the same process.

<Info>
  See the [Review Levels](/community/review/review-levels) page for a full comparison. In short: **full board review** is for higher-risk or sensitive studies and involves the whole board; **delegated review** is for lower-risk studies and involves a smaller subset; **administrative review** is for very minor changes or final reports and is handled by the REB office alone.
</Info>

## Timelines

Most REBs aim for the following turnaround:

- **Full board review**: 4–8 weeks from submission to decision.
- **Delegated review**: 2–4 weeks.
- **Administrative review**: 1–2 weeks.

Delays are usually caused by incomplete submissions or by researchers taking time to respond to conditions. Your job as a reviewer is to be as prompt as you can, so the process doesn't stall on your side.

<Tip>
  If you know you cannot review a file in time, tell the Coordinator immediately so they can reassign it. Silence on a deadline is worse than a decline — it holds the study up for everyone else.
</Tip>
