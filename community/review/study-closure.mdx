Every study ends. When it does, the researcher submits a **closure report** to the REB, and the board reviews it before formally closing the file.

Closure often gets less attention than the initial review, but the questions at the end are important. What happened to participants? What is being done with their samples and data? Will participants find out how the study turned out? These are core community-rep concerns.

## What a Closure Report Covers

<CardGroup cols={2}>
  <Card title="Enrollment" icon="users">
    How many participants were enrolled? How many completed? How many withdrew, and why?
  </Card>

  <Card title="Adverse events" icon="triangle-exclamation">
    Summary of AEs and SAEs over the life of the study.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Protocol deviations" icon="route">
    Any significant deviations from the approved protocol.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Results (if available)" icon="chart-line">
    A summary of the study's findings, when available at closure.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Sample and data disposition" icon="vial">
    What is happening to biological samples and personal data.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Participant follow-up plan" icon="envelope">
    How participants will be told about results (if at all).
  </Card>
</CardGroup>

## The Questions a Community Rep Should Ask

<Steps>
  <Step title="How will participants learn how the study turned out?">
    Under TCPS 2, participants who want to know should have a way to find out. Some studies plan a summary letter to all participants; others post results on a website; many do nothing. If the plan is "nothing," ask why. Participants who took the risks deserve to know what the results were.
  </Step>
  <Step title="What is happening to samples and data?">
    Being destroyed? Being kept for future research? Being transferred to a biobank? Being sold to a commercial partner? The consent form should have said what would happen — does the closure report match?
  </Step>
  <Step title="Was consent respected throughout?">
    If participants consented only to this study, their samples and data must be handled accordingly — not repurposed for something new without asking.
  </Step>
  <Step title="What did the withdrawal pattern look like?">
    A high withdrawal rate can indicate the study was more burdensome or distressing than expected. That is worth flagging for future studies of a similar design.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Were there complaints?">
    Some closure reports include a summary of any participant complaints. If concerns were raised, how were they handled?
  </Step>
  <Step title="What did the researchers learn about the ethical conduct of the study?">
    Not required, but worth asking. Was the consent form as clear in practice as it looked on paper? Did anything about the study feel different once it was actually running?
  </Step>
</Steps>

## Sharing Results with Participants

This deserves its own paragraph, because it is chronically neglected.

<Info>
  Under TCPS 2 (Article 3.2), participants should be given the option to receive information about study results in a form and at a time that is meaningful to them. This is not always done. Community reps can and should push for it.
</Info>

A good plan might include:

- A short letter or email to all participants when major results are published.
- Access to a plain-language summary of findings, either on a study website or by request.
- For studies of specific communities (patient groups, Indigenous communities, condition-based groups), a community-level report or presentation.
- For genetic studies, a plan for whether and how individual results will be shared — with the option for participants to receive them or not.

If a closure report has no plan for sharing results with participants, ask that one be added. Even a simple email with a plain-language summary is meaningful.

## Sample and Data Retention

Different kinds of study material have different rules:

<Tabs>
  <Tab title="Research records">
    Under Health Canada rules (Division 5), records of drug trials must be kept for at least 25 years. TCPS 2 requires the REB's own records to be kept for at least 5 years after study closure. These retention rules exist so that if problems emerge years later, the records are still available.
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Identifiable data">
    Should be kept only as long as needed. When it is no longer needed, it should be de-identified or destroyed. The consent form should have specified this.
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Biological samples">
    Depends on what participants consented to. If they consented only to this study, samples must be destroyed or de-identified at closure. If they consented to future research use (as an explicit, optional choice), samples may be retained — usually in a biobank with specific access rules.
  </Tab>
</Tabs>

## Special Cases

<Accordion title="Early termination">
  Sometimes studies close early — because they succeeded and further data isn't needed, because they failed and continuing was pointless, or because safety concerns forced a stop. Early termination reports need extra scrutiny: what does it mean for participants who were in the middle of treatment? Are they being transitioned to appropriate follow-up care? Are they being told what happened?
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Studies that never really ran">
  Occasionally studies close having enrolled no participants — because recruitment was impossible, funding fell through, or the researcher moved on. Closure is straightforward, but it is worth asking why enrollment failed. Was the study poorly designed? Was recruitment unrealistic? Lessons matter for future submissions.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Community-based and Indigenous research">
  Closure for these studies should include a community-level report, not just an academic paper. Was that done? Was the community involved in interpreting the results before publication? OCAP® principles apply to closure too.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Studies with ongoing biobank use">
  If participant samples continue to be used after "closure" (in a biobank, for example), the study is not really closed — the biobank use has its own oversight and its own consent. Make sure the boundary is clear.
</Accordion>

## The Value of a Careful Closure Review

<Warning>
  Closure reviews are sometimes treated as a formality. They should not be. This is the last chance for the REB to catch problems, ensure participants are respected at the end as they were at the beginning, and make sure lessons are captured for the future. A rushed closure review is a missed opportunity.
</Warning>

<Tip>
  When you review a closure report, note anything that surprised you — patterns you didn't expect, plans that seem thin, or gaps that could inform future reviews. Share those observations with the Coordinator and Chair. Closure reports are one of the best sources of learning for the REB as a whole.
</Tip>
