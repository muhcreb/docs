Every hospital study collects information about participants. Some of it is medical, some personal, some sensitive. How that information is protected, who sees it, and what happens to it in the end — all of this matters to participants, whether they realize it at the moment of consent or not.

As a community representative, you are not expected to know Québec's Law 5 or federal privacy law in detail (the legal representative on the board has that covered). But you are expected to ask the human questions that a member of the public would ask.

## The Basics

A hospital-run study will typically involve:

- **Health information** — things like your diagnosis, treatment history, test results, medications.
- **Personal information** — name, address, date of birth, contact info, sometimes finances or family history.
- **Study-specific data** — responses to questionnaires, samples (blood, tissue), imaging.

Under Québec's **Law 5** (the _Act Respecting Health and Social Services Information_), the hospital has strict rules about how it can use patient information for research. Under **TCPS 2 Chapter 5**, the REB must be satisfied that participants' privacy is protected. Under the **Civil Code**, participants must generally consent to how their information is used.

## Questions Community Reps Should Ask

<Steps>
  <Step title="Who will see my information?">
    The immediate study team? Other researchers? The sponsor company? Regulators like Health Canada? People at other hospitals? People in other countries? Every category should be clearly disclosed.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Will my information be identifiable?">
    Is it linked to a name and hospital record, or coded so that only a key can tie it back to me? "De-identified" and "anonymous" are not the same thing. Anonymous data can never be tied back to you; de-identified data usually can, by someone who has the key.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Where is my information stored?">
    On hospital servers? On a cloud service? In another province? In another country? The location can matter for legal reasons and for how safe participants feel.
  </Step>
  <Step title="How long is it kept?">
    For the length of the study? For a fixed number of years afterward? Forever? Under Law 5 and Health Canada rules, some records must be kept for many years — the consent form should say for how long and why.
  </Step>
  <Step title="What happens to it at the end?">
    Destroyed? Kept for future research? If kept, has the participant agreed to that future use?
  </Step>
  <Step title="What are my rights?">
    Can I ask to see my data? Can I ask that it be deleted if I withdraw? The answers may depend on the study, but the consent form should be clear.
  </Step>
</Steps>

## Common Privacy Concerns to Flag

<Accordion title="Vague 'confidentiality' statements">
  "Your information will be kept confidential." That is not enough. Confidential from whom? For how long? Stored where? Shared with whom? A good privacy section is specific.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Data leaving Québec or Canada">
  Some studies send data to sponsors in the United States or Europe. Others use cloud services that store data outside Canada. Cross-border data transfer has legal implications (Law 5 has rules about it) and it should be explicitly disclosed to participants.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Sponsor access to identifiable data">
  Industry-sponsored trials often give the sponsor access to a lot of participant data. Is the data properly coded before it goes to the sponsor? Or does the sponsor get identifiable records? What does the consent form say about that?
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Long or vague retention periods">
  "Data will be kept for at least 25 years." Why 25? If Health Canada requires it, say so. If it is for future research, that is a different justification. Participants deserve to know the reason.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="'Future use' clauses buried in the consent form">
  Some studies ask participants to consent, in the same document, both to this study and to unspecified future studies using their samples or data. That is often problematic. Participants should be able to consent to the current study without being locked into future ones — typically through a separate, optional check-box.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="No plan for what participants can access">
  Some studies say nothing about whether participants can request a copy of their data, ask that it be corrected if wrong, or request its destruction if they withdraw. Under Québec law, participants generally have these rights, and the consent form should acknowledge them.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Data breach plans that don't exist">
  What if there is a data breach? Under Law 5, there are notification requirements. The consent form should acknowledge that participants will be told if their information is exposed.
</Accordion>

## The "Waiver of Consent" Question

Some studies ask to look at hospital records without contacting the individual patients first. This is called a **waiver of consent**, and both Law 5 and TCPS 2 (Article 5.5) allow it under specific conditions:

- The research is important.
- Getting individual consent would be impossible or would defeat the purpose.
- The research poses minimal risk.
- Privacy safeguards are strong.
- Participants will be informed afterward where reasonable.

<Warning>
  Waivers of consent are legitimate and often appropriate — but they deserve careful scrutiny. Ask: is this really impossible to do with consent? Are the safeguards genuinely strong? Would participants, if asked, likely have agreed — or would many have declined? The community lens is especially important here, because the participants are not in the room.
</Warning>

## Samples, Tissues, and Biobanks

Biological samples raise their own privacy questions:

- **Where are they stored?** Freezer at the hospital? At a sponsor site? In another country?
- **What can be done with them?** Only the current study? Or future studies too?
- **Can they be sold or shared with commercial partners?** Under Québec Civil Code article 25, the human body and its parts cannot be commercialized — but the boundary is not always clear.
- **What if genetic analysis reveals something the participant didn't want to know?** Is there a plan for that?

Community reps should push for consent forms that are specific about samples, and that give participants meaningful choices — particularly the choice to consent to this study without consenting to future or commercial uses.

## Working with the Legal Rep

The legal representative on the board leads on technical privacy law analysis. Your role is complementary: you make sure the plain-English version in the consent form actually matches the technical reality, and that participants would understand and agree to what is being described.

<Tip>
  If a privacy section is dense and legalistic, ask the researcher to rewrite it in plain language for the consent form. "Under the _Act Respecting Health and Social Services Information_, personal information may be communicated to third parties for research purposes..." is not something a stressed patient can decode. "Your information will be shared with two other hospitals participating in this study. Those hospitals will not know your name — they will see a code instead" is honest, plain, and specific.
</Tip>
