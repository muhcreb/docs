Some groups of participants need extra care in research because their ability to consent freely, or their exposure to harm, is different from the general population. Recognizing when a study involves a vulnerable population, and knowing what protections should be in place, is one of the most important judgements a community representative makes.

## Who Counts as Vulnerable

The TCPS 2 recognizes that vulnerability is contextual — the same person may be vulnerable in one situation and not in another. But some groups almost always warrant heightened attention:

<CardGroup cols={2}>
  <Card title="Children and youth" icon="baby">
    Under 18, cannot consent for themselves, and depend on parents or guardians.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Adults with diminished capacity" icon="user-injured">
    People with dementia, severe mental illness, intellectual disability, or acute crises that impair decision-making.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Indigenous participants and communities" icon="feather">
    Historical harm, ongoing sovereignty concerns, and specific protocols (OCAP®, TCPS 2 Chapter 9).
  </Card>

  <Card title="Prisoners and people in detention" icon="lock">
    Constrained autonomy; risk of coercion; special legal protections.
  </Card>

  <Card title="People in dependent relationships" icon="user-doctor">
    Patients being recruited by their own treating doctor; employees being recruited by their employer; students being recruited by their teacher.
  </Card>

  <Card title="People in acute crisis" icon="heart-pulse">
    Trauma victims, emergency-room patients, people in psychiatric crisis — not able to reflect calmly.
  </Card>

  <Card title="People with low literacy or language barriers" icon="book">
    May not be able to read a standard consent form; may need translation, oral consent, or accessible formats.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Marginalized or stigmatized groups" icon="users-slash">
    People with substance use disorders, sex workers, undocumented migrants, people living with HIV — vulnerable to stigma and re-traumatization.
  </Card>
</CardGroup>

<Info>
  Being a member of a vulnerable group does not mean a person cannot participate in research. It means the study must be designed with extra care, and the REB must be satisfied that participation is genuinely voluntary, that risks are proportionate, and that the community is not being exploited.
</Info>

## Special Rules and Concepts

### Children (Civil Code article 21, TCPS 2 Chapter 4)

- Consent must be given by the parent or legal guardian.
- The child themselves must also **assent** — agree at a level appropriate to their age and maturity. A separate, age-appropriate assent document is often required.
- Studies with children must offer either direct benefit to the child, or pose only minimal risk if they do not.

### Adults Who Cannot Consent for Themselves (Civil Code article 21)

- A substitute decision-maker (typically a spouse, adult child, or legal representative) must consent.
- The participant must not object (their refusal, even if they cannot fully articulate reasons, must be respected).
- Research must relate to the participant's condition, or offer benefit to others in similar situations with minimal risk.

### Indigenous Participants and Communities (TCPS 2 Chapter 9)

- Research must respect the OCAP® principles: Ownership, Control, Access, and Possession — developed by and for First Nations.
- Community consent, in addition to individual consent, is often required.
- Community engagement must be genuine, not tokenistic — the community should have input into the study design, not just review it at the end.

### Prisoners

- Autonomy is legally constrained; the risk of coercion is high.
- Studies must offer the same access to benefits that would be available outside prison.
- Some Canadian institutions decline to review prison research at all; others review it under heightened criteria.

### Emergency Research (TCPS 2 Article 3.8)

- Very rarely, participants are enrolled in an emergency without their consent because they are unconscious or incapacitated.
- Requires exceptional justification, community consultation, and consent-as-soon-as-possible.

## Community Rep Questions for Vulnerable-Group Studies

<Steps>
  <Step title="Is the vulnerable population necessary for this research?">
    Or could the study be done with a less vulnerable group? "We want to test this drug in adults with dementia because it's easier to recruit" is not a good reason. "We want to test this drug in adults with dementia because dementia is what it's meant to treat" is.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Is the consent process appropriate?">
    Standard consent forms usually are not. Is there an assent document for children? Is there a substitute-decision-maker process? Is there attention to relational autonomy?
  </Step>
  <Step title="Are protections proportionate to the risk?">
    Higher-risk studies with vulnerable groups need stronger protections — an independent participant advocate, a Data Safety Monitoring Board, community observers.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Is the community involved, or is it being studied at?">
    Especially important for Indigenous research, community-based research, and research with stigmatized groups.
  </Step>
  <Step title="What happens if participants become more vulnerable during the study?">
    A participant with early dementia may lose capacity mid-study. A participant enrolled while housed may become homeless. Is there a plan?
  </Step>
  <Step title="How are benefits shared?">
    If the study succeeds, will the vulnerable group have access to the benefits? Or will they take the risks and see none of the payoff?
  </Step>
</Steps>

## Warning Signs

<Warning>
  Any of these should trigger a deep discussion.
</Warning>

<Accordion title="Convenience-based recruitment">
  A study recruiting from a vulnerable population because they are easier to reach — rather than because the research is about them — is a red flag. This is exactly what the justice principle warns against.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Standard consent form for a non-standard situation">
  A study of children that uses the adult consent form without adaptation. A study of people with dementia that has no substitute-decision-maker process. A study with Indigenous participants and no engagement plan. Missing tailoring is a red flag.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="No plan for changes in capacity or circumstances">
  If the study is long, participants' situations may change. What if a participant loses decision-making capacity mid-study? What if a homeless participant becomes hospitalized? Silence on these questions is a problem.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Community engagement described in vague terms">
  "We consulted with community members" is not enough. Who? When? What was said? What changed as a result? Real community engagement leaves a trail.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Missing accessibility">
  If a study involves people with disabilities, is the consent form accessible? Are visits physically accessible? Is there interpretation or captioning?
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Compensation that would be coercive for a vulnerable group">
  \$500 per visit is a lot to a homeless participant. Compensation appropriate for a middle-class outpatient may effectively coerce someone in poverty. Compensation levels must be considered in context.
</Accordion>

## The Community Rep's Role

When a study involves a vulnerable population, the community rep's voice is especially important. You may be the only person in the room who instinctively looks at the study through the lens of "how would this feel for someone in a hard situation?" That perspective is precisely what the ethical framework needs from you.

<Tip>
  If you are yourself a member of a vulnerable community relevant to a study — for example, a patient-partner from a specific patient population — disclose the connection to the Chair. Sometimes it means recusal; more often, it means your input is uniquely valuable and the Chair will want you to speak to it explicitly. Either way, disclosure comes first.
</Tip>
