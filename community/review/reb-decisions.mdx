At the end of each review, the board issues a decision. Understanding the categories — and knowing what happens after each one — helps you contribute to the vote thoughtfully and follow up when needed.

## The Four Main Decision Types

<CardGroup cols={2}>
  <Card title="Approved" icon="circle-check">
    The study can proceed as submitted. This is uncommon on first review — most studies come back with at least a few conditions.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Approved with conditions" icon="list-check">
    The study can proceed once specific changes are made. This is the most common outcome.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Deferred" icon="clock">
    The study is not approved yet. Significant changes are needed, and the revised version must come back for another full review.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Rejected" icon="circle-xmark">
    The study cannot proceed as submitted. Rejection is rare; usually studies with fatal problems get deferred first to see whether they can be salvaged.
  </Card>
</CardGroup>

## Approved

**What it means:** The board is satisfied the study meets ethical and legal requirements as submitted. The study can begin enrolling participants immediately once the researcher receives the decision letter (and any required Health Canada authorization).

**When it happens:** Rarely on first review. Most studies benefit from at least small revisions. A first-review "approved" outcome usually means the study was carefully prepared, or is very low risk.

**Your role after:** Standard — continuing review annually, respond to amendments and adverse events as they come up.

## Approved with Conditions

**What it means:** The board sees a study that will be ethical **once specific changes are made**. The researcher must make those changes and submit them for confirmation before enrolling participants.

**Types of conditions:**

- **Consent form revisions**: reword a section, add a missing risk, restructure the layout.
- **Recruitment material changes**: fix an overselling headline, add a phone number.
- **Protocol clarifications**: describe what happens if a participant withdraws mid-study; describe the safety monitoring plan more fully.
- **Documentation additions**: provide a translated consent form, provide an assent form for children.

**Who checks the revisions:** Usually the reviewer who raised the concern. If you flagged that the consent form was unclear, you may be asked to confirm that the revised version is now clear.

<Info>
  This is one of the most important follow-up moments in your role. When the revised documents come back, read them carefully — do they actually solve the problem, or do they add a sentence that technically satisfies the condition without meaningfully improving clarity? Community reps often catch this second pattern.
</Info>

## Deferred

**What it means:** The study has significant problems that cannot be fixed with minor revisions. The researcher must substantially revise and resubmit, and the whole board reviews the revised version at a future meeting.

**When it happens:** When the study has fundamental issues — the design is confusing, the risks are not well understood, the consent form needs a rewrite, the recruitment plan raises fairness concerns — that require rethinking rather than tweaking.

**Your role after:** When the revised study comes back, review it fresh — not as "is this better than last time?" but as "does this now meet the standard?"

## Rejected

**What it means:** The board has decided the study cannot proceed as submitted.

**When it happens:** Rare. Usually a serious study with correctable problems is deferred, not rejected. Rejection is for studies whose fundamental design is unethical (for example, involves risks disproportionate to any possible benefit) or that violate law.

**Appeal:** The researcher has the right to appeal a rejection through the institution's formal appeal process. Community reps are not usually involved in appeals unless specifically asked.

## How the Board Reaches a Decision

<Steps>
  <Step title="Primary reviewers present">
    Reviewers assigned to the file summarize their comments and concerns.
  </Step>
  <Step title="The researcher answers questions (if invited)">
    Some boards invite researchers to attend part of the meeting to explain their study and answer questions. The researcher then leaves.
  </Step>
  <Step title="The full board discusses">
    All members contribute. This is where the community perspective is crucial — speak up.
  </Step>
  <Step title="The Chair proposes a decision">
    Based on the discussion, the Chair suggests an outcome and a list of conditions if applicable.
  </Step>
  <Step title="The board votes">
    Consensus if possible; formal vote if not.
  </Step>
  <Step title="The decision is recorded">
    The Coordinator writes it up in the minutes and drafts the decision letter for the Chair to sign.
  </Step>
</Steps>

## What "Conditions" Look Like in Practice

Well-written conditions are specific and actionable. Vague conditions cause confusion and delays.

**Vague:** "Clarify the consent form."

**Specific:** "On page 4 of the consent form, revise the description of randomization to explain in plain language what a placebo is and how it will be used. On page 6, move the withdrawal-at-any-time paragraph to appear before the description of the study drug's known side effects."

<Tip>
  When you raise a concern at the meeting that becomes a condition, help the Chair or Coordinator make it specific. That saves time on the back end — the researcher knows exactly what to fix, and you know exactly what to check when it comes back.
</Tip>

## Your Role After the Decision

<Tabs>
  <Tab title="When conditions are being verified">
    Read the revised documents. Ask: does this actually solve the problem I raised? If yes, sign off and tell the Coordinator. If not, say so — and explain what still needs to change.
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="When the study is running">
    Continuing review (annually), amendments (as they come up), and adverse event reports (as they come up). Apply the same lens: does this still respect participants? Is the study still what it was represented to be at approval?
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="If problems emerge">
    If you hear from anyone — a participant, a family member, a staff member — about a concern with an ongoing study, tell the Coordinator. Do not act on the information yourself, and do not discuss it outside the board.
  </Tab>
</Tabs>

## Avoiding a Common Pitfall

<Warning>
  Community reps sometimes feel pressure to vote yes on borderline studies, so as not to "hold things up" or seem obstructive. Resist this. If a study is not ready, deferral or a longer condition list is the correct response — and future participants will benefit from your patience. A rushed approval is not a favour to anyone.
</Warning>
