A hospital REB is not a casual advisory group — it is a formal committee with specific rules about who serves on it, how it makes decisions, and who it reports to. Knowing how the board is built will help you understand where you fit in and who to ask when you have questions.

## Who Sits on the Board

The TCPS 2 (Article 6.4) sets minimum requirements for REB membership. Your hospital's board will meet or exceed these; the exact composition is written into the REB's Terms of Reference.

| Role | Typical Number | What They Do |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Chair** | 1 | Runs the meetings, sets the agenda, signs the decision letters, and speaks for the board with hospital leadership |
| **Vice-Chair** | Often 1 | Steps in when the Chair is away |
| **Scientists and Clinicians** | At least 2 | Judge whether the study is designed well and whether it is medically safe |
| **Community Members** | At least 2, with no ties to the hospital | Bring the perspective of the public — this is you |
| **Legal Representative** | At least 1 | Reviews the legal side: consent forms, contracts, privacy law |
| **Ethicist or Humanities Scholar** | At least 1 | Grounds discussion in ethical theory and research ethics history |
| **Patient / Participant Representative** | Increasingly common | Brings lived experience of being a patient or research participant — you may hold this role too |
| **Ad-hoc experts** | As needed | Outside specialists (for example, Indigenous engagement experts) invited to speak on specific studies |

<Info>
  **Quorum** is the minimum number of people who must be present for the board to make binding decisions. Under TCPS 2, quorum must include the right mix of members — which means a meeting of only scientists, even if there are enough of them numerically, does **not** count as quorum. A community member is almost always required for quorum. In other words: if you are the only community member who can make it, your attendance may be what allows the meeting to happen at all.
</Info>

## Voting and Decision-Making

<Tabs>
  <Tab title="Who Votes">
    Every formally appointed REB member — including you — has a full vote. Every vote counts the same. The board usually tries to reach **consensus**; when it cannot, the Chair calls a formal vote and the outcome is written into the minutes.
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Who Attends But Does Not Vote">
    - **REB Coordinator / Administrator**: runs the logistics, takes minutes, and communicates decisions to researchers.
    - **Ad-hoc experts**: give input on specific studies, then leave the room before the vote on that study.
    - **Hospital observers** (for example, VP Research): may sit in but must not participate in the debate or vote.
    - **Researchers**: may be invited to present their study and answer questions, but leave the room before the board deliberates.
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Abstaining and Conflicts">
    If you have a conflict of interest on a specific study — for example, you know the researcher personally, or the study is testing a treatment for a condition you or a loved one has — you must declare it, step out of the room during that discussion, and not vote. This is documented in the minutes. Abstaining just because a topic feels difficult, however, is discouraged — the board needs your voice.
  </Tab>
</Tabs>

## The REB's Relationship to the Hospital

The REB is at the same time **part of** the hospital and **independent from** it. This is intentional and it is important.

<CardGroup cols={2}>
  <Card title="Part of the Hospital" icon="building-columns" href="/community/onboarding/reb-structure">
    The hospital sets up the REB, appoints its members, funds its office, and is ultimately responsible for making sure the board follows TCPS 2 and the law. The REB reports to a senior hospital officer — usually the VP Research or Chief Research Officer.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Independent from the Hospital" icon="shield" href="/community/onboarding/reb-structure">
    The TCPS 2 requires the REB to operate independently of institutional, political, professional, or financial pressure. Hospital administration **cannot** overturn an REB decision to refuse or suspend research. Anyone who disagrees with a decision must go through a formal appeal process — not around it.
  </Card>
</CardGroup>

<Warning>
  If a hospital administrator, department head, or researcher ever approaches you outside a meeting to try to influence how you will vote on a specific study, tell the REB Chair immediately. Protecting the board's independence is everyone's job, and community reps in particular can be targets of this kind of pressure. Report it — that is the correct response.
</Warning>

## Reporting Lines

```text
Hospital Board of Directors / Senior Leadership
        │
        ▼
VP Research / Chief Research Officer
        │
        ▼
Research Ethics Board (Chair)
        │
        ├── REB Members (including Community Representatives)
        │
        └── REB Office (Coordinator / Administrator)
                │
                └── Researchers & Study Teams
```

For board matters, your line goes to the Chair. For day-to-day questions — scheduling, documents, agendas — your first contact is the REB Coordinator.

## The REB Office and Coordinator

The REB Coordinator (sometimes called the REB Administrator) runs the operational side of the board. For new members, they are the person you will interact with most.

<Accordion title="What the Coordinator does">
  - Puts together and sends out meeting agendas and study packages
  - Assigns studies to primary reviewers (they will make sure community reps get studies to lead on)
  - Records the minutes and sends the official decision letters to researchers
  - Runs the electronic submission system where studies are uploaded
  - Tracks continuing review deadlines, annual reports, and study changes
  - Contacts researchers on the board's behalf to request revisions
  - Keeps the official REB records
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="What the Coordinator does not do">
  The Coordinator is an administrative professional, not a research ethics expert or a scientific advisor. They do not make substantive review decisions for the board, and they will not tell you whether your concern about a study is "serious enough" to raise — that is your judgement. When in doubt, raise it.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="How to work well with the Coordinator">
  - Send in your written comments or questions before the deadline they set
  - Let them know as soon as you can if you cannot attend a meeting, or if you need to recuse yourself from a specific study
  - Ask them your logistical and administrative questions (when is the next meeting, where is document X, how do I log in) instead of the Chair
  - If something urgent comes up between meetings, contact the Coordinator first and they will escalate it to the Chair
</Accordion>

## Review Pathways

Not every study goes through the same review process. Understanding the pathways will help you anticipate when your input will matter most.

<Tabs>
  <Tab title="Full Board Review">
    Used for studies with more than minimal risk, studies involving vulnerable groups (children, older adults with cognitive impairment, Indigenous communities, and others), or any study the board considers sensitive. The whole board discusses and votes together at a meeting. **This is where your community voice is most visible and most important.**
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Delegated Review">
    Used for lower-risk studies. A smaller group — typically the Chair or a designated reviewer — handles the review without pulling the whole board together. Community members are sometimes included in delegated review; ask your Coordinator whether your REB does this. If a delegated file raises any concern, it gets escalated to the full board.
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Administrative Review">
    Very small changes to a study, or a final report at the end, may be handled by the REB office alone. Your input is usually not required, but the Coordinator will flag anything that looks unusual.
  </Tab>
</Tabs>

<Tip>
  During your orientation, ask the REB Coordinator for a copy of the board's **Standard Operating Procedures (SOPs)** and the current **Terms of Reference**. These documents are the final word on your REB's quorum rules, voting procedures, member expectations, and review pathways — and they always take precedence over anything general like this page.
</Tip>
