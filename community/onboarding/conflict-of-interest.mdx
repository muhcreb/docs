A conflict of interest (COI) is any situation where your personal interests, relationships, or beliefs could — or could appear to — affect how you evaluate a study. Declaring conflicts is not an accusation or a weakness. It is what a well-functioning REB expects every member to do, every time.

Community representatives can have real conflicts, and it is important to name them. This page walks through what counts, how to disclose, and what happens next.

## What Counts as a Conflict of Interest

COI comes in several forms. All of them matter.

<CardGroup cols={2}>
  <Card title="Personal Relationships" icon="user-group">
    The researcher, a co-investigator, or a study coordinator is a friend, family member, neighbour, or someone you have a personal history with.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Organizational Ties" icon="building">
    The study is being run by, or partnered with, an organization where you volunteer, sit on the board, receive services, or have any other significant role.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Lived Experience Overlap" icon="heart">
    The study is about a condition you or a close family member have. This is not automatically disqualifying — but it must be disclosed, and the Chair will decide how to handle it.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Financial Interests" icon="dollar-sign">
    You (or a close family member) have a financial interest in the study's sponsor, a competitor of the sponsor, or the product being tested.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Advocacy Positions" icon="bullhorn">
    You have publicly campaigned for or against something the study is testing or the group it involves. Public advocacy is legitimate; it can also create a real or perceived conflict.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Anything That Feels Off" icon="circle-question">
    If something about your relationship to a study makes you uncomfortable, disclose it. The Chair can always decide it is not a conflict, but only if you flag it first.
  </Card>
</CardGroup>

## "Perceived" Conflicts Count Too

<Info>
  It does not matter whether you believe the conflict would actually change your judgement. What matters is whether a reasonable outside observer might think it could. That is what "perceived conflict" means. Perceived conflicts must be disclosed and managed the same way as actual ones — they damage the board's credibility just as much if they are hidden.
</Info>

## The Special Case of Patient-Partner Community Reps

Many community representatives are patient-partners — people appointed specifically because they bring lived experience of a particular illness or of the healthcare system. This creates a nuanced situation:

- Your lived experience is **why you are on the board.** You do not need to declare "having been a patient" as a conflict on every study.
- But if a **specific** study is about your specific condition, your specific treatment, or your specific patient community — that is a real overlap and it should be disclosed.
- The Chair may still keep you in the discussion because your perspective is uniquely valuable. That is a decision for the Chair, not for you — disclose, and let them decide.

<Tip>
  A useful rule: if you would feel weird explaining, after the fact, why you voted on this study without mentioning your connection to it, you have a conflict. Disclose it.
</Tip>

## How to Declare

There are two moments where COI declarations happen.

<Steps>
  <Step title="On appointment (and annually)">
    When you join the board, and every year after, you will sign a general COI declaration listing standing conflicts (organizations you work with, family members in research, ongoing advocacy roles, etc.). Keep this up to date.
  </Step>
  <Step title="On each specific study">
    Before each meeting, the Coordinator will circulate the list of studies to be reviewed. Read it. If any of them raise a conflict for you — personal, organizational, financial, lived-experience, or advocacy — email the Chair or Coordinator immediately. Do not wait until the meeting.
  </Step>
  <Step title="During the meeting">
    If a conflict comes up in the moment (for example, when the researcher is named), speak up right away: "I need to declare a conflict on this file." The Chair will then decide next steps.
  </Step>
</Steps>

## What Happens After You Declare

The Chair will decide how to manage the conflict. Typical outcomes include:

<Tabs>
  <Tab title="Full recusal">
    You leave the room during the discussion and vote on that study. This is documented in the minutes. You return for the next item on the agenda.
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Present but not voting">
    You may be asked to stay and provide context (especially if you have relevant lived experience) but not to vote. This is less common and is used when your input is uniquely valuable and the conflict is manageable.
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="No action">
    The Chair may decide the disclosed relationship does not rise to the level of a conflict, and you continue as normal. Even in this case, your disclosure is documented — that protects both you and the board.
  </Tab>
</Tabs>

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

<Warning>
  Do not self-decide that a conflict is "too small to mention." That call is not yours to make. Declare, and let the Chair rule.
</Warning>

<Accordion title="Waiting until the meeting to disclose">
  If you know at the time you receive the agenda, disclose then. Meeting-day surprises make it harder to organize an alternate reviewer and can delay decisions on the study.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Assuming lived experience is not a conflict">
  It usually isn't. But when a specific study is about your specific situation, it can be. Err on the side of disclosing.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Hiding a conflict to stay involved in an interesting file">
  This is a serious breach of REB duty and can result in removal from the board. If a study is interesting because of a personal connection, that is exactly the situation you need to step back from.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Refusing to serve on any study that is 'close to home'">
  The opposite mistake. If you recuse yourself from every study that touches your community, you are not contributing what the board appointed you for. Disclose, discuss with the Chair, and only step back when there is a real conflict.
</Accordion>

<Info>
  When in doubt, disclose. The Chair would much rather hear about a non-issue than discover, later, that a real conflict was hidden. Disclosure is normal, professional, and expected of every REB member.
</Info>
