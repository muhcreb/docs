At the heart of the REB's work are three simple ideas. Every rule, every checklist, every consent form template you will ever see traces back to them. You do not need to be an ethicist to apply these principles — in fact, community representatives often apply them more clearly than anyone else in the room, precisely because they come to them without a professional filter.

## The Three Principles

<CardGroup cols={3}>
  <Card title="Respect for Persons" icon="person">
    People have the right to make their own decisions. People who cannot fully decide for themselves need extra protection.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Concern for Welfare" icon="heart-pulse">
    Researchers must minimize possible harms and protect participants' well-being throughout the study.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Justice" icon="scale-balanced">
    The benefits and burdens of research must be shared fairly. No group should be exploited, and no group should be shut out.
  </Card>
</CardGroup>

Canada's Tri-Council Policy Statement (TCPS 2) uses these three principles as its foundation. They came out of decades of learning from research abuses (see [History of REBs](/community/onboarding/history-of-rebs)) and they are the ethical DNA of everything the board does.

## Principle 1: Respect for Persons

**In one sentence:** treat participants as adults who can make their own choices, and give extra care to those who cannot fully do so.

Respect for persons is why consent forms exist. It is why researchers cannot enroll someone without explaining what the study involves and letting them freely decide. It is why participants can withdraw at any time, without giving a reason and without losing access to their normal medical care.

**Questions to ask when you apply this principle:**

- Would a person receiving this consent form actually understand what they are agreeing to?
- Is the participant free to say no — or is there something (their doctor asking, their financial situation, their language, their fear) that could make them feel they cannot?
- If the study involves children, people with dementia, or others who cannot fully consent, are there extra protections in place? Is someone speaking on their behalf, and is the participant themselves being asked to agree at whatever level they can?
- Is the participant treated as a partner — or as a source of data?

<Info>
  Respect for persons has a specifically Canadian dimension: **relational autonomy**. People make decisions inside families, cultures, and communities — not alone. A consent process that ignores that context is not respecting the person. This matters especially in research involving Indigenous communities (TCPS 2 Chapter 9) and in research involving families or dependents.
</Info>

## Principle 2: Concern for Welfare

**In one sentence:** the study must be designed so participants are safe, and their well-being — physical, mental, and social — is protected before, during, and after.

Concern for welfare is why the board asks whether the risks of a study are proportionate to what participants and society will gain from it. It is why safety monitoring is required. It is why privacy protections exist — a leaked health record can cause real harm.

**Questions to ask when you apply this principle:**

- What are the possible harms? Physical (side effects, procedures)? Emotional (distress, retraumatization)? Social (embarrassment, family conflict)? Financial (lost work, travel costs)?
- Have the researchers done what they reasonably can to minimize each of those harms?
- If something goes wrong, is there a clear plan? Who does the participant call? Is care available at no cost?
- What happens to participants after the study ends? Are they left in a worse position than they started?
- Is participant information handled with care? Who sees it? Where is it stored? For how long?

<Info>
  Concern for welfare extends beyond the enrolled participants. In genetic research, findings may affect biological relatives who did not consent. In community-based research, the whole community may be affected. In hospital studies, other patients on the same ward may be involved. The board asks about all of them.
</Info>

## Principle 3: Justice

**In one sentence:** the risks and benefits of research must be distributed fairly across society — no group should be exploited, and no group should be systematically shut out.

Justice is why we ask who is being recruited into a study, and why. It is why exploiting easy-to-reach populations (prisoners, people with severe mental illness, people who need money) for their convenience is not allowed. It is also why systematically excluding women, older adults, racialized communities, or people with disabilities from studies of treatments that could help them is a problem in the other direction.

**Questions to ask when you apply this principle:**

- Who is being recruited? Are they being asked because they are the right people for this research, or because they are easy to enroll?
- Are groups that could benefit from this research being included? Or are they being excluded without a good reason?
- If the study succeeds, who will get access to what it produces? Will the participants themselves benefit? Will their community?
- For research involving Indigenous participants: is this a partnership, or is the community being studied at? Have OCAP® principles — Ownership, Control, Access, and Possession — been respected?
- Is compensation fair? Not so low that it exploits people who need money, not so high that it pressures people into risks they would otherwise refuse?

<Info>
  Justice is not just about who is in the study. It is also about what happens to the knowledge afterwards. If a treatment turns out to work, does it reach the communities that took the risks of testing it? Or does it stay locked up for people who can afford it? These are legitimate REB questions.
</Info>

## Applying the Principles: A Simple Approach

You do not need to run every study through a formal checklist. Try this instead. When you read a study, ask yourself:

<Steps>
  <Step title="Respect: Would a participant really understand what they are getting into, and be free to say no?">
    If the answer is anything less than a confident yes, flag it.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Welfare: What could go wrong, and does the study protect against it?">
    Physical, emotional, social, financial — all count. If a harm is possible and the protection is thin, flag it.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Justice: Is this study fair to the people being asked to join, and to the groups they represent?">
    Both under-inclusion and over-inclusion of vulnerable groups can be unfair. If the recruitment plan feels off, flag it.
  </Step>
</Steps>

<Tip>
  When you raise a concern at the meeting, connect it to a principle in plain language. Instead of "I don't like this," try: "I'm worried about respect for persons — I don't think a stressed patient would understand this consent form." Or: "I have a justice concern — this study is only recruiting from the outpatient clinic, but the condition affects people in the community who don't come to the hospital." This kind of framing turns a gut feeling into a substantive contribution the board can act on.
</Tip>

## Common Pitfalls for Community Representatives

<Warning>
  Do not defer to "the experts" on ethical questions. Scientists know their science. Lawyers know their law. Neither of them is a better judge than you are of whether a consent form is understandable, whether a burden feels fair, or whether a study treats participants with respect. Those are the questions the principles are asking, and they are your questions.
</Warning>

<Accordion title="Assuming that 'compliant' means 'ethical'">
  A study can technically meet every rule in the TCPS 2 and still fail the spirit of it. A consent form can hit every required section and still be so full of jargon that no one understands it. Your job is to notice when a study meets the letter of the rules but not their intent.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Assuming ethics is somebody else's job">
  Some community members feel that because there is an ethicist on the board, they should leave the ethical framing to that person. Do not. The ethicist brings theory; you bring the perspective of a real person outside the research world. Both are needed.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Softening your concerns to be polite">
  If you are worried about a study, say so directly and specifically. "I have a concern about respect for persons in Section 4 of the consent form — the description of side effects is buried in a paragraph that most people will skim past." That is a professional, actionable comment. "I don't know, maybe it's fine" is not.
</Accordion>
