Everything you see and hear as an REB member is confidential. That includes the protocols, the researchers' identities, the consent forms, the discussions in the room, how each member voted, and any personal information that shows up in the file.

This is not a technicality. It is central to how the board functions. This page explains why, what specifically is covered, and how to handle it.

## Why Confidentiality Matters

Researchers submit their protocols to the REB in the knowledge that they will be handled with care. If members leaked what was in a submission, three things would break:

<CardGroup cols={2}>
  <Card title="Researcher trust" icon="user-shield">
    Researchers would submit less information, or delay bringing sensitive studies forward. That would make the board's job harder and put participants at greater risk.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Participant privacy" icon="lock">
    Some documents contain identifiable information — patient scenarios, community details, sponsor secrets. Leaking any of it can cause direct harm.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Honest deliberation" icon="comments">
    Members speak freely in the room because they know their comments stay there. If deliberations leaked, members would self-censor and the board's judgement would suffer.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Institutional trust" icon="building-shield">
    Confidentiality breaches damage the hospital's reputation and its ability to conduct research. They can also expose the hospital to legal liability.
  </Card>
</CardGroup>

## What Is Confidential

Assume that **everything you receive or hear as an REB member is confidential**, unless the Chair or Coordinator tells you otherwise. This includes:

- The full text of protocols, amendments, and supporting documents.
- Consent forms and recruitment materials in draft or final form.
- Investigator brochures and sponsor agreements.
- Adverse event reports and safety updates.
- The identity of researchers submitting studies to the board.
- Comments made by other members during deliberation.
- How each member voted.
- Anything shared verbally in the meeting, including asides, jokes, and speculation.
- Personal or health information that appears in case studies, appendices, or vignettes.

<Warning>
  This includes not sharing with your spouse, your children, your friends, or your colleagues at work — even in general terms. "I saw a really interesting study yesterday" is a breach.
</Warning>

## What Is Not Confidential

A few things are not:

- The fact that you serve on the REB (unless your board's SOPs say otherwise — most don't).
- General information about how REBs work, the TCPS 2, and research ethics — the material on this knowledge base, for example.
- Publicly posted information (some hospitals publish study titles after approval; if it is on the hospital's website, it is public).

If you are unsure whether something is public, ask the Coordinator before you share it.

## Handling Documents

<Steps>
  <Step title="Access documents only in secure ways">
    Use the electronic submission system your REB provides. Do not download meeting packages onto public computers, unencrypted USB drives, or personal cloud accounts.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Read in private">
    Do not read meeting packages on public transit, at a café where the screen is visible to others, or with family members reading over your shoulder.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Do not print unless necessary">
    If you must print, keep the printouts in a locked drawer, and shred them when you are done with the meeting cycle. Do not leave them in the recycling bin.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Do not forward">
    Do not forward REB emails, documents, or attachments to anyone — including other REB members outside the official channels. If someone else needs a document, the Coordinator will send it.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Delete or store securely after the meeting">
    After a study is closed, delete downloaded copies from your device. If your REB's SOPs require you to retain documents for a set period, follow those instructions.
  </Step>
</Steps>

## Handling Conversations

<Accordion title="With people outside the board">
  Say nothing. If someone asks what you did at the REB meeting, say something like "I can't discuss the studies, but the meeting went well." Practice this once, and it will be easy.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="With other REB members outside meetings">
  Even between members, discuss active studies only through official channels (email routed through the Coordinator, or scheduled between-meeting calls). Do not chat about a pending study in the hospital hallway or over lunch.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="With researchers">
  If you happen to know a researcher personally and they ask about their submission outside the REB process, decline politely: "I can't discuss the review with you — you'll hear from the Coordinator." If a researcher tries to pressure you outside the process, tell the Chair.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="On social media">
  Never post about studies, deliberations, or opinions on specific submissions. Even oblique posts ("just came out of a long REB meeting about a really tough drug trial") can allow people to identify studies. Play it very safe.
</Accordion>

## What About Advocacy Work?

Many community reps are also active in patient advocacy organizations. This is valuable and legitimate — but confidentiality still applies.

<Info>
  You can advocate publicly for research in your area of interest. You cannot use information you learned as an REB member to inform that advocacy. If you find yourself thinking "I heard about a great new study at the REB — our organization should push for something like it," stop. That information is not yours to use.
</Info>

## If a Breach Happens

Mistakes happen. If you accidentally disclose something — mentioning a study name to a spouse, leaving a document visible — tell the Chair or Coordinator immediately. Prompt disclosure is treated very differently from a breach that comes to light later. The board's response will focus on containment (limiting further spread) and learning, not on punishment for honest mistakes.

Deliberate breaches, or repeated careless ones, are a different matter and can result in removal from the board and potentially in legal consequences.

<Tip>
  When you have any doubt about whether to share something — with anyone, in any form — assume the answer is no. Ask the Coordinator before you share, not after.
</Tip>
