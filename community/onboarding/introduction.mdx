A Research Ethics Board (REB) is an independent, multi-disciplinary committee whose job is to protect the rights, safety, and well-being of the people who take part in research studies at the hospital. As a newly appointed community representative, you sit alongside doctors, scientists, ethicists, and lawyers — and your point of view is one of the most important the board has.

You may or may not identify as a patient-partner. Either way, you are here because you bring a perspective the board cannot get anywhere else: you look at research the way a member of the public would, not the way a researcher does.

## What Is a Research Ethics Board?

In Canada, hospital REBs operate under a national policy called the **Tri-Council Policy Statement: Ethical Conduct for Research Involving Humans (TCPS 2)**. Any institution that receives federal research funding — which includes virtually every hospital that does research — must have an REB that reviews and approves human-subjects research **before** it starts.

<Info>
  The TCPS 2 is not a law, but it is a binding condition of federal research funding. In addition, provincial laws — including Québec's _Civil Code_ and Law 5 (the _Act Respecting Health and Social Services Information_) — impose specific requirements the REB must respect.
</Info>

The REB's main job is to:

- Review research protocols **before** any participant is enrolled.
- Monitor ongoing studies for problems, changes, or new risks.
- Suspend or shut down research that turns out to be unsafe or unethical.

The board is not a rubber stamp. It has real authority to say no, to require changes, and to stop a study.

## Why Community Perspective Matters

Researchers and clinicians know their science. Ethicists know the theory. Lawyers know the rules. What they do not always know is what the study will actually feel like to the person being asked to participate.

<CardGroup cols={2}>
  <Card title="Plain-language check" icon="comment">
    Would a person outside the hospital actually understand this consent form? If you struggle to follow it, so will participants.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Real-world burden" icon="clock">
    How much time, travel, discomfort, and disruption is this study asking of participants — and is that reasonable in exchange for what they get?
  </Card>

  <Card title="Trust and respect" icon="handshake">
    Does the study treat participants as partners, or as data points? Are their questions, cultures, and communities respected?
  </Card>

  <Card title="Voice of the public" icon="users">
    You represent people who are not in the room — patients, families, and community members who will one day be asked to join a study like this one.
  </Card>
</CardGroup>

Without a community voice at the table, the board can approve studies that are scientifically sound but tone-deaf to the people they affect. That is a failure the whole system depends on you to catch.
