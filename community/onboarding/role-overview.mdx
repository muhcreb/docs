Your appointment as a community representative is a formal role — the TCPS 2 requires that every REB include community members who are not affiliated with the institution. You are not a guest and you are not there to observe. You hold a full voting seat, and you are expected to speak, ask questions, and vote on every file that comes before the board.

## Your Core Responsibilities

<Steps>
  <Step title="Read the protocols you are assigned">
    Before each meeting, the REB Coordinator will send you a package of studies to review. Read them. You do not need to understand every scientific detail — you need to understand what the study is asking participants to do, what they are being told, and what could go wrong. Focus especially on the consent form and any documents given directly to participants.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Speak up on behalf of participants and the public">
    Your job at the meeting is to ask the questions a member of the public would ask. "Would I understand this if I received it in the mail?" "Would I feel pressured to say yes?" "Is this a fair thing to ask of someone who is already sick?" These are not soft questions — they are central to whether the study is ethical.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Participate in deliberation and vote">
    Attend meetings, join the discussion, and cast your vote. Your vote counts exactly the same as the vote of the most senior scientist or lawyer in the room. Do not vote yes just to move things along, and do not abstain because you feel out of your depth — that is precisely when the board needs to hear from you.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Review consent documents in plain-language detail">
    Consent forms are the single most important document you review. Read them carefully. Mark anything that is confusing, jargon-heavy, or misleading. This is one of the areas where community members catch problems that no one else on the board notices.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Follow studies over time">
    Your role does not end at the initial approval. When researchers send annual updates, changes to the study, or reports of problems, apply the same plain-language, participant-centred lens. If something feels different — worse, riskier, or less clearly explained — say so.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Bring community and lived-experience knowledge into the room">
    If you are a patient-partner, your lived experience of illness, treatment, or the healthcare system is directly relevant. If you are a community member without that lived experience, you still bring the perspective of the general public. Both are valid, both are needed, and both should be spoken aloud in the meeting.
  </Step>
</Steps>

## Scope: What You Review vs. What Others Review

Understanding what falls in your lane — and what does not — will help you contribute confidently.

<Tabs>
  <Tab title="What You Review">
    - **Consent documents**: Is the language clear? Is it honest about risks? Is it free of pressure or hype?
    - **Recruitment materials**: Do posters, flyers, ads, or scripts feel fair? Do they oversell benefits or downplay risks?
    - **Participant burden**: How many visits, tests, hours, or trips is the study asking for? Is that reasonable?
    - **Compensation and reimbursement**: Is the amount fair? Could it pressure people into joining who otherwise would not?
    - **Respect and dignity**: Does the study treat participants — especially vulnerable groups — with respect?
    - **Communication and complaints**: If a participant has a question or a problem, do they know who to call and will they be listened to?
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="What Others Review">
    - **Scientific validity** (scientists and clinicians): Is the study designed well? Will it produce meaningful results? You are not expected to evaluate this — trust the science members of the board on the technical side.
    - **Statistical methods** (scientists): You do not need to check the math.
    - **Legal and regulatory compliance** (legal representative): Does the study meet the requirements of Québec law, federal law, and Health Canada rules?
    - **Clinical safety details** (clinician members): Are the medical safeguards in place? Are adverse event thresholds appropriate?
    - **Regulatory paperwork** (REB office): Has the researcher filed everything they need to file with Health Canada?
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Overlap">
    Many of the most important questions cut across roles. When a consent form is unclear, that is both an ethical issue (community rep) and a legal issue (legal rep). When compensation feels coercive, that is both a community concern and a scientific-integrity concern. Do not stay quiet on something just because you think "someone else will catch it." If you noticed it, say so.
  </Tab>
</Tabs>

## Authority and Its Limits

<Warning>
  You are a member of the REB, not a spokesperson for the hospital, the researcher, or any patient advocacy group. Do not talk about specific studies with people outside the board, and do not give medical, legal, or research advice to anyone based on your REB role.
</Warning>

The REB's authority is real but bounded:

- The board can **approve, approve with conditions, defer (send back for changes), or reject** a study.
- The board can **pause or shut down** a study it has already approved if new problems come up.
- The board **cannot** design the study for the researcher, run the study, or promise participants anything.
- Your voice informs the discussion — the **whole board** makes every decision by vote.

## Contributing Meaningfully Without a Science or Law Background

One of the most common concerns new community reps raise is feeling under-qualified next to physicians, scientists, and lawyers. That feeling is completely normal — and it is also usually wrong. Here is how to contribute powerfully.

<Accordion title="Ask questions, especially the 'obvious' ones">
  If something is unclear to you, it is very likely unclear to future participants. "Can someone explain what this word means?" is not a weak question — it is often the most important question in the room. If you feel a little silly asking, ask anyway.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Put yourself in the participant's shoes">
  Imagine you or someone you love was handed this consent form on a difficult day. Would you understand it? Would you feel free to say no? Would you feel respected? That mental exercise is exactly the contribution the board needs from you.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Do not try to become a scientist or a lawyer">
  Resist the temptation to try to match the technical vocabulary of other members. Your value is precisely that you are not one of them. If a scientific member starts explaining methodology, listen — but then bring the conversation back to what it means for the person in the study.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Speak from your own experience — but flag it as such">
  If you are a patient-partner, saying "speaking as someone who has been through cancer treatment..." is powerful and legitimate. If you are not, you can still say "as someone who is not a healthcare professional, I would want to know...". Both framings are welcome. Just be transparent about which one you are using.
</Accordion>

<Tip>
  If you are unsure whether something is a real concern or just an unfamiliar practice in a research area you don't know well, ask the researcher or study coordinator to explain that specific piece of the protocol in plain language. Clarifying questions almost always surface useful information — and demonstrating engaged, thoughtful review is exactly what your role calls for.
</Tip>

## Preparation and Attendance Expectations

<CardGroup cols={2}>
  <Card title="Before Each Meeting" icon="calendar-check" href="/community/onboarding/meeting-prep">
    Read the studies you have been assigned in full. Write down your questions. If you have serious concerns, share them with the Coordinator ahead of time so the Chair can build them into the agenda.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Attendance" icon="user-check" href="/community/onboarding/reb-structure">
    Most REBs expect members to attend a minimum share of meetings each year (often 75%). Let the Chair or Coordinator know as early as you can if you cannot make a meeting.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Conflicts of Interest" icon="triangle-exclamation" href="/community/onboarding/conflict-of-interest">
    If a study involves a friend, a family member, an organization you work with, or a condition you or your loved ones have, tell the Chair before the meeting. When in doubt, disclose.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Confidentiality" icon="lock" href="/community/onboarding/confidentiality">
    Everything you see and hear as an REB member is confidential. Do not share study details, participant information, or member discussions with anyone outside the board.
  </Card>
</CardGroup>
