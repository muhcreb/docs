You do not need to be a lawyer or a regulator to serve as a community representative. You do need to recognize the main frameworks the board applies, because they will come up in meetings and knowing the names will help you follow the conversation.

This page walks through them in plain language. Read it once. Come back when you need a refresher.

## TCPS 2 — The Tri-Council Policy Statement

**What it is:** Canada's national policy on the ethical conduct of research involving humans, issued by the three federal research funding agencies (CIHR, NSERC, SSHRC).

**Why it matters to the board:** Every hospital that receives federal research funding must follow the TCPS 2 as a condition of that funding. In practice, this means every study your REB reviews is measured against it.

**What it covers:** Consent, privacy, conflicts of interest, research with Indigenous peoples (Chapter 9), research with children and other groups who need extra protection, clinical trials, qualitative research, and much more.

**The chapters that come up most often at REB meetings:**

- **Chapter 3 — Free and Informed Consent**: how consent must be sought and documented.
- **Chapter 4 — Fairness and Equity in Research Participation**: making sure recruitment is fair and inclusive.
- **Chapter 5 — Privacy and Confidentiality**: handling personal information responsibly.
- **Chapter 9 — Research Involving the First Nations, Inuit and Métis Peoples of Canada**: partnership-based research with Indigenous communities.
- **Chapter 11 — Clinical Trials**: additional standards for clinical trials specifically.

<Info>
  You will not be expected to quote chapter and article numbers. But when the Chair says "this is a TCPS 2 Chapter 3 issue," now you know that means consent.
</Info>

## Food and Drug Act (Division 5) — Health Canada Rules for Clinical Trials

**What it is:** Canadian federal law. The _Food and Drug Regulations_, Part C, Division 5 sets the rules for clinical trials of drugs, biologics, and medical devices.

**Why it matters to the board:** For any trial testing a drug or device, the researcher must file a Clinical Trial Application (CTA) with Health Canada **and** get REB approval before enrolling anyone. Division 5 is enforced by Health Canada, and the hospital can be inspected. Getting it wrong is not just an ethical failure — it is a legal one.

**What community reps should know:**

- If a study is testing a new drug or device, Division 5 applies. You will see references to "CTA," "SAE reporting" (serious adverse event reporting), and "sponsor obligations." These are all Division 5 concepts.
- Consent forms for drug trials have specific required elements under Division 5. The legal representative on the board will lead on those details — but if the form is confusing to you as a reader, that is still worth saying.
- If something goes wrong in a drug trial, Health Canada gets involved. That is why safety monitoring is so tightly built into these studies.

## Québec Civil Code and Law 5

Québec has its own legal framework that adds to the federal rules. Two pieces will come up most often.

### The Civil Code of Québec (articles 20–25)

The **_Civil Code of Québec_** directly regulates consent to research on the human body. In plain language:

- Consent must be free and informed (article 20).
- Research involving children (minors) and adults who cannot consent for themselves has special rules — a substitute decision-maker is required, and the participant themselves must also agree at whatever level they can (article 21).
- Consent must generally be in writing and given before a competent REB.
- The human body and its parts cannot be commercialized (article 25).

These are not guidelines. They are law.

### Law 5 — the Act Respecting Health and Social Services Information (in force July 1, 2024)

**Law 5** governs how hospitals in Québec handle health and social services information — including for research. It sets out the rules for using patient records in research, when a participant's consent is required, when it can be waived, and what the researcher and the hospital must do to protect the information.

<Warning>
  Because your REB is at a Québec hospital, **Law 5 is the primary privacy regime** for hospital-held health information used in research. Law 25 (Québec's general privacy modernization act, formerly Bill 64) still applies to some personal information, but for information the hospital holds about its patients, Law 5 takes precedence.
</Warning>

**What community reps should know:**

- Any study that wants to look at hospital records must satisfy Law 5's conditions. The REB is one of the checkpoints.
- Participants have rights about how their information is used and stored. The consent form should explain those rights in language they can understand.
- If you feel a consent form's privacy section is impenetrable, that is a legitimate concern to raise — clarity here is required, not optional.

## ICH-GCP — The International Standard for Clinical Trials

**What it is:** The International Council for Harmonisation's Good Clinical Practice guideline (ICH-GCP E6(R3), adopted by Health Canada in 2025). It is an international quality standard for how clinical trials are designed, run, and documented.

**Why it matters to the board:** Health Canada expects sponsors and REBs of clinical trials to follow ICH-GCP. It is guidance, not law — but it defines the professional standard of practice. Hospital accreditation and Health Canada inspections are calibrated against it.

**What community reps should know:**

- ICH-GCP has a dedicated set of provisions about the REB itself — what it must review, what records it must keep, and how often it must review ongoing studies.
- If a scientific member says "this doesn't meet GCP," they mean the study falls short of that international standard.
- You are not expected to know GCP in detail. You are expected to recognize it as the reference the board uses for clinical trial conduct.

## Institutional Policies — Your Hospital's Own Rules

On top of national and provincial rules, your hospital has its own REB policies, Standard Operating Procedures (SOPs), and Terms of Reference. These translate all of the above into the day-to-day procedures your board actually follows.

<Tip>
  Ask the REB Coordinator for a copy of the board's SOPs and Terms of Reference during your orientation. These are the documents that will tell you exactly what your board's quorum, timelines, and voting rules are. They take precedence over any general guidance — including this page.
</Tip>

## How These Layers Fit Together

When the board reviews a study, all of these rules must be satisfied at the same time.

<Steps>
  <Step title="Federal law is the floor">
    The Food and Drug Act and Division 5 are non-negotiable for drug and device trials. No REB, no hospital administrator, and no researcher can approve conduct that breaks federal law.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Québec law adds specific requirements">
    The Civil Code (arts. 20–25) governs consent. Law 5 governs use of hospital-held health information. Both apply on top of federal rules.
  </Step>
  <Step title="TCPS 2 sets the national ethical standard">
    For all research — not just drug trials — the TCPS 2 defines what "ethical" means in Canada.
  </Step>
  <Step title="ICH-GCP defines the practice standard for clinical trials">
    For clinical trials specifically, ICH-GCP sets the international expectation.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Hospital policies operationalize everything">
    Your REB's SOPs turn all of the above into concrete meeting procedures, timelines, and documentation practices.
  </Step>
</Steps>

## Your Role in All This

<Info>
  You are not expected to become an expert in any of these rules. The legal representative, the scientific members, and the REB office will handle the technical compliance. Your job is to keep asking: "Even if this is technically compliant, does it actually respect participants? Is it fair? Is it safe? Would a member of the public look at this and think it's right?" That is the community lens — and no rulebook can replace it.
</Info>
