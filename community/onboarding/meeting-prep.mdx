Your first REB meeting will feel like a lot at once: unfamiliar vocabulary, thick document packages, and a room full of experts. That is completely normal, and it gets easier quickly. This page walks you through how to prepare so you can walk in ready to contribute.

## Before the Meeting: What You Will Receive

About one to two weeks before each meeting, the REB Coordinator will send you a package containing:

- **The agenda** — the list of studies to be discussed and the order they will come up.
- **The protocols** — the full study documents for the files you are assigned to review as a primary reviewer, and shorter summaries for the others.
- **Consent forms** — the documents that would be given to participants.
- **Recruitment materials** — posters, letters, ads, scripts.
- **Any supporting documents** — investigator brochures, sponsor agreements, previous review notes.

<Info>
  You do not need to read every page of every scientific protocol from cover to cover. The scientific reviewers do that. Focus your reading on the parts that speak to the participant's experience: the consent form, the recruitment materials, the study procedures section (what will actually happen to the person), and the risks section.
</Info>

## Reading a Protocol as a Community Representative

Here is a practical order of attack.

<Steps>
  <Step title="Read the consent form first">
    Not the protocol, not the summary — the consent form. This is the document a real participant will actually read. Read it as if you or a family member were being asked to join the study. Mark anything that is unclear, misleading, jargon-heavy, or too long.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Read the recruitment materials">
    Posters, letters, ads, scripts. Ask: is this fair and honest? Does it oversell benefits? Does it use pressure or urgency? Does it make the study sound like standard care when it isn't?
  </Step>
  <Step title="Skim the protocol's plain-language summary">
    Most protocols include a lay summary. Read it. If there is no plain-language summary and the study is complex, that itself is worth flagging.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Read the 'procedures' or 'study visits' section of the protocol">
    This tells you exactly what participants will be asked to do — how many visits, what tests, how much time, what kind of samples. Ask: is this burden reasonable? What would this feel like for a busy person, a sick person, a caregiver?
  </Step>
  <Step title="Read the 'risks' section">
    Focus on both physical and non-physical risks (emotional distress, privacy, family conflict, loss of work). Compare what is in the protocol against what is in the consent form. If a risk is described in the protocol but not in the consent form, flag that.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Note anything about vulnerable groups">
    Children, older adults with cognitive impairment, Indigenous communities, people in acute crisis, and others need extra scrutiny. If the study involves them, note what special protections are in place — and whether they seem adequate.
  </Step>
</Steps>

## Writing Down Your Comments

Most REBs ask primary reviewers to submit written comments before the meeting. If you are a primary reviewer, that is you.

**A good community-rep comment is:**

- **Specific** — point to a particular page, section, or sentence.
- **Framed around the participant** — "a participant reading this would probably not understand X."
- **Actionable** — suggest what could be changed. "The description of side effects could be moved above the description of benefits, and the list could be in bullet form."
- **Honest about uncertainty** — it is fine to write "I am not sure whether this is a serious issue, but it feels off to me and I would like to discuss it."

**A weak comment is:**

- "Looks fine." (Even if you think it is fine, say what you checked.)
- "The consent form is bad." (Which part? Why?)
- "I trust the researchers." (You are not there to trust the researchers — you are there to check.)

<Tip>
  If you have serious concerns — something that might change the vote — send them to the Coordinator before the deadline, not on the morning of the meeting. The Chair uses those notes to shape the agenda, and giving people time to think about your concern makes the meeting more productive.
</Tip>

## The Meeting Itself: What to Expect

<Tabs>
  <Tab title="Meeting structure">
    A typical full-board meeting runs 2–4 hours and covers several studies. For each study:

    1. The Chair introduces the study.
    2. Primary reviewers (usually one scientific member, and often a community or legal rep) present their comments.
    3. If the researcher is invited, they present briefly and answer questions.
    4. The researcher leaves the room.
    5. The board deliberates.
    6. The board votes.
    7. The decision and any conditions are recorded in the minutes.
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Speaking up">
    You are expected to speak. If the Chair does not call on you directly, watch for a pause and jump in. Your points do not need to be polished — they need to be honest. "On page 12 of the consent form, this sentence about randomization is going to lose people" is a real, useful contribution.
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Voting">
    When the vote comes, vote. Most REBs vote by voice, by show of hands, or by consensus. If you are unsure, it is fine to say "I would like to hear from more members before I vote" — but ultimately, cast a vote. Abstaining without a conflict of interest is discouraged.
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Confidentiality">
    Everything you saw, heard, and read is confidential. Do not discuss the studies, the researchers, or the deliberations with anyone outside the board — including your spouse or your friends. See [Confidentiality](/community/onboarding/confidentiality).
  </Tab>
</Tabs>

## A Sample First-Meeting Checklist

<Accordion title="Two weeks before">
  - Confirm attendance with the Coordinator.
  - Check that you can access the electronic submission system.
  - Review your conflict of interest declaration and update it if anything has changed.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="One week before">
  - Download or receive the meeting package.
  - Block off reading time on your calendar — typically 3–6 hours depending on the number of studies.
  - Read your assigned files. Take notes.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Two to three days before">
  - Submit your written comments to the Coordinator (if that is your board's expectation).
  - Note any conflicts of interest and tell the Chair.
  - Skim the summaries of the studies you are not primary reviewer on, so you can follow discussion.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Day of the meeting">
  - Bring your notes and the consent forms (paper or on a device).
  - Arrive a few minutes early — it helps to say hello to a few members before things start.
  - Bring water and a snack. Long meetings are long.
  - Speak up. That is why you are there.
</Accordion>

## After the Meeting

- Delete or store confidential documents according to your REB's rules — do not leave them lying around.
- Watch for follow-up: if the board asked for revisions on a study you reviewed, you may be asked to review the revised version.
- Debrief with yourself: what did you learn? What questions do you wish you had asked? Bring those to the next Coordinator check-in.

<Info>
  It is completely normal for your first meeting to feel overwhelming. Most community reps say the second meeting is dramatically easier, and by the third or fourth meeting they feel like they belong in the room. Stick with it — the board needs you.
</Info>
