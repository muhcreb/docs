Research ethics boards did not appear out of thin air. They exist because, over the last century, researchers — including highly respected doctors and scientists — caused real harm to real people, and society decided that would never be allowed to happen again without independent oversight. Understanding that history is the single most useful piece of context you can have as a community representative. It explains why the board exists, why its work is taken so seriously, and why your role in it matters.

This page walks through that story in plain language. You do not need to memorize dates. You do need to understand the arc.

## Before There Were REBs

For most of medical history, researchers made their own decisions about who to study, how, and what to tell them. There was no independent review, no formal consent process, and no requirement to explain risks in advance. Doctors were assumed to act in the best interest of patients, and "research" was often not clearly separated from ordinary treatment.

That assumption of good faith — well-meaning but unchecked — turned out to be catastrophically insufficient.

## The Events That Changed Everything

<Accordion title="World War II and the Nazi doctors' trials (1945–1947)">
  During the Second World War, Nazi physicians conducted horrific experiments on concentration camp prisoners — forced surgeries, exposure to extreme cold, deliberate infection with disease, and worse. The victims had no choice, no information, and no protection.

  After the war, some of those doctors were tried at Nuremberg. The court did not just condemn them — it laid out, for the first time in a formal international document, what ethical research on human beings requires. The result was the **Nuremberg Code (1947)**. Its very first principle: "The voluntary consent of the human subject is absolutely essential."

  The Nuremberg Code is the ancestor of every consent form you will ever review.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="The Tuskegee syphilis study (1932–1972)">
  In the United States, the U.S. Public Health Service ran a study of untreated syphilis in about 400 African-American men in Tuskegee, Alabama. The men were not told what they had. They were told they were being treated for "bad blood." Even after penicillin became the standard cure for syphilis in the 1940s, the researchers deliberately withheld it — for another **30 years** — so they could keep observing what the disease did.

  Men died. Wives were infected. Children were born with congenital syphilis. All of it was preventable, and all of it was known and permitted by the researchers.

  When a journalist broke the story in 1972, public outrage was immediate. The study was shut down. The United States government issued a formal apology decades later. And Tuskegee became — and remains — the reference point for what happens when research is done to a community rather than with it. It is a major reason why trust in medical research is still fragile in many racialized communities today.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Thalidomide (late 1950s–1960s)">
  Thalidomide was a sedative marketed to pregnant women for morning sickness. It had never been properly tested in pregnancy. It caused severe birth defects in thousands of babies around the world, including in Canada.

  The disaster reshaped how governments regulate drugs. It is the direct reason Canada's _Food and Drug Act_ requires clinical trials to be reviewed and authorized by Health Canada, and why the REB now plays a formal role in approving drug studies before they can begin.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="The Belmont Report (1979)">
  In response to Tuskegee, the United States convened a national commission. Its final report, the **Belmont Report**, distilled the ethics of human research into three core principles:

  - **Respect for persons**: people have the right to make their own decisions, and people with reduced ability to decide (children, people with cognitive impairment) need extra protection.
  - **Beneficence**: researchers must minimize harm and maximize benefit.
  - **Justice**: the risks and benefits of research must be shared fairly — no group should be exploited, and no group should be systematically shut out.

  These three principles are the foundation of modern research ethics worldwide. When you evaluate a study, you are applying them — whether you use those exact words or not.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Canada catches up: the Tri-Council Policy Statement (1998, revised 2010, 2014, 2018, 2022)">
  Canada was slower than the United States to formalize research ethics oversight, but through the 1980s and 1990s the pressure grew. In 1998, Canada's three federal research funding agencies — the Canadian Institutes of Health Research (CIHR), the Natural Sciences and Engineering Research Council (NSERC), and the Social Sciences and Humanities Research Council (SSHRC) — jointly issued the **Tri-Council Policy Statement: Ethical Conduct for Research Involving Humans**, known as the **TCPS**.

  The TCPS made a simple deal with Canadian universities and hospitals: if you want federal research funding, you must have an REB, you must follow national ethical standards, and you must submit every study involving humans for review before it starts. Every Canadian hospital that does research operates under this framework today. The current version is called TCPS 2.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Indigenous research and OCAP® (1990s–today)">
  Alongside the general framework, Indigenous communities in Canada pushed back against a long history of being studied without consent, without benefit, and without control over the resulting data. In response, Indigenous scholars and communities developed the **OCAP® principles — Ownership, Control, Access, and Possession** — which say that First Nations communities have the right to control research that involves them, including how their data is used and stored.

  Chapter 9 of the TCPS 2 is dedicated to research involving Indigenous peoples, and it draws directly on OCAP® and related frameworks. Research with Indigenous communities is not just "research on humans" — it is a partnership, and the REB's job is to make sure that partnership is genuine.
</Accordion>

## What This History Means for You

Every abuse in the list above shares the same underlying pattern:

1. Researchers had good intentions (or claimed to).
2. There was no independent, community-informed check on their decisions.
3. Participants — disproportionately from vulnerable or marginalized groups — were harmed.

The REB is the check. Independent members from outside the research world — that is your community seat — are the specific answer to the specific problem of "researchers deciding what is fine for other people."

<Info>
  When you sit at an REB meeting and ask, "Would the participants actually understand this?" or "Is it fair to ask people in this situation to take on this risk?", you are performing exactly the function that history has taught us cannot be left to researchers alone. That is the job. It is not a symbolic seat.
</Info>
