title: "Frequently Asked Questions for Community REB Reps"
sidebarTitle: "FAQ"
description: "Answers to the questions community representatives most often ask when starting on a hospital REB — practical, honest, and grounded in real board dynamics."
---

Common questions from new community representatives, with straight answers.

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="Do I need any professional qualifications to serve as a community rep?">
    No. You need judgement, common sense, and a willingness to speak up. Some community reps are patient-partners with lived experience; others are members of the general public with no specific health background. Both are valuable. What is expected is that you engage seriously with the material and are willing to voice concerns even when they push back on experts.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="How much time will this take?">
    A typical commitment is 4–8 hours per month: reading time before meetings (2–5 hours depending on the meeting load), the meeting itself (2–4 hours), and some follow-up. It is more in your first few months as you get up to speed. Ask your Coordinator for a realistic estimate based on your specific board's cadence.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Am I paid?">
    Many hospitals offer a small honorarium or stipend to community reps, and most cover expenses like parking and childcare. Ask your Coordinator what your board's practice is. Whatever it is, community reps almost universally say the role's value is not primarily financial — it is the impact.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Can I vote on studies I don't fully understand technically?">
    Yes — in fact, that is normal. No single member understands every technical aspect of every study. Trust the scientific members on the science and the legal member on the law, and vote based on your own contribution: is this ethical, is the consent form usable, is the burden fair, does it treat participants right. That is the community lens the board is asking for.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="What if I disagree with the rest of the board?">
    Say so, clearly. Explain your reasoning. If, after discussion, you are still in the minority, you have three options: vote no and be recorded as opposing, abstain (though this is discouraged without a conflict of interest), or agree that you have been persuaded and vote with the majority. All three are legitimate. What is not legitimate is voting yes because you feel outnumbered when you actually believe the study is not ready.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Can I tell my spouse or family what happened at the meeting?">
    No. Confidentiality applies to spouses, family members, and friends. You can say things like "the meeting was long," but not "we discussed a really tricky drug trial today." This can feel awkward at first — it becomes easier with practice.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="What do I do if a researcher I know socially asks about their submission?">
    Politely decline. "I can't discuss submissions outside the board process — the REB Coordinator will be in touch with you about the review." Tell the Chair afterward that the conversation happened. If the researcher was pressuring you, tell the Chair immediately.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="What if I realize partway through a review that I have a conflict of interest?">
    Declare it right away, verbally at the meeting: "I need to declare a conflict on this file. I did not realize until you named the co-investigator that they are my neighbour." The Chair will decide whether to recuse you. Declaring late is far better than not declaring — and no one will hold it against you.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Can I be sued for things I do as an REB member?">
    In practice, extremely unlikely. REB members act in an official capacity, and hospitals typically extend liability protection to their board members. Ask your Coordinator to explain the specifics of your board's insurance coverage. That said: acting in good faith is your best protection. Read the files, ask questions, disclose conflicts, and follow the SOPs.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="What if I miss a meeting?">
    Notify the Coordinator as early as possible. Most REBs allow a certain number of absences per year without consequence, but chronic absence affects quorum and can lead to removal. If life is genuinely getting in the way of your service, tell the Chair — the board would rather find a way to accommodate you than lose your voice entirely.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="How long is a term?">
    Terms vary by hospital. Two to three years is common, often with the option to renew for a second or third term. There is usually no requirement to serve indefinitely — the board expects turnover, and terms are a natural check-in on whether the role still fits your life.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Can I step down?">
    Yes, at any time, though as much notice as possible is appreciated. If you need to step down mid-term for any reason — health, family, work, or simply feeling the role is not right for you — email the Chair. There is no shame in this. The board would rather have a full seat filled by someone engaged than a nominal seat held by someone stretched thin.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="What if I feel out of my depth?">
    Very common in the first few months. Tell the Coordinator and the Chair. They can pair you with a more experienced member for orientation, adjust your file assignments while you get up to speed, or arrange additional training. Community reps who ask for support up front tend to grow into strong contributors quickly. Community reps who don't ask often struggle silently and disengage.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="What if I feel like I'm the only community rep who ever speaks up?">
    This is a real dynamic on some boards. Talk to the Chair about it. If other community members are consistently silent, that is a board culture issue — not a personal one — and it can be addressed through training, meeting facilitation changes, or how the Chair invites input. Do not let it silence you, though: the community perspective needs to be heard, and if you are the only one voicing it, that is even more reason to keep speaking.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="How do I know if I'm being effective?">
    Concrete signs: you read every file assigned to you, you raise at least one substantive question per meeting, you follow up on conditions when asked, and other members reference your points in later discussions. Ultimately, effectiveness is not about being loudest or most technical — it is about consistently bringing the participant's voice into a room that would otherwise not have it. If you are doing that, you are doing the job.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>
