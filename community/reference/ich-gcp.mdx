**ICH-GCP** stands for the International Council for Harmonisation's Guideline for **Good Clinical Practice**. It is the international quality standard for how clinical trials of drugs, biologics, and medical devices should be designed, conducted, recorded, and reported.

You will hear the term at REB meetings, especially when the board is reviewing an industry-sponsored clinical trial. Here is what community reps need to know about it — without becoming experts.

## What ICH-GCP Is (and Isn't)

<CardGroup cols={2}>
  <Card title="It IS the international quality standard" icon="earth-americas">
    Adopted by regulators worldwide as the benchmark for how clinical trials should be run.
  </Card>

  <Card title="It IS Health Canada's expectation" icon="maple-leaf">
    Health Canada has adopted ICH-GCP as guidance for clinical trials done in Canada.
  </Card>

  <Card title="It ISN'T Canadian law" icon="circle-xmark">
    ICH-GCP is guidance, not statute. Canadian law is the Food and Drug Act and its Regulations.
  </Card>

  <Card title="It ISN'T only about drugs" icon="microchip">
    Although focused on drug and device trials, its principles apply broadly to any clinical research.
  </Card>
</CardGroup>

The current version is **ICH-GCP E6(R3)**, adopted internationally in January 2025 and adopted by Health Canada the same year. It replaces the previous version, E6(R2) (2016). If you see older reference documents that talk about "E6(R2)" or cite numbered sections like "Section 3" or "Section 4.8," those come from the previous version — the topics are still relevant, but the structure has changed.

## What ICH-GCP Covers

E6(R3) is organized differently from earlier versions. Instead of eight numbered sections, it is built around a set of **overarching Principles** plus **Annex 1**, which covers traditional interventional clinical trials (drugs, biologics, devices). A second annex, **Annex 2**, is being developed to cover non-traditional trial designs (e.g., decentralized, pragmatic, and real-world evidence trials). The topics most relevant to REB work include:

<Accordion title="Overarching Principles">
  A short set of principles that anchor the whole guideline — things like: clinical trials should be conducted in accordance with the ethical principles of the Declaration of Helsinki; foreseeable risks and inconveniences must be weighed against anticipated benefits; the rights, safety, and well-being of participants take priority over the interests of science and society; trials should be designed with quality built in from the start. These are the ethical foundation.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="IRB / IEC (i.e., REB) responsibilities">
  What the REB itself must do: what it must review, what documents it must retain, how often it must review ongoing studies, how it must document its decisions. This is the topic that most directly governs your board's operations.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Investigator responsibilities">
  What researchers must do: qualifications, resources, medical care for participants, communication with the REB, informed consent, records, safety reporting. Community reps do not need to know this in detail, but it is what the researcher is being held to.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Sponsor responsibilities">
  What the sponsor company must do: quality management (with a strong emphasis in R3 on "quality by design" and risk-based approaches), monitoring, safety reporting, data handling, agreements with investigators. Explains why sponsored trials come with such thick documentation.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Data governance and essential records">
  Requirements around the trial's essential documents — the consent form, the investigator brochure, the CV of the investigator, the REB approval letter, and many others — including how they are generated, handled, and retained, whether on paper or electronically.
</Accordion>

## What Community Reps Should Know

1. **When a scientific or legal member says "this doesn't meet GCP," they mean the study falls short of that international standard.** You do not need to know the specific section — you just need to recognize that GCP is the benchmark being applied.
2. **ICH-GCP's ethical foundation is the Declaration of Helsinki**, which itself descends from the Nuremberg Code (see [History of REBs](/community/onboarding/history-of-rebs)). So even a highly technical document like ICH-GCP is rooted in the same ethical tradition you are helping to enforce.
3. **ICH-GCP compliance is what Health Canada looks for during inspections.** Your REB can be inspected by Health Canada. Records of your reviews — including your comments and votes on trials — may be examined. That is a good reason to be thorough in what you write, not just what you say.
4. **ICH-GCP does not replace the community perspective.** A trial can meet every technical requirement of GCP and still have a consent form that no participant will understand. That gap is exactly what you are there to catch.

## Where ICH-GCP Falls Short (and Where You Come In)

ICH-GCP is a quality standard. It is very good at asking questions like "is the sponsor's monitoring plan documented?" and much less good at asking "is this consent form actually understandable?" That is where the community lens fills the gap. GCP checks the paperwork; you check the humanity.

<Tip>
  You will not be quizzed on ICH-GCP. If a technical GCP debate is happening at your meeting, listen — you may learn something — but do not feel you need to jump in on the technical side. Your job is to keep asking: "Even if the GCP boxes are all ticked, does this study treat participants right?" That is not a small question. It is the whole question.
</Tip>
