Every hospital study collects information about participants. Several laws govern how that information can be used. You do not need to know them in legal detail — the legal representative on the board has that covered. But you should recognize the names and know at a high level what each one does.

## Law 5 — The Big One for Hospital Research

**Full name:** _Act Respecting Health and Social Services Information_.

**In force:** July 1, 2024.

**What it does:** Sets out how bodies in Québec's health and social services network (your hospital included) can collect, use, keep, and share information about patients.

**Why it matters:** Because your hospital holds patient records, and because research often uses those records, **Law 5 is the primary privacy regime for hospital-held health information used in research.** It replaces earlier provisions and takes precedence over Québec's general privacy law (Law 25) for anything within its scope.

**What community reps should know:**

- Any study that wants to look at hospital records must meet Law 5's conditions.
- The REB is one of the checkpoints for compliance.
- Participants have specific rights under Law 5 — to know how their information is used, to see it, to correct it, and (in some cases) to have it destroyed.
- Consent for use of health information in research is generally required. In some cases the REB can waive that consent — but the conditions are strict and the community rep's judgement matters.

<Warning>
  If a study wants to use hospital records without individual consent, ask why. Waivers are legitimate under specific conditions but they must not become the default. What is the study asking to do? Would participants, if asked, likely have agreed? Would they be uncomfortable if they learned about it later?
</Warning>

## The Civil Code (articles 20–25) — The Consent Foundation

**What it does:** The Québec Civil Code directly regulates consent to research involving the human body.

**Key provisions in plain language:**

- **Article 20:** Consent to research that may pose serious risk to health must be free and informed.
- **Article 21:** Research with children and adults who cannot consent has special rules. A substitute decision-maker must consent, the participant must not object, and the research must relate to their condition or offer benefit with minimal risk.
- **Article 24:** Consent to research must generally be in writing. It can be withdrawn at any time.
- **Article 25:** The human body and its parts cannot be the subject of commercial transactions.

**Why it matters:** This is not guidance. It is Québec law. Any study that violates the Civil Code's consent requirements is not just unethical — it is unlawful.

**What community reps should know:** The Civil Code is why you sometimes see study documents that are more formal, more explicit, and more careful about consent than you might expect. That formality is not overkill — it is legal necessity.

## Law 25 — The Broader Privacy Law

**Full name:** _Act to modernize legislative provisions as regards the protection of personal information_ (formerly Bill 64).

**What it does:** Modernizes Québec's personal information protection law across both the private sector and public bodies. It sets out obligations for organizations that hold personal information: how to collect it, use it, share it, secure it, and respond to breaches.

**Why it matters to research:** Law 25 applies to personal information that falls outside Law 5's scope — for example, information collected directly from participants by a private-sector sponsor, or information held by a public body that is not part of the health network.

**What community reps should know:** Law 25 is the backdrop; Law 5 is the foreground for hospital-held records. When a study involves personal information outside the hospital's records (survey responses collected by the sponsor, for example), Law 25 kicks in.

## Federal Rules — Food and Drug Act (Division 5)

**What it does:** Federal law regulating drug, biologic, and medical device clinical trials. Includes rules about participant records that overlap with, and sometimes exceed, provincial privacy requirements.

**What community reps should know:** For any drug or device trial, records must be kept for many years (typically 25) so that Health Canada can inspect them if needed. This retention requirement should be disclosed in the consent form. If it isn't, flag it.

## What Community Reps Should Actually Do

You are not expected to check technical compliance. The legal representative does that. Your job is to look at what the consent form says about privacy and ask:

<Steps>
  <Step title="Would a real person understand this?">
    If the privacy section is dense legalese, ask for a plain-language rewrite. Participants deserve to actually know what will happen to their information.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Does it name the specific players?">
    Who will see the information? The study team? The sponsor? Regulators? Other hospitals? Specific naming is better than "authorized persons."
  </Step>
  <Step title="Does it say where the data will go?">
    Stored at the hospital? On a cloud service? Sent to another country? Concrete is better than vague.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Does it say for how long?">
    A specific number of years, with a reason if the number is long.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Does it say what participants can do?">
    Access their data. Correct it. Ask about the study's decisions. Complain to the CAI (Commission d'accès à l'information) or the Privacy Commissioner. Participants have rights; the form should acknowledge them.
  </Step>
</Steps>

<Info>
  Québec's privacy framework is more protective in many ways than what applies in other provinces. That is a strength. But it also means that consent forms have to explain more — and if they don't do that in plain language, participants will not benefit from the protection they are legally entitled to. Your voice keeps that language honest.
</Info>
