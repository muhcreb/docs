These cases are illustrative composites — not real studies from any specific hospital — but they reflect the kinds of situations that come up in Québec REB work. Read them for pattern recognition and to see how community perspective shifts the analysis.

---

## Case 1: The Consent Form That Read Fine to Everyone Except the Community Rep

### The situation

A large pharmaceutical company submitted a Phase 3 trial of a new drug for a chronic autoimmune condition. The protocol was thorough, the safety monitoring plan was strong, and the consent form was 22 pages long — well within the norm for industry-sponsored trials of this size. The scientific and legal reviewers had no concerns; the board was ready to approve with minor conditions.

The community representative, who was a patient-partner in a related condition, spoke last. She said: "I read this three times. On page 8, in the paragraph about randomization, it says 'You will be assigned to one of two arms of the study.' I had to look up what 'arm' meant. Then it says the placebo will be 'administered per the same schedule as the investigational product.' What is 'administered'? What is 'the investigational product'? These are pills. Say pills."

### What happened

The board deferred the decision — not because of any major ethical problem, but because the consent form had been written for regulators, not for patients. The community rep sat down with the Coordinator and marked up specific passages. The sponsor came back with a revised consent form that was shorter (18 pages) and much clearer. Enrollment eventually opened, and the site had one of the highest consent rates in the international trial — the sponsor's team told the researcher they thought the improved form was part of the reason.

### The lesson

A consent form can pass every technical check and still be unusable for real participants. The community rep's plain-language reading is not a nice-to-have; it is a substantive contribution to whether the study is genuinely ethical.

## Case 2: The Study That Recruited Only from the Wealthy Suburb

### The situation

A researcher submitted a study on cardiovascular disease outcomes in Québec adults. Recruitment was planned exclusively through a large family medicine clinic in a wealthy suburb. The protocol was scientifically strong. The consent form was clear. Sample size was justified.

The community rep asked: "Cardiovascular disease affects everyone, but it disproportionately affects lower-income and racialized communities. Why are we recruiting only from a suburb where those groups are underrepresented?"

### What happened

The researcher had chosen the site because they had an existing relationship there and could recruit quickly. There was no scientific reason. The board approved with a condition: the study must expand recruitment to at least two additional sites in more diverse neighbourhoods, or provide a written justification for why the study would still be generalizable without them.

The researcher pushed back, saying the additional sites would delay the study by six months. The community rep responded (in writing, calmly): "A six-month delay to have a study that reflects the population it is meant to help is not a bad trade-off." The board held the condition. The researcher expanded recruitment. The study eventually enrolled a population that better reflected who has cardiovascular disease in Québec.

### The lesson

Justice is not automatic. Studies default to the easiest recruitment sites unless someone pushes back. Community reps can be that push — and their perspective is often the only one in the room that treats participant demographics as an ethics issue and not just a feasibility issue.

## Case 3: The "Minimal Risk" Survey That Wasn't

### The situation

A researcher submitted a study using online surveys to explore the experience of postpartum depression among mothers who had lost pregnancies. The proposal called for a 90-minute survey. It was classified for delegated review as "minimal risk" — no physical procedures, no medication, no clinical interventions.

The community rep on the delegated panel was a patient-partner whose lived experience did not include this specific loss, but who had close family members who had. She said: "There is nothing physically risky here. But you are asking people to spend 90 minutes remembering the worst experience of their lives. That is not minimal risk. That is real emotional risk, and the study has no support plan for participants who become distressed. There is a link to a general 811 mental health line at the bottom of the last page. That is not enough."

### What happened

The delegated panel escalated the study to full board. The full board agreed with the community rep. The study was approved with conditions: a proper distress protocol, a named contact for participants who needed follow-up support, a shorter version of the survey with the most difficult questions optional, and a warm hand-off to a local perinatal mental health service for anyone who indicated they were in crisis.

### The lesson

"Minimal risk" in TCPS 2 language usually means minimal physical risk. Emotional and social risks can be substantial and get overlooked. Community reps who have lived experience — their own or through family — often catch this before anyone else.

## Case 4: The Amendment That Was Really a New Study

### The situation

A study of a device for monitoring blood glucose in adults with Type 2 diabetes had been approved and had been running for a year. The researcher submitted an amendment: extend eligibility to include "adults with any form of diabetes," add pediatric participants aged 12–17, and extend follow-up from six months to two years. The amendment was framed as a minor scope adjustment.

The community rep raised her hand. "This isn't an amendment. Adding Type 1 diabetes and adding children under 18 changes almost everything about who is in the study. The children need age-appropriate consent and assent — the existing consent form doesn't have that. And two years is not six months — that changes the burden for every participant, including the ones already enrolled who agreed to six months."

### What happened

The board agreed. The "amendment" was returned to the researcher with a decision that it should be treated as a new submission, with a new consent form (or forms, given the new populations), a new assent process for children, and a re-consenting process for existing adult participants who agreed to a six-month study and were now being asked to commit to two years.

The researcher was frustrated but understood. When the new submission came back, it was clearly a different — and better — study.

### The lesson

Some amendments are so big that they are effectively new studies. The board has the authority to treat them that way, and community reps are often the ones who spot the shift because they are looking at the participant experience rather than the paperwork classification.

## Common Threads

Across all four cases:

<Steps>
  <Step title="The technical review was fine">
    Every study met scientific, legal, and administrative standards.
  </Step>
  <Step title="The community rep asked a participant-centred question">
    Not a technical one — a human one.
  </Step>
  <Step title="The board took the question seriously">
    Because the community rep was specific and grounded, not vague.
  </Step>
  <Step title="The study got better">
    In every case, the outcome was a stronger study that served participants better.
  </Step>
</Steps>

<Tip>
  If you ever wonder whether your voice is making a difference, remember: the studies you help improve go on to affect hundreds or thousands of participants. Even a single well-framed community question can change how a study is experienced by every person who takes part in it.
</Tip>
