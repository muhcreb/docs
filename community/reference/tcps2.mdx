The Tri-Council Policy Statement (**TCPS 2**) is the national policy that governs research involving humans in Canada. Every study your REB reviews is measured against it. You do not need to memorize it, but knowing what it is, how it's organized, and which chapters come up most will help you follow board discussions confidently.

## What the TCPS 2 Is

The TCPS 2 is a joint policy of Canada's three federal research funding agencies:

- **CIHR** — Canadian Institutes of Health Research
- **NSERC** — Natural Sciences and Engineering Research Council
- **SSHRC** — Social Sciences and Humanities Research Council

Any Canadian institution that receives funding from these agencies must comply with the TCPS 2. That includes essentially every hospital that conducts research. The current version is called TCPS 2, and it has been revised several times since first issued in 1998 — most recently in 2022.

<Info>
  The TCPS 2 is not a law. It is a policy. But because it is a condition of federal research funding, hospitals treat it as binding — and Health Canada, in its own inspections of clinical trials, expects compliance with it.
</Info>

## The Three Core Principles

At the heart of TCPS 2 are three principles:

<CardGroup cols={3}>
  <Card title="Respect for Persons" icon="person">
    Autonomy for those who can decide; protection for those who cannot.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Concern for Welfare" icon="heart-pulse">
    Minimize harms, protect participants' well-being across all dimensions.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Justice" icon="scale-balanced">
    Fair distribution of risks and benefits; no exploitation of vulnerable groups.
  </Card>
</CardGroup>

Everything else in the policy flows from these. See [Ethical Principles](/community/onboarding/ethical-principles) for a full plain-language walkthrough.

## How the TCPS 2 Is Organized

The TCPS 2 has 12 chapters. The ones community reps encounter most often are:

<Accordion title="Chapter 1: Ethics Framework">
  Sets out the three core principles. Establishes the concept of proportionate review — that the level of scrutiny should match the level of risk. Community reps do not need to know this chapter in detail but its framing shapes every other chapter.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Chapter 2: Scope and Approach">
  Defines what counts as "research involving humans" (broader than you might think — includes some quality improvement projects, secondary use of data, and interviews). Also introduces the concept of "minimal risk" and delegated review.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Chapter 3: Free and Informed Consent">
  **The chapter you will hear about most.** Sets out the elements of valid consent, when consent can be waived, requirements for ongoing consent (participants can change their mind), and requirements for special populations. Community reps: this is your home turf.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Chapter 4: Fairness and Equity in Research Participation">
  How to include diverse populations, when it's appropriate to include or exclude specific groups, and how to think about vulnerable populations in general. The justice principle in action.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Chapter 5: Privacy and Confidentiality">
  How researchers should handle personal information. When consent can be waived for secondary use of data. What safeguards must be in place. Community reps: this is where the "how will my information be used?" question is answered.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Chapter 6: Governance of Research Ethics Review">
  How REBs are supposed to work. Composition, quorum, review pathways, appeals. This is the chapter that makes your board's rules and gives you your seat.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Chapter 7: Conflicts of Interest">
  How researchers and REB members should manage conflicts of interest. See [Conflict of Interest](/community/onboarding/conflict-of-interest) for the community rep version.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Chapter 8: Community-Based Research">
  Special considerations for research done with, not on, communities. Particularly relevant to studies involving racialized, marginalized, or geographically defined communities.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Chapter 9: Research Involving the First Nations, Inuit and Métis Peoples of Canada">
  **A critical chapter.** Sets out OCAP® (Ownership, Control, Access, Possession) principles. Requires genuine partnership, not tokenistic consultation. Any study involving Indigenous participants or communities must meet Chapter 9 standards.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Chapter 10: Qualitative Research">
  How ethics principles apply to interview studies, ethnographic research, and other qualitative methods. Community reps often bring valuable perspective to qualitative studies because the human dimension is so central.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Chapter 11: Clinical Trials">
  Additional standards for clinical trials specifically — including registration in a public trials registry, safety monitoring, and disclosure of results. Overlaps significantly with Health Canada's Division 5 rules.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Chapter 12: Human Biological Materials Including Materials Related to Human Reproduction">
  Rules for studies involving human tissue, samples, and reproductive materials. Includes rules about biobanking and future use.
</Accordion>

## What Community Reps Should Know

1. **You do not need to memorize chapters and article numbers.** You will hear other members refer to them ("this is a Chapter 3 issue" means consent, "this is a Chapter 5 issue" means privacy, "this is a Chapter 9 issue" means Indigenous research). Recognizing the shorthand is enough.
2. **The principles matter more than the specific rules.** If you get a rule wrong, another member will correct you gently. If you lose sight of the principle, no one else in the room may notice.
3. **Where TCPS 2 is silent, principles fill the gap.** New technologies, new research designs, and new situations often outrun the specific rules. When that happens, the board falls back on the three principles — and that is where the community voice matters most.

## Where to Find the Full Text

The TCPS 2 is publicly available at [ethics.gc.ca](https://ethics.gc.ca/eng/policy-politique_tcps2-eptc2_2022.html). You do not need to read it cover to cover. The Introduction and Chapter 1 (about 20 pages) will give you the framework; Chapter 3 (Consent) and Chapter 5 (Privacy) are the two chapters most worth reading in detail as a community rep.

<Tip>
  If you have time, read Chapter 9 (Research Involving the First Nations, Inuit and Métis Peoples of Canada) even if no such study is on your immediate agenda. It is one of the strongest sections of the TCPS 2 and it will change how you think about community engagement in research generally.
</Tip>
