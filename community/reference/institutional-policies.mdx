On top of national frameworks like TCPS 2 and provincial laws like Law 5, your hospital has its own internal rules for how the REB operates. These translate the broad principles into concrete meeting procedures, timelines, and expectations.

As a community rep, you should know they exist and know how to look things up in them when you have a question.

## Terms of Reference

The Terms of Reference (ToR) is the founding document of your board. It sets out:

- What the board is authorized to do.
- Who serves on it and in what roles.
- Quorum rules — how many members must be present, and what mix of member types.
- How decisions are made.
- How members are appointed, reappointed, and removed.
- How disputes are resolved.
- How the board reports to hospital leadership.

<Info>
  If you ever have a question about your rights, your responsibilities, or the board's authority, the ToR is the place to start. Ask the Coordinator for a copy during your orientation.
</Info>

## Standard Operating Procedures

The SOPs turn the ToR into day-to-day practice. They typically cover:

<Accordion title="Meeting procedures">
  How meetings are scheduled and run. How the agenda is set. When materials are distributed. How much lead time reviewers get. What happens if quorum is not met.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Review procedures">
  What documents make up a complete submission. How primary reviewers are assigned. What written comments should include. How researchers are notified of decisions.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Decision documentation">
  How the board's decisions are recorded, communicated, and stored. What the decision letter looks like. How appeals are handled.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Conflict of interest">
  How members must declare conflicts. What happens after a declaration. What the Chair's role is in adjudicating close cases.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Confidentiality">
  How documents must be handled. How discussions must be protected. What happens after a member leaves the board.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Continuing review">
  How often ongoing studies must be re-reviewed. What documents are required for continuing review. Who handles it.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Amendments and adverse events">
  Timelines and procedures for reviewing amendments and adverse event reports.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Record retention">
  How long REB records must be kept, and how they can be accessed.
</Accordion>

## Institutional Research Policies

Alongside the REB's own documents, your hospital has broader research policies. These often include:

- **Research integrity policy** — rules against research misconduct (fabrication, falsification, plagiarism).
- **Conflict of interest policy** — for researchers and administrators (separate from the REB's own COI rules for members).
- **Data governance policy** — rules for handling patient data across the hospital.
- **Intellectual property policy** — who owns what when research produces inventions or discoveries.
- **Publication and authorship policy** — rules for crediting research contributions.
- **Public engagement policy** — rules for communicating with participants and the public.

You will not encounter these directly in most reviews, but they form the institutional context in which the REB operates.

## When Institutional Policies Seem to Conflict with TCPS 2 or Law

Sometimes an institutional policy appears more permissive, or more restrictive, than TCPS 2 or provincial law. When that happens, the higher-order source wins:

<Steps>
  <Step title="Federal and provincial law take precedence">
    No hospital policy can authorize something that violates the Food and Drug Act, Québec's Civil Code, or Law 5.
  </Step>
  <Step title="TCPS 2 comes next">
    Since compliance with TCPS 2 is a funding condition, hospital policies must be consistent with it.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Institutional policies operate within the space above allows">
    They can be more restrictive than the higher framework (adding extra protections) but not less.
  </Step>
  <Step title="If you notice a conflict, raise it with the Chair">
    Do not try to resolve it yourself. Note it, flag it, and let the Chair decide how to handle it — typically by consulting institutional legal counsel.
  </Step>
</Steps>

## The Community Rep's Practical Toolkit

During your orientation, ask the Coordinator for:

1. **A copy of the current Terms of Reference.**
2. **A copy of the current REB SOPs.**
3. **A brief overview of any other institutional research policies you should know about.**
4. **A copy of the REB's Code of Conduct or Member Handbook, if one exists.**

Keep these somewhere you can find them. You will not read them cover to cover, but they are the reference when a question comes up: "What is our quorum?" "How much notice does the researcher get to respond to conditions?" "Can I bring my Coordinator a question by email between meetings?" All answered in the SOPs.

<Tip>
  Your board's SOPs take precedence over any general guidance in this knowledge base. If this site says one thing and your SOPs say another, follow your SOPs. This site is a training resource, not a substitute for your board's actual rules.
</Tip>
