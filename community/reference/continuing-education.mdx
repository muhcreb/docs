Your REB service is not a set-it-and-forget-it appointment. Research ethics evolves — new technologies emerge, laws change, communities' expectations shift. Staying informed makes you a more effective board member and keeps you comfortable in a role that can otherwise start to feel unfamiliar.

This page walks through what training is expected, what is recommended, and where to grow over time.

## Required Training

Most REBs require every new member to complete some basic training within the first few months. The specific requirements come from your hospital's SOPs, but they typically include:

<CardGroup cols={2}>
  <Card title="TCPS 2 CORE tutorial" icon="graduation-cap">
    A free online course from the Panel on Research Ethics. About 3–4 hours; produces a certificate.
  </Card>

  <Card title="REB orientation session" icon="chalkboard-user">
    A one-on-one or small-group session with the Chair and Coordinator to walk through your board's specific procedures.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Confidentiality training" icon="lock">
    Often part of the hospital's general privacy training. Refreshed annually.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Conflict of interest declaration" icon="triangle-exclamation">
    Complete on appointment and update annually.
  </Card>
</CardGroup>

### The TCPS 2 CORE Tutorial

The **TCPS 2 CORE (Course on Research Ethics)** is a free online course produced by the Panel on Research Ethics. Nearly every Canadian REB requires all members to complete it.

- **Time commitment:** Plan for 3–4 hours. You can do it in chunks.
- **Format:** Self-paced online modules with short quizzes.
- **Output:** A certificate of completion — submit this to the Coordinator.
- **Where:** [https://tcps2core.ca](https://tcps2core.ca)

<Info>
  Even if you have already done CORE for a different role (patient advocacy, employment, another board), your REB may want a fresh certificate. Ask the Coordinator whether an old one counts.
</Info>

## Recommended Reading

You do not need to read anything below cover to cover. Skim, bookmark, come back when relevant.

<Accordion title="TCPS 2 itself (introductory chapters)">
  Read the Introduction and Chapter 1 for the framework. Then read Chapter 3 (Consent) and Chapter 5 (Privacy) for the areas that come up most often in community-rep work. [ethics.gc.ca](https://ethics.gc.ca).
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="TCPS 2 Chapter 9 (Research Involving First Nations, Inuit and Métis Peoples of Canada)">
  A masterclass in what genuine community engagement looks like in research. Applies far beyond Indigenous research — the principles are transferable.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Rebecca Skloot, 'The Immortal Life of Henrietta Lacks'">
  A well-known book that traces how cells were taken without consent from a Black woman in the 1950s and became one of the most valuable research tools in modern medicine — without her family's knowledge or benefit. A powerful case study in consent, race, and research.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Harriet A. Washington, 'Medical Apartheid'">
  A history of medical experimentation on African-Americans. Essential context for anyone doing research ethics work.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Reports from the Truth and Reconciliation Commission of Canada">
  Especially the Calls to Action related to health research. Grounds Canadian research ethics in the specific history and present of Indigenous peoples in Canada.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Your hospital's own annual report on research ethics">
  Many hospitals publish an annual overview of REB activity. It will show you what studies have come through, what patterns emerged, and how the board is evolving.
</Accordion>

## Ongoing Learning Habits

<Steps>
  <Step title="Attend REB retreats and refresher sessions">
    Many REBs hold annual retreats or refresher training. Go. These are where you learn from your colleagues and where the board's culture is transmitted.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Ask questions in meetings">
    If a term or concept is unfamiliar, ask. Every meeting is a learning opportunity, and the other members are usually happy to explain.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Follow up on cases that surprised you">
    If a study raised a question you didn't fully understand at the meeting, ask the Coordinator or another member afterward. That is how expertise builds.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Read reports from major research ethics bodies">
    The Canadian Association of Research Ethics Boards (CAREB), the Nuffield Council on Bioethics (UK), and the Presidential Commission for the Study of Bioethical Issues (US) all publish accessible reports on emerging issues.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Follow patient-partner networks">
    If you are a patient-partner, organizations like the Canadian Institutes of Health Research (CIHR) Strategy for Patient-Oriented Research (SPOR) run networks for continuing learning. Even if not, the community of practice is worth being connected to.
  </Step>
</Steps>

## Emerging Topics Worth Watching

Research ethics is not static. Areas that are actively evolving as of 2024–2026 include:

<CardGroup cols={2}>
  <Card title="AI and machine learning in research" icon="robot">
    Use of AI to analyze health data, predict outcomes, and design studies. New privacy and consent questions.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Decentralized clinical trials" icon="house-medical">
    Trials that happen partly at home, with remote monitoring and telehealth. Changes what "participation" looks like.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Genomic and genetic research" icon="dna">
    Genetic findings can affect family members who didn't consent. Ongoing questions about how to handle incidental findings.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Biobanks and future use" icon="vial">
    How to handle consent to future, unspecified uses of samples and data.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Patient-partnered research" icon="user-group">
    Growing recognition that patients should shape research questions, not just participate in studies designed without them.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Indigenous data sovereignty" icon="feather">
    OCAP® and related frameworks are being applied more rigorously across all research involving Indigenous participants.
  </Card>
</CardGroup>

## Raising Learning Needs

If you feel out of your depth on a topic, that is a signal for the board — not a personal failing. Tell the Chair or Coordinator, and they can:

- Arrange training for the whole board on the topic.
- Bring in an outside expert for the relevant meeting.
- Adjust file assignments to give you time to grow into new areas.

<Tip>
  Community reps sometimes hesitate to say "I don't understand this well enough yet." Do not hesitate. Every experienced REB member started where you are, and the boards that develop the strongest community voices are the ones where new members feel safe asking for growth support.
</Tip>
